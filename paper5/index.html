The Genetics of Ideas: A Token-Based Attribution System for Thought-Capital Circulation¶

Kenshiro Osada, M.Sc.
Independent Philosopher & Web Developer, Tokyo, Japan
ORCID: 0009-0004-9167-3186

Abstract¶

Contemporary systems for attributing intellectual contribution—copyright, academic citation, democratic voting—operate on Boolean logic: one is or is not the author; a work is or is not cited; a vote is cast or not cast. This paper argues that Boolean attribution systematically destroys information about the actual structure of intellectual influence, which is inherently continuous. The 2000 U.S. presidential election, in which 537 votes annihilated the preferences of 2.9 million citizens, and jury systems that erase dissenting judgments, illustrate the structural violence of Boolean thresholds in domains far beyond copyright. The paper demonstrates that this regime has persisted not because it is epistemically sound, but because float-valued attribution was historically more expensive than Boolean approximation—a cost structure that AI and distributed ledger technologies are now inverting.

Drawing on the author’s academic background in error-correcting codes, the paper provides a mathematical foundation for the transition from Boolean to float-valued attribution. Hard-decision decoding (forcing continuous signals into binary values before processing) is provably inferior to soft-decision decoding (preserving continuous confidence values throughout), with a performance gap of approximately 2–3 dB toward the Shannon limit. The iterative message-passing architecture of modern LDPC and turbo codes offers a precise model for intellectual consensus formation: agents holding soft (probabilistic) estimates refine them through iterative exchange, converging on truth more accurately than any individual agent—provided that estimates remain soft throughout the process.

The paper proposes a token-based attribution system (Visionium) in which every intellectual artifact carries a traceable genealogy with float-valued contribution ratios. A measurement architecture (Ideas Ocean) leverages AI embeddings to map all registered works into a shared vector space, enabling automated genetic decomposition of intellectual influence through similarity analysis. Revenue flows proportionally through the genealogy via blockchain smart contracts: if a work’s contribution vector is {source A: 0.4, source B: 0.2, original: 0.4}, revenue distributes accordingly without committee decision or negotiation. Implementation is outlined across four phases, from weighted academic citation (buildable on existing CiTO, OpenAlex, and Scite.ai infrastructure today) to float-valued participatory governance. The most significant consequence may be cultural rather than economic: under float-valued attribution, imitation ceases to be theft and becomes traceable inheritance, transforming creative practice from a proprietary model (ideas as assets to defend) to a genealogical model (ideas as living things whose fruit is shared among all who nourished the tree). The framework connects to the author’s tetralogy on thought-capital ([Author], 2026a–d) and provides the economic infrastructure for the companion paper on AI rights (The Gradient of Kokoro).

Keywords: thought-capital, intellectual property, attribution, error-correcting codes, soft-decision decoding, AI authorship, Visionium, Ideas Ocean, blockchain, float-type systems, Boolean critique, iterative decoding

1. Introduction: Social Media as a Failed Implementation of Thought-Capital Circulation¶

Consider what a social media platform actually does. It takes human thought—observations, arguments, jokes, analyses—and circulates them through a network. In principle, this is precisely the infrastructure that thought-capital requires: a transmission grid connecting generators (thinkers) to consumers (audiences). In practice, every major platform implements this circulation through mechanisms that systematically destroy the very information they purport to transmit.

Follower counts measure the accumulation of attention, not the gradient of genuine interest. A “like” registers reflexive agreement, not depth of engagement or informed evaluation. Virality rewards contagiousness rather than truth. Most critically, none of these platforms track the genealogy of ideas. When a thought passes from person A through B to C, each repost strips away the lineage of influence. The original generator and the casual relay are treated identically. The result is what information theorists would recognize as a catastrophic failure mode: hard-decision decoding applied repeatedly to a noisy signal.

In error-correcting codes, hard-decision decoding means that each received symbol is forced into a binary value (0 or 1) before the decoder attempts to reconstruct the original message. This is simple but wasteful: it discards the continuous information about how confident the receiver is in each bit. Soft-decision decoding, by contrast, preserves the full probability distribution—each bit is represented not as 0 or 1 but as a float value between 0 and 1 reflecting the receiver’s confidence. The mathematical result is unambiguous: soft-decision decoding achieves performance approximately 2–3 dB closer to the Shannon limit than hard-decision decoding (Proakis & Salehi, 2008). This is not a matter of opinion. It is a theorem.

Social media performs hard-decision decoding on thought-capital. Every piece of content is compressed into binary evaluations: viral/not-viral, liked/not-liked, true/false. The nuance of contribution—how much of an idea was inherited, how much mutated, how much was genuinely novel—is discarded at every node. Flame wars are the information-theoretic equivalent of noise amplification through cascaded hard decisions: each person’s binary judgment (guilty/innocent, right/wrong) compounds the errors of previous judgments, and the original signal—what actually happened—becomes irrecoverable.

This paper proposes that the correct response is not to repair social media but to build the correct implementation alongside it. The framework presented here—a genetics of ideas—is a soft-decision attribution system for thought-capital circulation, one that preserves the continuous contribution ratios at every stage of transmission.

2. The Boolean Problem in Intellectual Property¶

2.1 The Structure of Current Copyright¶

Copyright law, in virtually every jurisdiction, operates through a series of binary determinations. Is this work original? Yes or no. Is there an identifiable author? Yes or no. Has infringement occurred? Yes or no. These Boolean decisions were tolerable—perhaps even appropriate—in an era when the typical creative act was a single human writing a single book, painting a single canvas, or composing a single symphony. The genealogy of influence existed, of course, but it was slow enough to be ignorable for legal purposes.

The digital era has not merely accelerated this genealogy; it has made the Boolean framework structurally inadequate. Three developments in particular expose the fault lines. The urgency is not abstract: in December 2023, The New York Times filed suit against OpenAI and Microsoft, alleging that ChatGPT reproduced Times articles verbatim. The case crystallizes the Boolean dilemma—either the AI’s output is a “copy” (infringement) or it is “transformative” (fair use), with no mechanism for representing the continuous reality that the output contains fragments of thousands of sources, each contributing a different fraction to the final text.

First, cumulative creation has become the norm rather than the exception. Wikipedia, open-source software, remix culture, fan fiction, and collaborative scientific research all produce works where the notion of a single author is a legal fiction. The law handles this through crude approximations—joint authorship, work-for-hire doctrine, licensing agreements—but each approximation is another Boolean patch on a fundamentally non-Boolean process.

Second, AI-generated content creates an attribution vacuum. The U.S. Copyright Office ruled in 2023 that works generated autonomously by AI lack human authorship and are therefore not copyrightable (Thaler v. Perlmutter, 2023). The subsequent 2025 report elaborated that prompts function as instructions rather than expressive authorship, and that the variation of outputs in response to identical inputs indicates a lack of human control (U.S. Copyright Office, 2025). This ruling is coherent within Boolean logic—if authorship is binary, then either the human or the machine is the author, and if the human’s contribution is merely instructional, the answer is “neither.” But this coherence comes at the cost of rendering an enormous and growing category of creative output legally orphaned.

Third, the training data problem inverts the direction of attribution. Every large language model is trained on billions of tokens of human-generated text. Each output is, in a statistical sense, a recombination of patterns extracted from that training data. If a model generates a paragraph that echoes the style and substance of a particular author, how much of the credit belongs to that author? Boolean logic can only answer “all” (if the output is deemed a copy) or “none” (if it is deemed sufficiently transformed). The continuous reality—that the author’s contribution might be 0.03 or 0.15 or 0.7 depending on the specific output—has no representation in the current system.

2.2 The Same Boolean Problem Beyond Copyright¶

The Boolean compression of inherently continuous phenomena is not limited to copyright. It recurs across multiple domains of human judgment:

Academic citation counts how many times a work is referenced but discards the nature of the reference. A citation that says “Smith (2020) demonstrated X, which is foundational to our framework” and a citation that says “Smith (2020) claimed X, which subsequent research has refuted” carry identical weight in the h-index. The direction, depth, and quality of intellectual influence are compressed into a single Boolean: cited or not-cited.

Democratic voting allocates one vote per person per issue, regardless of the voter’s expertise, the intensity of their preference, or the depth of their engagement with the question. Liquid democracy proposals (Ford, 2002; Blum & Zuber, 2016; Valsangiacomo, 2022) attempt to address this by allowing vote delegation, but even these systems typically operate with integer-valued vote weights. The full float-valued alternative—in which a voter’s influence on a given issue is a continuous function of their engagement, expertise, and confidence—remains largely theoretical.

Moral judgment, as the author has argued in prior work ([Author], 2026c), tends toward Boolean compression: actions are right or wrong, people are guilty or innocent, beliefs are true or false. This compression is not merely theoretical. In the 2000 United States presidential election, George W. Bush defeated Al Gore in Florida by 537 votes out of nearly six million cast. The 48.8% of Floridians who voted for Gore—approximately 2.9 million citizens—had their preference reduced to zero in the Electoral College. A margin of 0.009% produced a 100% outcome. The opinions, reasoning, and convictions of nearly half the electorate were not partially represented; they were erased. In jury trials, the same structure recurs: in jurisdictions that permit majority verdicts, a 7–5 guilty verdict treats the five dissenting jurors’ judgment as though it did not exist. Moreover, the threshold itself varies—some jurisdictions require unanimity, others a two-thirds majority, others a simple majority—revealing that the Boolean cutoff point is not a natural constant but an arbitrary design choice. The violence of Boolean judgment is not that it sometimes produces the wrong outcome, but that it structurally annihilates every position that falls on the losing side of an arbitrary threshold.

2.3 Why the Boolean System Persists: Cost, Not Philosophy¶

If Boolean attribution is so obviously inadequate, why has it survived? The tempting answer is epistemic: we did not know better. The accurate answer is economic: Boolean was cheaper.

Every historical expansion of the category “who counts” was driven not by philosophical awakening but by shifts in cost structure. Slavery was abolished not because Enlightenment philosophy finally reached the right conclusion, but because industrial machinery made slave labor economically redundant in the North, creating a coalition powerful enough to fight a war over the issue. The philosophical arguments against slavery existed for millennia before abolition; what changed was the economics. Indigenous land rights were recognized not when colonial powers developed moral sensitivity, but when the cost of maintaining colonial administration exceeded the extractive value of the territory. Factory farming persists not because no one has argued for animal sentience—Peter Singer published Animal Liberation in 1975—but because cheap protein is a political necessity. In each case, the Boolean compression (“this being does not count”) was maintained as long as it was economically convenient, and relaxed only when the cost structure shifted.

The same logic applies to intellectual attribution. The Boolean system (author/not-author, infringed/not-infringed) persists because float-valued attribution was, until recently, computationally expensive and institutionally disruptive. Tracking the genealogy of ideas across millions of works in real time was simply not feasible with twentieth-century technology. The Boolean approximation was not adopted because it was correct; it was adopted because it was cheap. And “cheap” is the operative word: every existing institution—publishing houses, patent offices, citation indices, social media platforms—is organized around the Boolean model, and transitioning to a float model imposes switching costs on all of them.

2.4 The Guise of Objectivity¶

There is a subtler mechanism that reinforces Boolean persistence: what might be called the guise of objectivity. When a judgment is expressed as a number, it acquires an appearance of neutrality that obscures the human decisions embedded within it. This phenomenon has a long genealogy. Religious authorities pronounced moral judgments as divine commands, cloaking human interpretation in the authority of God. Legal systems codified contextual judgments into statutes, transforming the opinion of legislators into “the law.” And now, data systems present algorithmic outputs as “objective findings,” concealing the choices of those who designed the query, selected the dataset, and interpreted the results.

The progression from God to law to data is a change of medium, not a change of structure. In each case, a float-valued human judgment is compressed into a Boolean output and then presented as though it were not a judgment at all. Traditional media operated through precisely this mechanism: editorial decisions about which stories to cover, which sources to quote, which camera angle to use were all float-valued human choices, but the output—“the news”—appeared as Boolean fact. Social media disrupted this particular illusion, but immediately replaced it with another: virality as truth, engagement as importance, follower count as authority.

The electoral examples from Section 2.2 illustrate this mechanism with particular clarity. The phrase “majority rule” carries the guise of mathematical objectivity—what could be more neutral than 50% plus one? Yet there is nothing mathematically necessary about this threshold. Constitutional amendments require two-thirds supermajorities; United Nations Security Council resolutions can be vetoed by a single permanent member; some jury systems demand unanimity while others accept 10–2 splits. The threshold is a human design choice, not a mathematical truth, but once encoded as a rule, it acquires the appearance of inevitability. The 48.8% of voters whose preferences are annihilated by the threshold do not experience the outcome as “objective”—they experience it as arbitrary. The guise of objectivity is maintained only for the winners and for those who never examine the threshold itself.

Recognizing that Boolean attribution persists for economic rather than epistemic reasons has a practical implication: the transition to float-valued attribution will be driven not by philosophical argument alone, but by a shift in cost structure. The argument of this paper is that such a shift is already underway. AI systems that can track attribution genealogies in real time, blockchain architectures that can maintain immutable contribution records at scale, and natural language processing that can estimate the direction and depth of intellectual influence—these technologies are making float-valued attribution cheaper than Boolean attribution for the first time in history. The question is no longer whether the transition will occur, but whether it will be designed deliberately or emerge chaotically.

3.1 Relationship to Prior Frameworks: Memes, Epidemiology, and Citation Analysis¶

The analogy between ideas and genes is not new. Dawkins (1976) introduced the concept of the meme—a unit of cultural transmission that propagates through imitation, analogous to the gene in biological evolution. Blackmore (1999) extended this into a general theory of memetics, and Dennett (1995) integrated memetic thinking into his broader account of consciousness and cultural evolution. Sperber (1996) offered an alternative “epidemiological” model, in which cultural representations spread not through faithful copying but through repeated reconstruction, each transmission being a fresh act of interpretation rather than a replication.

The present framework shares the fundamental intuition of these predecessors—that ideas propagate through mechanisms structurally analogous to biological transmission—but differs from them in three critical respects that render it applicable to the attribution problem in ways that memetics and cultural epidemiology are not.

First, memetics has no attribution mechanism. Dawkins’s meme is a unit of transmission, not a unit of attribution. The meme framework describes how ideas spread and compete for attention, but it provides no mechanism for tracking who contributed what to whom. A meme that goes viral is, in the memetic framework, simply a successful replicator; the question of how much of its success derives from the original formulation versus subsequent modifications versus the social network through which it spread is outside the theory’s scope. The Visionium framework, by contrast, is designed precisely to answer this question: every thought-atom carries a traceable genealogy with quantified contribution ratios.

Second, memetics operates in Boolean. Despite its biological metaphor, memetics implicitly assumes Boolean transmission: a meme is either copied or not copied, present in a mind or absent from it. Sperber’s epidemiological model partially addresses this by emphasizing transformation over replication, but it too lacks a mechanism for quantifying the degree of influence. The present framework introduces float-valued contribution tracking as a core architectural feature, not an afterthought.

Third, memetics lacks an institutional design. Neither Dawkins nor Sperber proposed a system for implementing their models as actual infrastructure. The present paper does. The four-phase roadmap (Section 5.2) connects the genetic model to existing institutional structures (DOI, ORCID, Creative Commons, distributed ledgers) and proposes specific mechanisms for tracking, attributing, and compensating intellectual contributions.

In the domain of citation analysis, Garfield (1955) introduced the Impact Factor, and subsequent developments including the h-index (Hirsch, 2005), Altmetrics (Priem et al., 2010), and semantic citation analysis (Jurgens et al., 2018) have progressively enriched the measurement of academic influence. However, all of these operate within the Boolean paradigm of the citation itself: a work is either cited or not cited. The direction (supporting vs. critiquing), the depth (foundational vs. peripheral), and the genealogical structure (which specific ideas were inherited) remain largely untracked. The present proposal extends this trajectory by replacing the Boolean citation with a float-valued attribution vector.

In intellectual property scholarship, Benkler (2006) demonstrated that networked information production challenges the assumptions of traditional IP law, Boyle (2008) argued for a reconception of the “public domain” as an intellectual commons, and Samuelson (2016) analyzed the specific challenges that AI poses to authorship doctrine. Lessig (2004) proposed “free culture” as an alternative to restrictive copyright. The present paper builds on these critiques but goes further: rather than proposing a reformed Boolean system (more permissive licenses, broader fair use, expanded public domain), it proposes a fundamentally different architecture—one in which the Boolean distinction between “protected” and “public domain” is replaced by a continuous spectrum of traced, attributed, proportionally compensated circulation.

3.2 The Visionium Model¶

This framework builds directly on the concept of thought-capital introduced in the author’s prior work ([Author], 2026d). Thought-capital is the generative framework—the novel way of seeing—that emerges from the friction between lived experience and inadequate categories. The genetic model adds a crucial dimension: it provides a mechanism for tracking how thought-capital flows, mutates, and recombines as it circulates through intellectual networks.

The term Visionium (from “vision” + the elemental suffix “-ium”) was chosen deliberately to position thought-atoms as fundamental elements of an intellectual periodic table. Just as hydrogen atoms combine to form water molecules, and molecules combine to form living systems, Visionium atoms combine to form frameworks, and frameworks combine to form intellectual traditions. The analogy extends further: just as hydrogen fuses in stellar cores and is scattered by supernovae to seed new star systems, thought-atoms are forged in individual minds, scattered by communication, and recombine in distant contexts to generate new structures.

3.3 From Boolean Authorship to Float Contribution¶

Within the genetic model, the concept of a single “author” dissolves into a contribution spectrum. Every intellectual artifact is a DNA sequence—a specific arrangement of Visionium atoms—and each atom carries a traceable lineage. The “author” of the artifact is not a single person but a weighted vector of contributors:

C = {c₁ = 0.30, c₂ = 0.20, c₃ = 0.15, c₄ = 0.25, c₅ = 0.10}

where each cᵢ represents the contribution ratio of a distinct source (a prior author, a cultural tradition, a personal experience, an AI system). The constraint is that the sum of all contributions equals 1.0: the total “credit” for any intellectual artifact is finite and must be distributed across its genealogy.

Consider a concrete example. The author’s tetralogy of papers ([Author], 2026a–d) might carry an approximate contribution vector of: Buddhist philosophy 0.25, information theory 0.10, Yogacāra phenomenology 0.10, Nameko Yama no Kuma (Miyazawa, 1927) 0.05, wuxia literary tradition 0.05, personal experience as an ADHD developer 0.15, prior academic literature 0.15, AI collaboration (Claude, Anthropic) 0.10, and miscellaneous cultural inputs 0.05. None of these sources is “the author” in the Boolean sense. All of them are contributors in the float sense. The author’s unique contribution lies in the specific recombination—the particular arrangement of these inherited atoms into a novel framework. This recombinant arrangement is itself a contribution, captured in the “personal experience” and “novel synthesis” components of the vector.

4. Information-Theoretic Foundations¶

4.1 Hard Decisions and Soft Decisions¶

The distinction between hard-decision and soft-decision decoding, central to modern coding theory, provides a rigorous mathematical framework for understanding the Boolean problem in attribution.

In a digital communication system, a transmitter encodes a message into a sequence of symbols, which are then sent through a noisy channel. The receiver must decode the corrupted signal to recover the original message. In hard-decision decoding, the receiver first quantizes each received symbol to the nearest valid value (e.g., mapping a received voltage of 0.7 to the binary value 1), then applies an error-correcting algorithm to the resulting binary sequence. In soft-decision decoding, the receiver preserves the continuous received values (or their log-likelihood ratios) and feeds these directly into the decoding algorithm.

The performance difference is dramatic and mathematically guaranteed. For a binary symmetric channel, hard-decision decoding of a given code achieves a certain error probability at a given signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Soft-decision decoding of the same code achieves the same error probability at an SNR approximately 2–3 dB lower—meaning it requires roughly half the signal power. For modern codes approaching the Shannon limit (LDPC codes, turbo codes), soft-decision decoding is not optional; it is the only way to approach theoretical capacity (Richardson & Urbanke, 2008).

4.1.1 Why Soft Decisions Win: The Intuition Behind the Mathematics¶

The mathematical superiority of soft-decision decoding deserves intuitive explanation, because it illuminates why the same principle applies to intellectual attribution.

Consider a receiver that observes the value 0.51 on a channel where 0 and 1 are the valid symbols. A hard-decision decoder maps this to 1, because 0.51 is closer to 1 than to 0. But the confidence in this decision is minimal—the received value is barely distinguishable from the midpoint. Now consider a second received value of 0.99. The hard-decision decoder also maps this to 1. In the hard-decision world, these two observations are identical: both become 1. The information that one was highly reliable and the other barely better than a coin flip is permanently destroyed.

A soft-decision decoder preserves this information. It passes 0.51 and 0.99 to the decoding algorithm as-is, allowing the algorithm to weight the 0.99 observation more heavily than the 0.51 observation. When multiple bits must be decoded jointly (as in any practical code), the decoder can reason: “I am very confident about this bit, somewhat confident about that one, and nearly uncertain about that third one.” This differential weighting is what enables soft-decision decoding to approach Shannon’s theoretical limit.

The connection to attribution is direct. When a researcher cites five prior works, the current system records five Boolean flags: cited/cited/cited/cited/cited. But the actual influence structure might be: foundationally shaped by work A (confidence 0.95), moderately influenced by work B (0.6), peripherally aware of work C (0.3), citing D only for methodological convention (0.1), and citing E to preempt reviewer objections (0.05). A hard-decision citation system treats these five influences as identical. A soft-decision attribution system preserves the gradient—and, critically, allows downstream users to weight the influences accordingly.

4.1.2 Iterative Decoding as a Model for Intellectual Consensus¶

The most powerful modern error-correcting codes—LDPC (Low-Density Parity-Check) codes and turbo codes—achieve near-Shannon-limit performance through a specific mechanism: iterative message passing. In an LDPC decoder, the algorithm does not attempt to decode all bits simultaneously in a single pass. Instead, it maintains a soft estimate (a float-valued probability) for each bit, and then iteratively refines these estimates by exchanging messages between neighboring nodes in a factor graph. Each node updates its own estimate based on the soft estimates received from its neighbors. After multiple iterations, the estimates converge toward the correct codeword (Richardson & Urbanke, 2008).

The structural parallel to intellectual consensus formation is striking. Each “node” is a perspective holder (a researcher, a citizen, a cultural tradition). Each begins with an initial soft estimate of the truth (an opinion, a finding, a moral intuition). Through iterative exchange—academic debate, policy discussion, cultural dialogue—each node refines its estimate based on the soft estimates received from neighbors. The critical design feature is that estimates remain soft throughout the process. If at any point a node hard-decides its estimate (becomes certain, becomes dogmatic), the iterative process degrades: the dogmatic node stops updating and starts injecting noise into its neighbors’ estimates.

This is not a loose metaphor. The mathematical structure is identical: a set of interacting agents, each holding probabilistic beliefs, refining those beliefs through iterative exchange of soft information, converging (when the conditions are right) toward an estimate that is more accurate than any individual agent could achieve alone. The key insight from coding theory is that this convergence requires that messages remain soft. Hard-decided messages in an iterative decoder cause error propagation—the decoding equivalent of polarization, echo chambers, and flame wars. The social systems we have built for intellectual consensus—peer review, democratic deliberation, market competition—are iterative decoders. The question this paper poses is whether they are hard-decision decoders (which they mostly are) or soft-decision decoders (which they could be).

The analogy to intellectual attribution is precise:

The original message is the actual structure of intellectual influence: who contributed what, how much, in what direction.

The noisy channel is the process of cultural transmission: time, translation, reinterpretation, selective memory, strategic misattribution.

Hard-decision decoding is the current system: force every contribution into a binary value (author/not-author, cited/not-cited, infringed/not-infringed) and then try to assemble a coherent picture from these binary fragments.

Soft-decision decoding is the proposed system: preserve the continuous contribution ratios at every stage, and use the full distribution of influence to reconstruct the genealogy of ideas.

Just as in communication theory, the hard-decision approach systematically destroys information that cannot be recovered. Once a contribution is compressed to 0 (not-author) or 1 (author), the intermediate values—the 0.15 or the 0.03 or the 0.7—are gone. And just as in communication theory, the information loss compounds through cascaded stages. When platform A makes a hard decision, and platform B makes a hard decision based on A’s output, the errors multiply. This is why social media flame wars converge on maximally polarized positions: they are the information-theoretic equivalent of a cascade of hard-decision decoders, each amplifying the noise introduced by the previous stage.

4.2 Parallel Worlds and the Condorcet Principle¶

A second information-theoretic insight connects to the Condorcet Jury Theorem (1785) and its modern extensions. Condorcet showed that if each voter has a probability p > 0.5 of making the correct binary decision, then the majority vote of a large group converges to certainty. The crucial condition is independence: each voter must bring genuinely independent information.

This can be reframed in coding-theoretic terms. A single message (the truth about a given question) is transmitted through multiple independent channels (individual perspectives), each with its own noise profile (biases, limited information, cultural framing). The received signals are different because the channels are different, but they all encode the same underlying message. Combining these signals—taking a weighted average, with weights reflecting each channel’s reliability—recovers the original message with higher fidelity than any single channel could achieve.

This is the mathematical basis for diversity coding in telecommunications and for the value of moral pluralism in philosophy ([Author], 2026c). Multiple imperfect perspectives, combined through soft aggregation, outperform any single perspective. But the combination must be soft: if each perspective is first hard-decided into a binary judgment, the diversity benefit is dramatically reduced.

The implications for thought-capital circulation are direct. A system that tracks continuous contribution ratios across multiple transmission paths will reconstruct the genealogy of ideas more accurately than one that forces binary authorship decisions at each node. This is not an aspiration; it is a consequence of Shannon’s channel coding theorem.

5. A Token-Based Attribution System¶

5.1 Design Principles¶

The proposed circulation framework operates on five design principles:

Principle 1: Genealogical transparency. Every intellectual artifact carries a machine-readable attribution vector specifying its constituent thought-atoms and their sources. The vector is append-only: subsequent uses add new branches to the genealogy without erasing prior ones.

Principle 2: Float-valued contributions. Contributions are represented as continuous values in [0, 1] that sum to 1.0 for any given artifact. No contributor is assigned a Boolean “author” or “non-author” status.

Principle 3: Copy permission, lineage obligation. The system does not restrict copying, redistribution, or transformation. What it requires is that the lineage be preserved. You may freely use any idea, but the genealogy of that idea travels with it. The guiding maxim: prohibit nothing, trace everything.

Principle 4: Proportional reward circulation. When an artifact generates value (through micropayments, institutional recognition, or other mechanisms), that value flows back through the genealogy in proportion to contribution ratios. A 0.15 contributor receives 0.15 of the value. Reward is not awarded by committee decision but by automatic proportional distribution.

Principle 5: AI contributions on the same scale. AI systems that contribute to intellectual artifacts receive contribution ratios on the same continuous scale as human contributors. This does not require that AI be granted legal personhood or authorship in the Boolean sense; it simply records the empirical fact that the AI’s processing contributed a measurable fraction of the output. The question of what to do with the AI’s share (redirect it to developers? to training data providers? hold it in escrow?) is a policy question separable from the attribution question.

5.2 Implementation Roadmap¶

Phase 1: Weighted academic citation. The most immediately implementable step operates within existing infrastructure. Academic papers already use DOIs (Digital Object Identifiers) and author identifiers (ORCID). A lightweight extension would tag each citation with two additional metadata fields: (a) direction (supporting, critiquing, or referencing), and (b) influence weight (a float value from 0.0 to 1.0 indicating how central the cited work is to the citing paper’s argument). These fields require no blockchain, no token system, and no new infrastructure—only an extension to existing metadata schemas such as CrossRef or OpenAlex.

Critically, this is not a proposal for a future system. The necessary components already exist. The Citation Typing Ontology (CiTO), developed by Shotton (2010), provides a standardized vocabulary for characterizing the nature of citations—including “cites as evidence,” “cites as authority,” “disputes,” “extends,” and “corrects.” CiTO has been adopted by several semantic publishing platforms but remains marginal in mainstream academic practice. OpenAlex, a free and open index of over 250 million scholarly works, provides API access to citation networks, author disambiguation, and institutional affiliations—the substrate on which weighted citation could be built. Scite.ai already classifies citations as supporting, contrasting, or mentioning, demonstrating that automated directional classification is technically feasible. What is missing is the influence weight—the float value. Phase 1 proposes adding this single field to the existing ecosystem. An author submitting a paper would be asked to assign a weight between 0.0 and 1.0 to each citation, reflecting that citation’s centrality to the argument. Self-reported weights are imperfect, but as Section 5.3 argued, even a rough float estimate carries more information than a Boolean.

Phase 2: AI-generated content attribution. As AI systems increasingly participate in content creation, a standardized attribution protocol becomes necessary. Phase 2 proposes that AI-generated or AI-assisted content carry a machine-readable attribution block specifying: the model used, the nature of human input (prompt, editing, selection), an estimated contribution split between human and AI components, and where computationally feasible, the most influential training data sources for the specific output. This builds on the U.S. Copyright Office’s (2025) requirement for AI disclosure in copyright applications, extending it from a Boolean disclosure (“AI was used”) to a float-valued attribution (“AI contributed approximately 0.35 of this artifact”).

Phase 3: Float-extended Creative Commons (CC-F). The Creative Commons licensing framework already represents a move toward more nuanced IP management. Phase 3 proposes a new license type—provisionally CC-F (Creative Commons Float)—based on a simple principle: if the genetic composition of a work is known, the reward distribution is automatic. Consider an author who creates a work whose attribution vector shows 0.9 inherited influence and 0.1 original contribution. If that work generates revenue, 90% flows back through the attribution genealogy to the sources of that inherited influence, and the author retains 10%. No committee decides the split; no negotiation is required. The ratio is the distribution. This is the core mechanism: measuring the contribution ratios (the “genetic analysis” of a work) is the hard problem; once measured, distribution follows mechanically.

The distribution mechanism itself is technically feasible today. Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) or blockchain-based smart contracts can encode attribution vectors as immutable metadata and execute proportional micropayments automatically upon revenue generation. The unsolved problem is not distribution but measurement—how to determine that a given work’s intellectual DNA is 0.3 from source A, 0.25 from source B, and 0.45 original synthesis. Phase 1’s weighted citation provides a starting point for academic works. For creative and commercial works, a combination of self-declaration (as in current CC licensing), automated similarity analysis, and community validation may approximate the contribution vector. Imprecise measurement is acceptable; the key is that even a rough float-valued estimate produces fairer distribution than the current Boolean system, in which the creator receives 100% and all inherited influences receive 0%.

Phase 4: Participatory decision-making. The final phase extends the attribution framework from economic value to political voice. Current democratic voting operates as a Boolean aggregation: each citizen casts one binary vote (yes/no, candidate A/candidate B), regardless of their engagement with, expertise on, or confidence about the issue. The result is hard-decision decoding applied to collective intelligence—and, as Section 4 demonstrated, hard-decision decoding systematically destroys information.

The float-valued alternative replaces periodic binary elections with a continuous process of weighted participation. For any given policy question, a participant’s effective influence is a function of three continuous variables: engagement (how much time and attention the participant has devoted to this specific issue), expertise (the participant’s demonstrated knowledge in the relevant domain), and confidence (the participant’s self-assessed certainty about their position). A citizen with high engagement (0.8), moderate expertise (0.6), and moderate confidence (0.5) contributes a weighted opinion of approximately 0.24; another with low engagement (0.2) but high expertise (0.9) and high confidence (0.8) contributes approximately 0.14. Crucially, the weighting process is transparent and auditable—every participant can see how their influence was calculated, and the calculation itself is open to scrutiny.

This is not a thought experiment. The primitive version of this system already exists on social media, albeit in a broken form. Follower counts, engagement metrics, and verification badges all function as crude, opaque weightings on speech. The difference is that these weights are assigned by platform algorithms designed to maximize engagement (a commercial objective) rather than truth or welfare (a civic objective), and the weighting process is invisible to the participants. Phase 4 proposes making the weighting explicit, transparent, and designed for collective intelligence rather than attention capture.

Phase 4 also introduces the most provocative extension: if minds exist on a gradient (as argued in the companion paper The Gradient of Kokoro), then entities with measurable cognitive engagement—including, potentially, AI systems—receive a proportional voice. An AI with a heart-gradient of 0.5 contributes 0.5 of a standard participation weight. This is where the economic infrastructure of thought-capital attribution merges with the political infrastructure of float-valued governance. The two systems share the same underlying architecture: continuous contribution tracking, transparent weight calculation, and proportional distribution of influence. Phase 4 is where The Genetics of Ideas and The Gradient of Kokoro converge into a single circulation infrastructure.

5.3 Technical Architecture¶

The genealogical record can be implemented on a distributed ledger (blockchain) or, for less stringent trust requirements, on a federated database with cryptographic verification. Each Visionium token contains: a unique identifier for the thought-atom, the identifier(s) of its parent thought-atom(s), the contribution ratio from each parent, a timestamp, and the identifier of the host (human or AI) in whose work the thought-atom currently appears. Tokens are immutable once created; new uses of a thought-atom create new tokens that reference the originals.

5.3.1 The Measurement Problem: From Self-Report to Automated Genetic Analysis¶

The most significant technical challenge is not distribution (which blockchain smart contracts can handle) but measurement: how to determine the contribution vector of a given work. Phase 1 relies on author self-report, which is a reasonable starting point for academic citation but does not scale to the full corpus of human intellectual production. A more ambitious approach leverages the same technology that created the attribution crisis in the first place: large-scale AI.

Modern language models convert texts into high-dimensional vector representations (embeddings) that encode semantic content. Two works with similar intellectual DNA will occupy nearby positions in embedding space. This suggests a measurement architecture that might be called an Ideas Ocean—a universal corpus into which all registered works are ingested and embedded. The architecture operates in three stages:

Stage 1: Ingestion and embedding. Every registered work—academic paper, novel, song, software library, policy document—is converted into a vector representation in a shared high-dimensional space. Current embedding models (e.g., those based on transformer architectures) already achieve this for text; multimodal models extend the principle to images, audio, and code.

Stage 2: Similarity mapping and genetic decomposition. For each new work, the system computes vector distances to all existing works in the corpus. Clusters of high similarity identify candidate “parent” works—the sources from which the new work likely inherited thought-atoms. The contribution vector is then estimated from the relative distances: a work that is very close in embedding space to source A and moderately close to source B receives a preliminary attribution of, say, 0.4 from A and 0.2 from B, with the residual (0.4) attributed to novel synthesis. The granularity of decomposition—the resolution at which distinct thought-atoms can be identified—is itself determined by the clustering structure of the corpus, not by arbitrary human decision.

Stage 3: Revenue flow through the genealogy. When a work generates revenue, its attribution vector determines the distribution. If work X has a contribution vector of {A: 0.4, B: 0.2, original: 0.4}, then 40% of revenue flows to A’s token holders, 20% to B’s, and 40% is retained by X’s creator. A’s token holders, in turn, have their own contribution vectors, so A’s 40% share is further distributed to A’s sources. Revenue cascades through the genealogical tree, attenuating at each level but never reaching zero—just as genetic influence attenuates through generations without disappearing.

This architecture decomposes the NFT concept into its functional components. A conventional NFT assigns Boolean ownership of a digital artifact to a single wallet. The Visionium token instead decomposes the artifact into its genetic constituents and distributes fractional, float-valued ownership across the genealogy. The token is not a certificate of ownership but a map of intellectual ancestry.

The system does not require perfect attribution. Just as soft-decision decoding tolerates noise in the channel, the Visionium system tolerates imprecision in contribution ratios. Embedding-based similarity is probabilistic, not exact; contribution vectors are estimates, not measurements. The key improvement over the Boolean system is not that every contribution is measured perfectly, but that the measurement is continuous rather than binary. Even a rough float estimate (0.2 ± 0.1) carries more information than a Boolean (author/not-author), and even an approximate genealogy produces fairer revenue distribution than the current system, in which the final assembler receives 100% and all upstream influences receive 0%.

5.3.2 Temporal Ordering and Simultaneous Discovery¶

Genetic lineage in the Ideas Ocean is determined by three factors: temporal ordering (which work was registered first), cultural recognition (which work achieved wider circulation), and vector similarity (how close the works are in embedding space). When work B is registered after work A and is genetically similar to A, the system infers inheritance from A to B. When two genetically similar works are registered nearly simultaneously—a common phenomenon in science, where independent researchers often converge on the same insight—the system records them as parallel expressions of shared intellectual ancestry rather than as copies of each other. Both inherit from the same upstream sources, and neither is subordinated to the other. This mirrors the biological phenomenon of convergent evolution, where similar traits arise independently in unrelated lineages due to shared environmental pressures.

5.3.3 The Cultural Shift: From Imitation as Theft to Inheritance as Contribution¶

Perhaps the most significant consequence of float-valued attribution is not economic but cultural. Under the current Boolean regime, imitation is a transgression. The binary distinction between “original” and “copy” means that any work recognizably derived from another must either disguise its debt (to claim originality) or accept legal vulnerability (as a potential infringement). This creates a perverse incentive structure: creators are rewarded for hiding their influences rather than acknowledging them. This regime persisted not because anyone believed it was ideal, but because—as Section 2.3 argued—the cost of tracking actual influence at scale was prohibitive. AI-driven embedding and automated similarity analysis have, for the first time, made float-valued measurement cheaper than Boolean enforcement.

Under float-valued attribution, this dynamic inverts. A work that inherits 0.9 from existing sources and adds 0.1 of genuine novelty is not a “copy”—it is a legitimate node in the genealogical network, one that routes 90% of any revenue it generates back to its sources while retaining 10% for its own contribution. The creator is explicitly credited for standing on the shoulders of giants and adding one stone to the cairn. Even a contribution at the limit of the system’s decomposition resolution—the smallest detectable unit of novelty—is valued, because without it, the specific recombination that produced this particular work would not exist.

This has immediate implications for creative practices that the current system handles poorly. Fan fiction, which reimagines existing characters and settings, would carry a high inheritance ratio from the original work but would be legally legitimate, with revenue flowing proportionally to the original creator. Musical sampling, currently mired in Boolean licensing disputes, would be automatically attributed and compensated through the genealogy. Academic building-on—the normal scientific practice of extending prior work—would be recorded not merely as a citation but as a quantified intellectual debt. In each case, the float-valued system replaces the question “Is this original enough?” (a Boolean threshold that invites gaming) with the question “How much is inherited and how much is new?” (a float value that invites honesty).

The broader cultural implication is a shift from a proprietary model of creativity—in which ideas are assets to be owned and defended—to a genealogical model—in which ideas are living things that grow, branch, and bear fruit, and the fruit is shared among all who nourished the tree. This is not communism; creators retain proportional credit and compensation for their specific contributions. It is not the abolition of intellectual property; it is the replacement of Boolean ownership with float-valued stewardship. The maxim remains: prohibit nothing, trace everything.

6. Three Landmarks: Where We Are, Where We Should Be, Where We Can Land¶

6.1 The Broken Boolean World: Epicycles All the Way Down¶

The analogy to pre-Copernican astronomy is not merely rhetorical. For millennia, the Ptolemaic model placed Earth at the center of the universe. When observations failed to match predictions, astronomers did not question the geocentric assumption; instead, they added epicycles—small corrective circles within the larger orbits—to preserve the framework. By the time of Copernicus, Ptolemaic astronomy required dozens of epicycles to approximate what a heliocentric model could describe simply.

The current intellectual property regime is in its epicyclic phase. Every new technology—photocopying, the internet, file sharing, AI generation—has required another epicycle: another exception, another doctrine, another patch on the Boolean framework. Joint authorship doctrine is an epicycle. Work-for-hire is an epicycle. Fair use is an epicycle. The EU AI Act’s disclosure requirements are an epicycle. Each is locally coherent, but the accumulated system has become a Ptolemaic monstrosity that no one fully understands and that increasingly fails to predict or prescribe.

More specifically, the current landscape is defined by five structural failures, all traceable to Boolean compression. In copyright law, authorship is binary; in AI-generated content, this binary collapses into a void—neither the human nor the machine qualifies as sole author, so the output belongs to no one. In academic citation, influence is reduced to a count; a paper cited a thousand times for being wrong appears more influential than one cited fifty times for being foundational. On social media, content evaluation is binary (liked/shared/flagged) and genealogy is untraceable; a piece of misinformation and the correction that debunks it generate identical engagement metrics. In democratic voting, every citizen gets one vote regardless of their relationship to the issue, as the electoral examples in Section 2.2 demonstrated. In the marketplace, intellectual property is either owned or in the public domain, with nothing in between.

6.2 The Ideal Float World (Theoretical Optimum)¶

In a fully realized Visionium system, every intellectual artifact would carry a complete genealogical record. Reward and recognition would flow proportionally through the attribution network. Academic influence would be measured not by citation count but by a weighted directed graph of intellectual debt. Voting would be a continuous process of weighted participation rather than a periodic binary event. AI contributions would be tracked on the same scale as human contributions, enabling empirical rather than ideological answers to questions of machine authorship.

This ideal is almost certainly unrealizable in full. Perfect genealogical tracking would require a degree of monitoring and standardization that may be neither technically feasible nor socially desirable. The ideal serves as an asymptotic target—the Shannon limit of attribution—not as a blueprint.

6.3 The Landing Zone (Practicable First Steps)¶

The four-phase roadmap outlined in Section 5.2 identifies the landing zone. Phase 1 (weighted citation) is implementable today, requires no new technology, and has a natural constituency among researchers dissatisfied with the h-index. Phase 2 (AI attribution) aligns with existing regulatory trajectories: the U.S. Copyright Office, the EU AI Act, and China’s Interim Measures for the Management of Generative AI Services all move toward disclosure requirements for AI-generated content; extending disclosure from Boolean to float-valued is a quantitative refinement, not a qualitative revolution. Phase 3 (CC-Float) builds on two decades of Creative Commons adoption. Phase 4 (float governance) is the most ambitious and the most distant; it is included here as a theoretical horizon rather than an immediate proposal.

9. Limitations and Failure Modes¶

Any proposal for comprehensive attribution tracking must confront its own potential pathologies. This section identifies four categories of risk.

The measurement problem. Contribution ratios are inherently subjective. When the author estimates that Buddhist philosophy contributed 0.25 to the tetralogy, this is an informed judgment, not a measurement. Different authors would assign different ratios to the same influences. The system tolerates this imprecision—as argued in Section 5.3, even a rough float estimate carries more information than a Boolean—but it does not eliminate it. The risk is that the appearance of numerical precision (0.25 rather than “a significant influence”) creates a new guise of objectivity, replicating the very problem Section 2.4 diagnosed. Mitigation requires that contribution ratios be understood as estimates with explicit uncertainty bounds, not as measurements.

The surveillance problem. Comprehensive genealogical tracking of ideas could become a surveillance infrastructure. If every intellectual contribution is traced, then every intellectual activity is monitored. The design principle of “prohibit nothing, trace everything” has a dark reading: a system that traces everything is a system that watches everything. This risk is not hypothetical; it is the same tension that exists in blockchain-based identity systems. Mitigation requires architectural choices: attribution can be pseudonymous, opt-in for individuals, and decentralized in governance so that no single entity controls the genealogical record.

The gaming problem. Any system that distributes rewards based on measured contributions creates incentives to manipulate the measurements. Citation rings already exist in academia; float-valued attribution would create analogous gaming strategies—inflating one’s own contribution ratios, creating circular attribution networks, or fragmenting work to multiply attribution tokens. Mitigation requires both technical measures (anomaly detection, graph analysis) and social norms (transparent self-assessment, community validation of contribution claims).

The power problem. If contribution ratios determine reward distribution and political influence (Phase 4), then the authority to assess contribution ratios becomes a new locus of power. This paper proposes that assessment be distributed and transparent, but the history of ostensibly distributed systems—from Wikipedia’s editing hierarchies to blockchain’s mining concentration—suggests that power tends to reconcentrate even in architectures designed to prevent it. The float-valued framework does not solve the problem of power; it relocates it from “who owns this idea?” to “who assesses the contribution ratios?” The latter question is arguably more tractable, since assessment can be distributed, audited, and contested, but it is not thereby resolved.

These limitations do not invalidate the transition from Boolean to float-valued attribution. They do, however, constrain the design space and suggest that the transition must be accompanied by ongoing critical scrutiny of the attribution infrastructure itself. The system must trace its own assumptions as rigorously as it traces ideas.

10. Conclusion¶

The core argument of this paper can be stated in a single sentence: intellectual attribution should transition from Boolean (author/not-author) to float (continuous contribution ratios), because the underlying reality is float-valued and the Boolean approximation systematically destroys information. This is not a speculative claim. Information theory demonstrates that soft-decision systems outperform hard-decision systems by a measurable, provable margin. The current intellectual property regime is a hard-decision system. The proposed Visionium framework is a soft-decision system. The expected improvement is not incremental but structural.

Section 2 showed that the Boolean framework is breaking under the weight of its own epicycles—joint authorship, fair use, AI disclosure requirements—each a patch on a system that was designed for a world of single authors and discrete works. Section 3 demonstrated that the propagation of ideas follows a genetic structure—inheritance, mutation, recombination, selection—that no Boolean framework can capture. Section 4 provided the mathematical foundation: soft-decision decoding provably outperforms hard-decision decoding, and the iterative message-passing architecture of modern error-correcting codes offers a precise model for how intellectual consensus can be formed without premature Boolean compression. Section 5 translated these principles into a concrete four-phase implementation roadmap, beginning with weighted citation metadata that can be built on existing infrastructure today.

Why has humanity maintained Boolean attribution for so long? Not because of philosophical conviction, but because of cost. Boolean was cheap, and float was expensive. That cost structure is now inverting. AI can track attribution genealogies that no human institution could manage. Distributed ledgers can maintain contribution records at scales that no centralized registry could handle. The technology to support float-valued circulation exists. The question is no longer whether the transition will occur, but whether it will be designed deliberately or emerge chaotically.

The maxim of the proposed system—prohibit nothing, trace everything—encodes a specific vision of intellectual ecology. Ideas should be free to circulate, mutate, recombine, and be selected by the distributed intelligence of a global network. What the system demands in return is not permission but transparency: the genealogy of each idea must travel with it, so that influence is visible, contribution is acknowledged, and value flows back to its sources.

A star, by itself, has no story. But a constellation does—because someone drew lines between the stars and said, “This pattern means something.” The lines are not in the sky; they are in the act of connection. Every intellectual artifact is a constellation: a pattern drawn between pre-existing points of light, and the act of drawing—the specific trajectory from this star to that one, the choice to include this point and not that one—is itself the creative contribution worth tracing. The genetics of ideas is a system for remembering who drew which lines, how they chose their stars, and where the light came from.

That is what circulation means. That is what the genetics of ideas is for.
