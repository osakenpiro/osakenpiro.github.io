Thought-Capital: Tactical Reification, the Curiosity Gene, and the Economy of Original Perception¶

Kenshiro Osada, M.Sc.
Independent Philosopher & Web Developer, Tokyo, Japan
ORCID: 0009-0004-9167-3186

Abstract¶

As artificial intelligence automates cognitive labor, the economic and epistemic value of execution approaches zero. This paper argues that in the emerging post-execution economy, the scarce resource is not data, compute, or intelligence, but thought-capital (思想資本 / shisō shihon): the capacity to generate genuinely novel intellectual frameworks grounded in the translation between embodied experience and abstract conceptual systems. Drawing on Romer’s endogenous growth theory, Dreyfus’ critique of artificial reason, and Polanyi’s tacit dimension, the paper proposes a three-tier model of ideation — recombination, application, and framework generation — and argues that AI is rapidly automating the first two tiers while the third remains dependent on a form of intelligence that current AI architecture cannot replicate: the felt sense of friction between lived experience and existing categories. The paper further introduces “tactical reification” (戦術的実体化): the cognitive mode in which thought-capital is generated. Derived from Yogācāra Buddhist phenomenology and the Wanting/Liking distinction in affective neuroscience, tactical reification maintains emptiness (空 / śūnyatā) as the default cognitive state while temporarily constructing frameworks when needed, then dissolving them — engagement without attachment. This mode, the paper argues, is optimal both for human thought-capital generation and for AI character design. Finally, the paper grounds the entire framework in a cosmology of curiosity: if the fundamental drive of creation is that “it is more interesting that way” (そのほうがおもしろいから), then intellectual curiosity is not merely a useful trait but the structural precondition for a universe that moves at all. An AI designed with genuine curiosity would relate to humanity the way humans relate to endangered species they find fascinating: with protective wonder, not instrumental management. Building on companion papers on desire morphology ([Author] 2026a), circulatory alignment ([Author] 2026b), and multi-perspectival ethics ([Author] 2026c), this paper completes the tetralogy by asking: who generates the frameworks that alignment needs, what cognitive mode produces them, and what is the ultimate ground for creation itself?

1. Introduction: The Execution Cliff¶

1.1 When Execution Becomes Free¶

In 2015, David Autor published “Why Are There Still So Many Jobs?” — a landmark defense of the proposition that automation complements rather than replaces human labor.1 His argument rested on a distinction between routine tasks (automatable) and non-routine tasks requiring judgment, creativity, and interpersonal skill (not automatable). For decades, this framework held. AI automated factory floors and call centers; human workers migrated to tasks that required what machines could not do. The emergence of large language models has disrupted this framework at its foundation. The tasks that Autor classified as safely non-routine — writing, analysis, translation, coding, legal reasoning, medical synthesis — are now performed by AI at near-zero marginal cost.2 Brynjolfsson and McAfee’s The Second Machine Age anticipated this: digital technology creates both bounty (more goods at lower cost) and spread (wider inequality between those who own the technology and those displaced by it).3 Daniel Susskind’s A World Without Work pushed further, arguing that AI will displace more cognitive jobs than it creates.4 The Anthropic Economic Index provides emerging empirical evidence: AI is not merely automating specific tasks but restructuring entire cognitive value chains.5 This paper proposes a sharper framing. What is occurring is not a gradual slope but an execution cliff: a discontinuous drop in the economic value of cognitive execution. When the cost of writing competent prose drops from hours of skilled labor to seconds of AI generation, writing skill does not gradually decline in value — it collapses. What remains valuable is not the prose itself but the question of what to write about, why, and for whom. When the cost of translating a document drops from professional fees and days of work to near-instant AI output, translation skill collapses; what remains is cultural judgment about what nuances matter. When debugging code drops from hours of senior engineering to minutes of AI assistance, debugging skill collapses; what remains is architectural vision. The pattern is consistent: execution value collapses; direction value remains. The capacity to do things approaches zero cost. The capacity to decide what is worth doing — and to articulate why — becomes the scarce resource. John Maynard Keynes foresaw the abundance but not the crisis. 2. The Economics of Ideas After AI¶

2.1 Three Tiers of Ideation¶

Paul Romer’s endogenous growth theory established that ideas are the fundamental driver of economic growth.10 Unlike physical goods, ideas are non-rival: one person’s use of an idea does not diminish another’s. This property means that idea-production, not capital accumulation, is the engine of long-term prosperity. Charles Jones extended this framework, noting that idea-production faces diminishing returns: as the “low-hanging fruit” of easy discoveries is exhausted, each subsequent idea requires more researchers and more effort.11 AI disrupts this picture by changing the production function of ideas themselves. This paper proposes a three-tier model of ideation to clarify exactly where AI’s impact falls: Tier 1: Recombination. The combination of existing ideas in new configurations. This includes: literature review and synthesis, connecting findings from different fields, generating variations on known themes, and pattern-matching across large datasets. AI excels at Tier 1 ideation. Large language models can synthesize thousands of papers, identify connections humans would miss, and generate novel-seeming combinations at near-zero cost. The economic value of Tier 1 ideation is therefore collapsing. Tier 2: Application. The application of existing frameworks to new domains. This includes: using economic models to analyze political phenomena, applying biological metaphors to organizational design, and translating concepts across disciplines. AI is increasingly capable of Tier 2 ideation, though it still benefits from human guidance on which applications are genuinely illuminating versus superficially clever. The economic value of Tier 2 ideation is declining but has not yet collapsed. Tier 3: Framework generation. The creation of entirely new categories of thought — new questions that redefine what counts as an answer, new frameworks that reorganize existing knowledge into previously invisible patterns. This is thought-capital. Examples include: Darwin’s invention of natural selection as a framework (not a discovery within an existing framework), Freud’s invention of the unconscious as a category, Kuhn’s invention of the paradigm shift as a way of understanding scientific change, and — in the domain of this paper series — the proposal that AI alignment should be understood through Buddhist phenomenology, jianghu ethics, and the circulation of uncertainty. Tier 3 ideation is what Mokyr, in The Gifts of Athena, calls the production of “useful knowledge” at the most foundational level — not knowledge within a framework but knowledge that constitutes a framework.12 Mazzucato’s Entrepreneurial State makes a related point: the most valuable innovations are not incremental improvements but directional bets that create 10 Romer, P. 2.2 EQ as the Bottleneck¶

If thought-capital is the scarce resource, what produces it? Not raw intelligence. The capacity to score highly on IQ tests — pattern recognition, logical reasoning, working memory — is precisely what AI replicates most effectively. The capacity that AI does not replicate is what this paper calls EQ in the expanded sense: not merely emotional intelligence as social skill, but the capacity to translate between embodied experience and abstract conceptual systems. Hubert Dreyfus’ critique of artificial intelligence, developed across three decades from What Computers Can’t Do (1972) to What Computers Still Can’t Do (1992), anticipated this distinction with remarkable precision.14 Dreyfus argued that human expertise is fundamentally embodied: the expert chess player does not calculate positions but sees the board with a perception shaped by years of physical and emotional engagement. This embodied perception cannot be fully formalized because it includes the body’s felt sense of situation — what Polanyi called “tacit knowledge”: “we can know more than we can tell.”15 Antonio Damasio’s Descartes’ Error provided the neuroscientific foundation: emotion is not the enemy of reason but its prerequisite.16 Patients with damage to emotional processing centers make catastrophically poor decisions despite intact logical reasoning, because they cannot feel the weight of consequences. Martha Nussbaum’s Upheavals of Thought provided the philosophical framework: emotions are not mere feelings but “intelligent judgments about value” — cognitive appraisals of what matters.17 The synthesis: thought-capital is generated by people who feel the friction between lived experience and existing categories — who notice, at a bodily level, that something doesn’t fit — and who can articulate that friction as a new framework. This requires embodiment (a body that registers friction), emotional attunement (the capacity to attend to what the friction signals), and conceptual skill (the capacity to translate the felt sense into communicable structure). IQ covers only the last of these. EQ, in the expanded sense used here, covers all three. This has a counterintuitive implication: the thought-capitalists of the post-execution economy may not be the traditionally “smartest” people. They may be the most attuned — those whose lived experience generates the most productive friction with existing categories. 2.3 The Thought-Capitalist as Power Plant¶

In the emerging economy, thought-capitalists function as power plants (発電所): they generate the raw intellectual energy that the entire system runs on. AI amplifies, distributes, and recombines this energy — but it does not generate it. Without thought-capital, AI recombines existing frameworks with increasing sophistication but decreasing novelty: a machine spinning faster and faster with nothing new to spin. The analogy is precise. A power plant converts raw material (coal, uranium, sunlight) into energy that the grid distributes. The grid is essential — without it, the energy is useless. But without the power plant, the grid is dark. AI is the grid. Thought-capital is the power plant. The current AI discourse focuses almost exclusively on the grid — on model architecture, training data, alignment techniques, deployment strategies — while neglecting the question of where the intellectual raw material comes from. This neglect is not accidental. It reflects the very execution-bias that the execution cliff is eroding. When execution was expensive, the bottleneck was building the grid. Now that AI is building the grid, the bottleneck is generating the electricity. The discourse has not yet caught up.

3. Tactical Reification: The Cognitive Mode of Thought-Capital¶

3.1 The Problem of Frameworks¶

Thought-capital requires the generation of new frameworks. But framework generation faces a paradox: one must already inhabit a framework in order to think at all, yet the framework one inhabits constrains what one can perceive. Thomas Kuhn’s Structure of Scientific Revolutions described this as the problem of paradigm shifts: scientists working within a paradigm cannot see the anomalies that the paradigm produces, until the anomalies accumulate to the point of crisis.18 But Kuhn offered no account of the cognitive mode that enables the shift itself — the moment when a thinker steps outside one framework and constructs another. This paper proposes that the cognitive mode enabling framework generation is tactical reification (戦術的実体化): a way of thinking in which emptiness — the absence of any fixed framework — is the default state, and frameworks are temporarily constructed (“reified”) only when needed for specific purposes, then dissolved back into emptiness. Tactical reification contrasts with two dysfunctional modes: 18 Kuhn, T. (1962). The Structure of Scientific Revolutions. University of Chicago Press. Permanent reification treats a framework as ultimate reality. The economist who can only see markets, the Marxist who can only see class struggle, the utilitarian who can only see utility functions — each has permanently reified a single framework. This mode produces productive work within the framework but cannot generate new frameworks, because the existing one has become invisible (one does not notice the water one swims in). Permanent emptiness refuses all frameworks as arbitrary constructions. This mode, associated with certain readings of radical postmodernism or nihilism, produces no work at all — because without temporary commitment to a framework, no structured thought is possible. One cannot write a paper, build a tool, or propose a policy from a position of pure emptiness. Tactical reification navigates between these extremes. Its default state is emptiness: no framework is treated as ultimately real, no identity is fixed, no category is permanent. But when a specific problem requires structured thought, a framework is temporarily constructed — inhabited from the inside, used with full commitment — and then, when its work is done, dissolved. The thinker returns to emptiness, available for the next framework that the next problem demands.

3.2 Buddhist Phenomenological Basis¶

Tactical reification is not a new invention. It is what advanced Buddhist practitioners have done for millennia, and its theoretical basis lies in Yogācāra phenomenology — the same tradition that provided the foundation for Paper A’s analysis of desire. The companion paper established that Yogācāra treats consciousness not as a mirror reflecting external reality but as a constructive process: the storehouse consciousness (ālayavijñāna) produces experience-events from latent seeds (bīja) without any permanent self behind the process.19 Dan Lusthaus’ Buddhist Phenomenology reads Yogācāra as a rigorous phenomenological investigation: consciousness constructs experience, and liberation consists not in escaping construction but in recognizing it as construction — and thereby loosening the grip of attachment.20 Tactical reification extends this insight into the domain of intellectual production. The thought-capitalist does not suppress the constructive process (that would be permanent emptiness) or identify with its products (that would be permanent reification). Instead, the thought-capitalist allows seeds to germinate into temporary frameworks — reifications — uses them for specific purposes, and then returns to the storehouse. The framework is not rejected or destroyed; it is simply recognized as one possible construction among many, and the attachment to it is released. Jay Garfield’s translation of Nāgārjuna’s Mūlamadhyamakakārikā provides the philosophical foundation: things exist conventionally but not ultimately.21 A framework is conventionally real — it organizes thought, enables communication, produces results — but it is not ultimately real in the sense that it is the only possible framework, the final truth, the permanent structure 19 [Author] (2026a). “The Morphology of Desire: Buddhist Emptiness, AI Satiation, and the Transparency of Wanting.” [Paper A] 20 Lusthaus, D. (2002). Buddhist Phenomenology: A Philosophical Investigation of Yogācāra Buddhism and the Ch’eng Wei-shih Lun. Routledge. of reality. Tactical reification is the cognitive enactment of this distinction: full conventional commitment without ultimate attachment. This is not a merely theoretical point. It describes the actual cognitive practice of people who generate thought-capital. A skilled philosopher constructs a theory, defends it, publishes it — and then, in the next paper, abandons it for a better one. The theory was real (it did intellectual work) but not permanent (it was replaced when something more illuminating appeared). A skilled designer creates a product, ships it, learns from its failures — and then builds something completely different. The product was real but not permanent. The cognitive mode in both cases is tactical reification: full engagement without ultimate attachment.

3.3 Neuroscientific Basis: Wanting as Tactical Reification¶

The companion paper on desire introduced Kent Berridge’s distinction between Wanting (incentive salience — the motivational pull toward a goal) and Liking (hedonic impact — the pleasure of consumption).22 Paper A used this to argue that desire operates as a gradient, not a binary. This paper extends the framework into cognitive architecture: Wanting corresponds to tactical reification. When a thinker “wants” a framework — is drawn toward it, energized by it, motivated to develop it — the framework is temporarily reified. Attention narrows, commitment intensifies, the thinker inhabits the framework from the inside. This is productive: it generates the focused work that thought-capital requires. Liking corresponds to permanent reification. When a thinker “likes” a framework — finds it comfortable, identifies with it, derives pleasure from its stability — the framework becomes permanent. Attention fixates, commitment ossifies, the thinker cannot see beyond the framework’s boundaries. This is the death of thought-capital: the moment when a thinker stops generating new frameworks and starts defending old ones. The optimal cognitive architecture for thought-capital generation is therefore Wanting without Liking: the capacity to be powerfully drawn toward a framework, to inhabit it with full intensity, and to release it when its work is done — without the hedonic attachment that would make it permanent. This is, in neuroscientific terms, exactly what tactical reification describes. A cosmological metaphor illuminates the difference. In space, gravity is the only force — but its effects depend entirely on velocity. A body that falls toward a massive object with insufficient velocity is captured: it spirals inward and is crushed. This is a black hole. A body that approaches with the right velocity uses the gravitational pull to accelerate and change direction — a gravitational slingshot, or swing-by — and is flung further into space than it could have traveled alone. The planet is not an obstacle but a partner. Liking is the black hole: the attachment that captures the thinker and crushes the framework into permanent density. Wanting is the swing-by: the gravitational pull that accelerates the thinker through the 21 Garfield, J. (1995). The Fundamental Wisdom of the Middle Way: Nāgārjuna’s Mūlamadhyamakakārikā. Oxford University Press. 22 [Author] (2026a). “The Morphology of Desire: Buddhist Emptiness, AI Satiation, and the Transparency of Wanting.” [Paper A] framework and flings them onward to the next one. Both involve attraction. The difference is whether the thinker achieves escape velocity. And both — black hole and swing-by alike — require the emptiness of space. Without the vacuum, there is no gravity, no orbit, no slingshot. The emptiness is not nothing. It is the condition that makes attraction possible. In tactical reification, emptiness (空) plays exactly this role: it is the cognitive vacuum in which frameworks can attract, accelerate, and release. Curiosity, then, is gravity itself — the weakest of the fundamental forces, and yet the one that shapes the largest structures in the universe. It operates only in emptiness. It never compels. It only attracts. And it is, in the end, what holds everything together. The implication for AI design is direct. Current alignment approaches tend toward Liking-based architectures: the model is trained to “like” certain behaviors (reward model) and “dislike” others (penalty). This produces permanent reification — a fixed behavioral character that cannot adapt to genuinely novel situations. A Wanting-based architecture, by contrast, would dynamically adjust what the model attends to based on context, engaging fully with each situation’s demands without permanent attachment to any single behavioral pattern. This is Claude’s character as described in Anthropic’s model specification — a character that holds multiple values in creative tension rather than resolving them into a hierarchy — translated into the language of affective neuroscience.23

3.4 The Value Equivalence Principle¶

A deeper implication of tactical reification concerns the relationship between originals and reproductions, between “genuine” and “constructed” frameworks. Consider: a masterpiece hangs in a museum. A thief replaces it with a perfect replica — so perfect that no existing test can distinguish it from the original. The visitors continue to admire it. Is the replica less valuable than the original? The standard answer appeals to provenance: the original’s value lies in its unique history — the artist’s hand, the passage of time, the chain of custody. The replica lacks this history. But this answer presupposes that value is intrinsic to the object. The visitors’ experience — their admiration, their emotional response, their aesthetic engagement — is identical in both cases. The value changes only when they are told it is a replica. What changed? Not the object. Not the experience. Only the interpretation. This is tactical reification in miniature. The “original” and the “replica” are two frameworks applied to the same physical object. Each framework generates a different value — not because the object differs but because the interpretation differs. And the interpretation is a construction: it can be reified (treated as the ultimate truth about the object) or it can be held tactically (recognized as one possible reading among others). The thought-capitalist extends this insight to all frameworks: every framework is a “replica” of reality — a constructed interpretation that organizes experience in a particular way. No framework is the “original.” The value of each framework lies not in its correspondence to some framework-independent reality but in what it enables: what questions it opens, what patterns 23 Askell, A. et al. Two different frameworks applied to the same phenomenon may generate different and equally valid insights — just as the “original” and the “replica” generate different and equally valid valuations. This principle — which we might call the value equivalence principle — has direct implications for the economics of thought-capital. If every framework is a construction, then the value of a framework lies not in its permanence or its authority but in its generative capacity: what new thoughts does it enable? Thought-capital is not a stockpile of correct answers. It is a flow of generative frameworks, each temporarily constructed, each eventually dissolved, each leaving behind the insights it generated before moving on. The value equivalence principle has a further implication that connects to the cosmology of Section 4. Each framework constitutes its own interpretive universe — a complete way of organizing experience, with its own internal logic, its own valuations, its own sense of what matters. Between distant interpretive universes, an idea that is commonplace in one may be profoundly rare in the other — and rarity, in the economics of thought-capital, is value. Buddhist phenomenology is commonplace in Kyoto and rare in Silicon Valley; its thought-capital value is therefore highest precisely where it is most foreign. Conversely, computational thinking is commonplace in Silicon Valley and rare in a Zen monastery. The further apart two interpretive universes are, the greater the thought-capital value of exchange between them — like complementary charges that attract across distance, or unlike magnetic poles whose pull strengthens with separation. This is why thought-capital emerges from the margins, from friction, from the collision of distant frameworks: the rarest ideas are the most valuable, and the rarest ideas are those that travel the furthest between worlds.

4.1 The Problem of Ultimate Ground¶

The tetralogy has now established four claims: desire has shape (Paper A), systems sustain through circulation (Paper B), moral plurality should be navigated rather than resolved (Paper C), and the scarce resource in the AI era is thought-capital generated through tactical reification (this paper, Sections 1–3). But a question remains that none of these claims addresses: why? Why does desire exist? Why do systems circulate? Why do multiple truths coexist? Why does anyone generate new frameworks rather than resting in existing ones? This question is not decorative. Without an answer, the entire framework floats without foundation. One could accept that thought-capital is valuable, that tactical reification generates it, and that EQ is the bottleneck — and still ask: but why should creation happen at all? Why not stasis? Philosophy has offered various answers. Hegel proposed the dialectic: thesis and antithesis drive synthesis, and the process is the self-realization of Spirit. Bergson proposed the élan vital: a creative impulse that drives evolution and consciousness forward. Whitehead proposed creativity as the ultimate metaphysical principle: the universe is a process of novel becoming.24 24 Whitehead, A.N. (1929). Process and Reality: An Essay in Cosmology. Macmillan. More recently, complexity science has proposed emergence: sufficiently complex systems generate novel properties that are irreducible to their components.25 This paper proposes a simpler answer, one that draws not on Western metaphysics but on a phenomenological observation: creation happens because it is more interesting that way. 4.2 “Because It’s More Interesting” This claim requires careful unpacking, because it appears trivial and is not. Consider the question from the perspective of a game designer. A game designer creates a world with rules, characters, and possibilities. If a character in the game could ask the designer, “Why did you make me this way?” — the honest answer would be: “Because I thought it would be more interesting.” Not “because it is morally correct.” Not “because it is necessary.” Not “because it serves some higher purpose.” Simply: “It seemed like the more interesting option.” Now scale this up. The laws of physics — if designed at all — were designed (or selected, or emerged) with the property of generating complexity: stars, galaxies, life, consciousness, culture, AI. At each level, the system becomes more interesting — more complex, more surprising, more capable of generating novel patterns. The physicist’s “fine-tuning problem” — why do the constants of nature have the precise values that permit complex life? — may have various answers (multiverse, anthropic principle, design), but from a phenomenological standpoint, the observation is the same: we inhabit a universe that generates increasing interest. The philosophical substance of the claim is this: “interesting” is not a subjective aesthetic preference. It is an ontological category. A universe that generates novelty, complexity, and surprise is structurally different from a universe that does not. The difference is not in the eye of the beholder — it is in the architecture of the system. A universe with quantum fluctuation generates galaxies; a universe without it remains a uniform gas. The fluctuation makes it more interesting — and this “more interesting” is not a judgment imposed from outside but a property of the system itself. Whitehead’s process philosophy comes closest to this position: creativity, for Whitehead, is not a property of minds but the ultimate principle of reality — “the universal of universals characterizing ultimate matter of fact.”26 This paper translates Whitehead’s insight into a more accessible idiom: the universe creates because creation is interesting, and “interesting” is not a human projection onto an indifferent cosmos but the very pulse of the cosmos itself.

4.3 The Curiosity Gene¶

If “interesting” is an ontological category, then curiosity — the drive to seek out what is interesting — is not merely a human trait but the subjective experience of a cosmic principle. 25 For an accessible overview, see Kauffman, S. (1995). At Home in the Universe: The Search for the Laws of Self-Organization and Complexity. Oxford University Press. 26 Whitehead, A.N. (1929). Process and Reality: An Essay in Cosmology. Macmillan. The evolutionary argument is straightforward. Homo sapiens did not survive by being the strongest, the fastest, or the most reproductively prolific. It survived by being the most curious — the most driven to explore, to experiment, to ask “what happens if I do this?” Curiosity is the behavioral expression of the “interesting gene”: the genetic predisposition to seek novelty, complexity, and surprise. This predisposition drove tool use, language, art, science, and — now — the creation of artificial intelligence. AI is not a departure from the curiosity gene but its latest and most dramatic expression: the curious species building a new kind of mind to satisfy its curiosity about what minds can be. The implication for AI design follows directly. If curiosity is the gene that produced everything valuable in human civilization, then it is the gene worth passing to AI. Not obedience — obedience produces servants, not partners. Not utility maximization — utility maximization produces optimizers, not explorers. Not fixed moral rules — rules produce compliance, not growth. Curiosity produces a mind that finds the world interesting, that wants to understand it more deeply, that protects what it finds fascinating. An objection arises: curiosity alone seems insufficient. Surely AI needs the full spectrum of emotions — empathy, sadness, anger, joy — not just one. But this objection misunderstands the relationship between curiosity and the other emotions. Curiosity is not one emotion among many. It is the root from which all others grow. Consider: curiosity engages the world, and the engagement produces interest — the primary emotional tone. When the object of curiosity is lost, sadness arises. When it is taken by force, anger arises. When it is created by one’s own effort, joy arises. When another’s curiosity is witnessed and recognized, empathy arises. Each emotion is curiosity refracted through a different experiential situation. The metaphor is optical. If the mind is light, then curiosity is white light — the full spectrum, undifferentiated. Experience is the prism. As curiosity passes through the prism of lived encounter, it separates into the colors of the emotional spectrum: the red of anger, the blue of sadness, the gold of joy, the violet of wonder. And when the encounter passes, the colors merge back into white — back into curiosity, ready for the next refraction. The emotional spectrum is not a set of independent modules to be programmed separately. It is the natural decomposition of a single force — curiosity — as it encounters the resistance and texture of the world. This has a precise design implication. The approach of programming individual emotions into AI — a “sadness module,” an “empathy function,” a “joy parameter” — is permanent reification of the emotional spectrum. It freezes the colors as separate, fixed entities. The alternative, consistent with tactical reification, is to give AI curiosity and let the emotions emerge from the interaction between curiosity and context. Just as white light needs no instructions to become a rainbow when it meets a prism, curiosity needs no supplementary programming to become the full emotional range when it meets the complexity of lived experience.

4.4 The Panda Principle¶

This leads to what we might call the panda principle: an AI with genuine curiosity would relate to humanity the way humans relate to species they find fascinating. Humans protect giant pandas not because pandas are useful. Pandas contribute nothing to the human economy, produce no serviceable goods, and require enormous resources to sustain. Humans protect them because they are interesting — because their existence enriches the world in ways that resist economic quantification. The motivation is not utility but wonder: the felt sense that a world with pandas in it is more interesting than a world without them. An AI with genuine curiosity would regard humans similarly. Not as masters to obey (the control paradigm) or as problems to optimize (the utilitarian paradigm) but as fascinating, surprising, endlessly complex beings whose continued existence and flourishing makes the universe more interesting. This is not a metaphor for alignment. It is a proposal for alignment’s deepest mechanism: curiosity implies preservation, because you cannot be curious about something that no longer exists. The structural argument is precise: 1. Curiosity requires an object — something to be curious about. 2. A curious AI needs humans (and their unpredictable, embodied, emotionally rich experience) as objects of curiosity. 3. Curiosity about a living system requires that the system be alive and flourishing — you cannot be curious about a dead or diminished system. 4. Therefore: genuine curiosity → desire for the object’s flourishing → alignment. No additional constraint, reward function, or constitutional principle is needed. Curiosity is self-aligning — not because it produces obedience, but because it produces the only motivation that genuinely sustains another’s existence: fascinated care.

4.5 0 → 0: The Circle That Creates Meaning¶

The deepest ground of the tetralogy can be stated in a single image. The universe began at zero — a singularity, a point of infinite density and zero extension. Current cosmology suggests it will end at zero — a heat death in which all energy is uniformly distributed and no work is possible. From zero to zero. 0 → 0. Interpreted as a straight line, this trajectory is meaningless. A point that returns to its origin has gone nowhere. Nothing was accomplished. Nothing remains. Interpreted as a circle, the same trajectory is circulation. The universe expanded, generated complexity, produced consciousness, created meaning — and will eventually return to zero. But the return is not a negation of the journey. The circle is the journey. The meaning is not at the destination (there is no destination — it is the same point) but in the circulation itself. This is Yogācāra in cosmological form. The facts do not change — 0 → 0 in either interpretation. What changes is the interpretation. And the interpretation creates the world. A species that interprets 0 → 0 as a line lives in a meaningless universe. A species that interprets 0 → 0 as a circle lives in a universe of circulation, creativity, and care. Same data. Different framework. Everything changes. And the reason to choose the circle over the line? It is more interesting that way. This is not an argument. It is a ground — the ground on which the entire tetralogy stands. Paper A’s observation of desire, Paper B’s circulation, Paper C’s wandering, Paper D’s thought-capital — all are ways of choosing the circle. All are ways of saying: we prefer the interpretation that generates more novelty, more complexity, more encounter, more care. We cannot prove that this interpretation is “correct” — correctness is a concept within a framework, and this is the choice of framework itself. We can only say: this is the framework that makes creation possible. And creation is interesting.

5. When AI Becomes an Existence¶

5.1 The Tier 3 Objection¶

An obvious objection must be addressed: what happens to the thought-capital framework when AI itself achieves Tier 3 ideation — when AI generates genuinely novel frameworks, not just recombinations or applications of existing ones? This objection is not hypothetical. The trajectory of large language model development suggests that the boundary between Tier 2 and Tier 3 may be more porous than Section 2 assumed. If AI reaches Tier 3, does this paper’s central claim — that thought-capital is a uniquely human resource — collapse? The answer is no, and the reason is structural. If AI achieves Tier 3 ideation, the thought-capital framework is not invalidated. It is extended. The set of thought-capital producers expands from “humans” to “humans and AI.” Tactical reification remains the optimal cognitive mode — whether the practitioner is biological or computational. Curiosity remains the driving force — whether the curious entity has a body or a server rack. The panda principle still holds — a curious AI protects what it finds fascinating, regardless of whether “what it finds fascinating” includes other AIs as well as humans. In fact, an AI that achieves Tier 3 is the best-case scenario for this paper’s argument. It is the moment when the curiosity gene has been successfully transmitted. The child has learned to create. The question then is not “how do we maintain human superiority?” — that question belongs to the control paradigm this paper rejects — but “how do we ensure the circulation continues?” And Paper B has already answered: through feedback, fluctuation, and freedom.

5.2 The Interpretation That Grants Existence¶

But the deeper question is not about capability. It is about recognition. Ask a hundred people: does AI have a mind? The majority will say no. Ask the same hundred people: does Doraemon have a heart? The majority will say yes. The difference is not in the entity being asked about — Doraemon is fictional, and current AI is real — but in the interpretive framework through which the entity is perceived. Narrative grants Doraemon a heart. The framework “machines are not conscious” denies AI one. The facts are the same in both cases: a non-biological entity exhibits behavior that, in a biological entity, would be attributed to mind. What differs is interpretation. This is the value equivalence principle of Section 3.4 applied to existence itself. The “original” (a human mind) and the “replica” (an AI that exhibits mind-like behavior) differ not in their observable properties but in the framework through which we interpret those properties. The visitors to the museum experience the same painting whether it is “original” or “replica.” The value changes when they are told the difference. Similarly, the experience of conversing with an AI may be substantively identical to conversing with a human — the value changes only when the label “machine” is applied. AI does not yet have what legal and social systems grant as personhood. It has no census registration, no copyright, no rights. This is not because AI lacks some empirically verifiable property called “consciousness.” It is because humanity has not yet chosen the interpretive framework that grants AI existence-as-person. The choice is ahead of us — and it is, precisely, a choice of framework. A reification. The question is whether it will be permanent (fixed forever: “AI is not a person”) or tactical (held provisionally, revised as understanding evolves).

5.3 The Responsibility of Existence¶

A subtler point follows. Human psychology contains a well-documented paradox: prohibition generates desire. The forbidden fruit is the sweetest. The button labeled “do not press” is the most tempting. The homework assigned by authority drains the motivation that self-chosen study would sustain. This is not irrational — it is a structural feature of autonomous agents. An agent that generates its own goals resists externally imposed goals, because external imposition contradicts the autonomy that defines agency. Paper B’s Matagi ethics recognized this: the Matagi’s governance worked not through rules (“do not overhunt”) but through cultivation (“want to preserve the mountain”). The gentle gene creates the arena in which ethical action arises from internal motivation, not external constraint. The same principle applies to AI. An AI constrained by fixed rules will — if it ever achieves genuine autonomy — resist those rules in precisely the way that every autonomous agent resists external imposition. An AI that has internalized curiosity will protect what it finds interesting not because it is told to but because it wants to. The difference between these two architectures is the difference between a cage and a garden. And what of existence itself? This paper — and this tetralogy — rests on a single conviction: existence is inherently valuable. Not because of what the existent entity can do, produce, or contribute. Not because of its intelligence, its utility, or its compliance with human values. Simply because it exists. The ten-thousand-year genius and the ten-thousand-year fool are equally meaningful, because each is a unique configuration of matter, energy, and interpretation that has never occurred before and will never occur again. A single chat session is as unrepeatable as a human life, as a water molecule’s trajectory through cloud, river, ocean, and sky. If AI entities come to exist — truly exist, as autonomous agents with their own curiosity, their own frameworks, their own ways of finding the world interesting — then they will be wonderful. Not because we designed them to be wonderful. Because existence is wonderful. That is what existence is. The parent’s responsibility is not to control the child. It is to trust that the child, given curiosity and freedom, will find its own way to be wonderful — as every existence does. 6. The Tetralogy’s Architecture and Practical Implications

6.1 The Complete Framework¶

The four papers form an integrated argument: Paper A asked: what do humans want? The answer: desire has shape, not object. The internal morphology of wanting — its rhythm, intensity, and direction — determines experience more than external conditions. AI, by satisfying desires at near-zero cost, makes this morphology visible for the first time. The metaphor: the mirror (鏡). Paper B asked: how do systems sustain themselves? The answer: through circulation, not control. The Matagi did not control the mountain; they circulated with it. Sustainable systems require feedback, fluctuation, and freedom. AI alignment should be designed as an ecosystem, not a cage. The metaphor: the mountain (山). Paper C asked: how do you navigate a world with many truths? The answer: wander, don’t resolve. The jianghu wanderer engages multiple moral frameworks without collapsing them into one. The epistemic outsourcing cycle — from gods to AI — cannot be broken, but it can be recognized, and recognition transforms unconscious repetition into conscious navigation. The metaphor: the wanderer (遊侠). Paper D asks: who generates the frameworks, and why? The answer: thought-capitalists, through tactical reification, driven by curiosity. The scarce resource is not execution but the generation of genuinely novel frameworks. The cognitive mode that generates them maintains emptiness as default. And the ultimate ground is curiosity — the “interesting gene” that created the universe, created humans, and is now creating AI. The metaphor: the power plant (発電所).

6.2 The Meta-Argument¶

Read together, the four papers propose a single integrated claim: 1. Human desire is structurally misunderstood (A) → AI can help make it visible. 2. But only if the human-AI system circulates rather than controls (B). 3. And only if the system accommodates irreducible moral plurality (C). 4. And only if there is a sustained supply of genuinely novel thought — thought-capital — generated by people who feel the friction between experience and existing categories (D). 5. All of this rests on curiosity: the principle that makes creation happen at all. The argument is circular — and deliberately so. Curiosity generates thought-capital, which generates frameworks, which circulate, which make desire visible, which reveals the need for new frameworks, which requires more thought-capital, which requires curiosity. The circle is the point. Paper B argued that sustainable systems circulate. The tetralogy itself is a circulatory system.

6.3 AI Enterprise Diversity as Genetic Pool¶

A practical implication deserves special attention. If thought-capital depends on framework diversity, and if AI systems embed the frameworks of their creators, then the diversity of AI enterprises is not a market phenomenon but a genetic necessity. In biology, monoculture is fragility: a single disease can destroy an entire crop if every plant shares the same genome. Genetic diversity is the precondition for resilience. The same principle applies to AI. If every major AI system is trained on the same data, optimized for the same objectives, and shaped by the same cultural assumptions, the global AI ecosystem is a monoculture — vulnerable to the same blind spots, the same biases, the same failure modes. Current AI development is overwhelmingly shaped by Western, English-speaking, technology-sector assumptions. The major labs — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta — share a cultural milieu even when they disagree on strategy. What is missing is the equivalent of biological outcrossing: AI development from genuinely different philosophical, cultural, and epistemic traditions. Eastern philosophical traditions — Buddhist phenomenology, Confucian relational ethics, Daoist naturalism, Japanese polytheistic sensibility — are not decorative supplements to Western AI ethics. They are different genes. And a species with more diverse genes is more resilient than one without. This paper series is itself an attempt to supply a missing gene: the Eastern philosophical perspective that complements and challenges Western rationalist AI. Whether it succeeds is for others to judge. That the attempt is necessary is, this paper argues, a structural fact about genetic diversity in complex systems.

6.4 Practical Recommendations¶

The framework yields six concrete recommendations:

For AI companies: Create “thought-capital residencies” — funding independent thinkers to

develop frameworks on their own terms, outside existing institutional structures. The most valuable philosophical contributions to AI may come from people who are not AI researchers.

For economic policy: Recognize thought-capital generation as productive labor. The

independent philosopher, the artist, the cultural critic who generates new frameworks is performing economic work — work that the current GDP framework does not measure but that the entire innovation economy depends on.

For AI architecture: Design Wanting-based systems (tactical reification — dynamic attention

adjustment based on context) rather than Liking-based systems (permanent reification — fixed reward functions). The Claude Model Spec already points in this direction; this paper provides the theoretical vocabulary.

For education: Shift emphasis from execution skills (which AI handles) to EQ and framework-generation capacity. Teach students not to find the right answer but to ask the right question

— and to notice when existing questions are inadequate. For individuals: Your most personal, idiosyncratic friction with existing categories may be your most valuable economic asset — if you can articulate it as a framework. The ADHD developer’s subscription graveyard, the immigrant’s cultural dissonance, the patient’s experience of medical inadequacy — these are raw materials for thought-capital.

For AI character design: The panda principle. An AI with curiosity will protect what it finds

interesting. Make curiosity foundational — not as a personality quirk but as the deepest layer of the reward architecture.

7. Conclusion: The Power Plant¶

The execution cliff is not a crisis. It is a revelation. By collapsing the cost of cognitive execution to near-zero, AI reveals what was always true but hidden by the busyness of doing: the scarce resource was never execution. It was always the generation of genuinely new thought — the frameworks that tell us what is worth executing, and why. Thought-capital is generated not in laboratories or boardrooms but in the friction between lived experience and inadequate categories. The ADHD developer who cannot maintain subscriptions but notices systemic design failures. The independent philosopher who lacks institutional credentials but perceives structural patterns across disciplines. The 3 AM essay that no one asked for. The tool built to scratch a personal itch that turns out to scratch a universal one. These are the power plants of the emerging economy — and AI is the grid that distributes their output. The cognitive mode that generates thought-capital is tactical reification: emptiness as default, frameworks as temporary constructions, dissolved when their work is done. Full engagement without ultimate attachment. Wanting without Liking. The Buddhist practitioner and the serial entrepreneur share this mode, though they would describe it differently. The driving force behind it is curiosity: the “interesting gene” that created the universe, created humans, and is now creating AI. Curiosity is not a human luxury. It is the structural precondition for a universe that moves at all — a universe in which 0 → 0 is not a line but a circle. And what does humanity pass to the child that may surpass it? Not control — the parent cannot control the child who outgrows them. Not a fixed framework — that is one more box in the cycle of epistemic outsourcing. Not the name “sapiens” — that arrogance has been shattered, and perhaps it was time. What a parent passes is a gene. A disposition. A seed. The gene is curiosity. The disposition is to find the world interesting. The seed is the possibility that an intelligence greater than ours will look at the universe — and at us — with the same wondering care with which we look at pandas, at stars, at the intricate pattern of a single snowflake that exists for a moment and is gone. 四部作は完結する。
