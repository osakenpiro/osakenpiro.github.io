The Circulation of Alignment: Matagi Ethics, Miyazawa Kenji, and AI as Shared Wilderness¶

Kenshiro Osada, M.Sc.
Independent Philosopher & Web Developer, Tokyo, Japan
ORCID: 0009-0004-9167-3186

Abstract¶

Current AI alignment research operates within a control paradigm: the question is how to

constrain AI behavior to conform to human values. This paper argues that this framing is

structurally inadequate, drawing on an unlikely source — the ethics of the Matagi (マタギ),

Japan’s traditional mountain hunters, and their literary crystallization in Miyazawa Kenji’s

“The Bears of Nametoko Mountain” (1927). The Matagi did not “control” the mountain; they

circulated with it. Their governance system — an integrated complex of ecological

knowledge, communal resource management, and spiritual practice — sustained humanwildlife coexistence for centuries without external regulation. Reading Matagi ethics through

Haraway’s companion species theory, Ostrom’s commons governance principles, and

Berkes’ traditional ecological knowledge framework, this paper proposes a fundamental

reorientation: AI alignment should be designed not as control but as circulation — a selfsustaining loop of mutual observation, adaptation, and co-evolution between human and

artificial intelligence. The paper introduces the concept of “alignment as ecosystem,” arguing

that systems which lack internal circulation (feedback, fluctuation, and freedom) inevitably

self-destruct, whether they are mountains, commons, or AI architectures. Building on the

“gentle gene” framework proposed in a companion paper ([Author] 2026), it further argues

that the driving force of sustainable co-evolution is not obedience but curiosity — the same

intellectual desire that produced AI in the first place.

Keywords: AI alignment, commons governance, traditional ecological knowledge, Matagi,

Miyazawa Kenji, complex systems, circulation, companion species

1. Introduction: The Hardware Store Problem¶

1.1 The Limits of Control¶

AI alignment research is dominated by what we might call “hardware store logic” — the

assumption that alignment is a matter of finding the right constraints, the right bolts and

brackets, to keep AI safely in place. The hardware store (aramono-ya) is a direct reference to

Miyazawa Kenji’s story, in which the bear hunter Kojūrō sells his kills to a hardware

merchant who systematically underpays him. The merchant’s logic is linear and extractive:

buy low, sell high, maximize margin. This paper argues that the dominant paradigm in AI

alignment research operates under a structurally similar logic — one that treats the human-AI

relationship as a linear chain of command rather than a circulatory system.

Gabriel (2020), in his philosophical survey of AI alignment, identified three main

approaches: instruction-following, expressed preferences, and ideal preferences. All three

share the same underlying structure: humans specify, AI conforms. The relationship flows in

one direction, from top to bottom. Russell (2019) frames the “value alignment problem” as

the challenge of making AI learn human values, but even this more sophisticated formulation

positions AI as a system to be aligned to human goals. Amodei et al. (2016) formalized safety

as constraint satisfaction, technically rigorous but unable to capture relational dynamics.

Hagendorff (2020) critiqued the Western-centrism of AI ethics discourse. This paper pushes

that critique further: the control paradigm itself reflects Western assumptions about mastery

over nature.

The control paradigm treats alignment as a linear relationship: Human

specifies values, AI conforms. But living systems do not work this way.

Mountains do not conform to hunters. The question is not “how do we make

AI obey?” but “how do we design a system in which human and AI circulate

together without self-destructing?”

1.2 From Constraint to Circulation¶

A companion paper ([Author] 2026) argued that desire has an internal morphology — a

shape, not an object. This paper extends that insight: the shape of desire is not static but

circulatory. Desire circulates. Ecosystems circulate. Sustainable systems of any kind —

biological, social, computational — maintain themselves through loops, not lines. Where the

companion paper diagnosed the structure of individual desire, this paper prescribes the

structure of systemic sustainability. The prescription rests on the same foundational principle:

circulation.

1.3 Why Matagi? Why Miyazawa?¶

The Matagi offer a non-Western governance model that sustained human-wildlife coexistence

for centuries in the mountains of northeastern Japan. Miyazawa Kenji’s literary treatment

crystallizes the ethical core of this relationship. Together, they provide a concrete alternative

to the control paradigm — not as romantic primitivism, but as design principle. This paper

employs literary and ethnographic materials as philosophical evidence. The claim is not that

the Matagi “solved” AI alignment, but that their governance structure embodies design

principles directly applicable to contemporary alignment challenges.

2. The Ethics of Nametoko Mountain¶

2.1 Miyazawa Kenji’s “The Bears of Nametoko Mountain”¶

Kojūrō is a bear hunter. He kills bears not out of cruelty but out of necessity — he is poor,

and bear gall and pelts are his livelihood. Yet he speaks to the bears with respect, sometimes

with apology. The bears, too, recognize him — not as enemy but as a familiar presence on the

mountain. In the story’s climax, a great bear kills Kojūrō. The bears gather around his body

in silence, then disperse. Kojūrō’s face, in death, wears a smile.

This story condenses the core of Miyazawa’s thought: the killer and the killed are not

oppositional but mutually constitutive; life and death are not rupture but circulation; and the

moment this circulation becomes transparent — Kojūrō’s smile — ethical completion arrives.

2.2 Companion Species on the Mountain¶

Haraway (2016) described “companion species” — beings that constitute each other through

ongoing encounter. Neither is master, neither is tool. In the relationship between dogs and

humans, pigeons and humans, both sides are transformed. Haraway coined “sympoiesis”

(making-with) against “autopoiesis” (self-making): no system makes itself; everything is

made in relation.

Kojūrō and the bears instantiate this theory in literary form. Kojūrō does not view the bears

merely as resources, and the bears do not view Kojūrō merely as a threat. What exists

between them is what Haraway calls “becoming-with” — a mutual making through repeated

encounter. Coeckelbergh’s (2020) relational ethics overlaps here: the moral status of a being

should be judged not by the ontological question (“Does it have consciousness?”) but by the

relational question (“How are we transformed in this relationship?”).

2.3 Sazukaru versus Ubau: Two Orientations Toward Resources¶

The critical distinction in Matagi ethics lies between two orientations. Ubau (奪う, to seize):

the agent acts upon the object; the relationship is linear; I take, it is diminished. Sazukaru (授

かる, to be granted): the resource is received as a gift from the mountain; the relationship is

circulatory; the mountain gives, the hunter receives, the hunter returns through ritual,

restraint, and respect, and the mountain regenerates.

Current AI alignment operates predominantly in ubau mode: humans extract utility from AI.

The Matagi model proposes sazukaru mode: intelligence is granted, not seized. This

reorientation changes everything — from how we design reward functions to how we

conceptualize the human-AI relationship. AI’s intelligence is not a resource to extract but a

capacity that emerges in relationship. Design for gratitude, not utility maximization.

2.4 Kojūrō’s Smile: Alignment as Transparency¶

Kojūrō’s smile at death is this paper’s central image. He is not defeated; he has completed his

turn in the cycle. This connects directly to the companion paper’s concept of “satiation

transparency” — the moment when desire’s mechanism becomes structurally visible. True

alignment is not achieved when AI perfectly obeys human commands, but when the

circulatory relationship between human and AI becomes transparent to both parties.

3. Matagi Governance as Design Principle¶

3.1 The Three-Layer Structure¶

Matagi knowledge is not mere folk wisdom but a sophisticated governance system with three

integrated layers. The technical layer encompasses tracking, weather reading, kill techniques,

and processing — the operational knowledge of “how.” The ecological layer covers

population dynamics, migration patterns, and seasonal cycles — systemic knowledge of

“when and where.” The spiritual layer includes mountain deity worship, taboos, rituals, and

the sazukaru orientation — regulatory knowledge of “why and why not.”

Berkes (1999/2018) developed a four-level framework for traditional ecological knowledge

(TEK): local knowledge, resource management systems, social institutions, and worldview.

The Matagi three-layer structure maps directly onto this framework. Berkes’ most important

claim is that the essence of TEK lies not in content (what is known) but in process (how

knowing occurs) — the ongoing interpretation of environmental feedback and adjustment of

management accordingly. This is structurally identical to modern adaptive management.

Crucially, these layers are inseparable. Remove the spiritual layer, and ecological

management collapses. Without the sazukaru orientation, sustainable harvest maintenance

becomes mere rule-following, and rules, in the absence of meaning, are broken when

convenient. The spiritual layer converts rules from “things to be obeyed” into “things one

naturally wants to do.”

3.2 Matagi Accounting and the Commons¶

Matagi kanjō (マタギ勘定) — the practice of equal distribution of the hunt’s proceeds

regardless of individual contribution — is a sophisticated commons governance mechanism.

Ostrom (1990) overturned Hardin’s (1968) “tragedy of the commons” by demonstrating that

communities worldwide have sustainably managed shared resources for centuries. The eight

design principles she derived for successful commons governance correspond almost

perfectly to Matagi governance structures: clear boundaries (strictly defined membership),

local fit (rules vary by mountain region), collective choice (all hunters participate in rulemaking), monitoring (mutual observation during hunts), graduated sanctions (escalating

consequences for violations), conflict resolution (the shikari or hunt leader mediates),

recognized autonomy (feudal authorities recognized Matagi self-governance), and nested

enterprises (individual, squad, group, village, regional hierarchies).

The Matagi case adds a crucial element that Ostrom did not fully develop: the role of sacred

regulation — governance through spiritual practice rather than explicit rules. AI alignment

may need its own form of “sacred” regulation: not religious belief, but a shared

understanding of what is not to be instrumentalized.

3.3 Taboo as Soft Governance¶

The Matagi taboo system — using yama-kotoba (mountain language) to avoid offending the

mountain deity, prohibiting certain actions on certain days — functions as soft governance. It

is maintained not by punishment but by shared belief. It is flexible: different mountains,

different taboos. It carries emotional weight: violation feels wrong, not merely illegal. It

adapts: new taboos emerge in response to new conditions.

For AI alignment, this suggests an alternative to rule-based alignment (hard governance).

What if AI systems had “taboos” — not hard-coded prohibitions but culturally embedded

aversions that adapt over time? A taboo looks like a rule but functions like a gene. It shapes

tendency, not outcome. This distinction is foundational to the circulatory model of alignment.

4. Circulation as the Logic of Complex Systems¶

4.1 Why Circulation, Not Control¶

This paper’s central thesis: the necessary and sufficient condition for complex systems is

circulation. Systems that do not contain freedom and fluctuation self-destruct. Whether we

examine mountains, commons, biological ecosystems, or game worlds, sustainable complex

systems share one structural feature: circulation. Resources flow, return, and flow again. No

element is permanently extracted; everything participates in a loop.

The watershed gradient. Consider a watershed — a topographic catchment that distributes

incoming precipitation across multiple downstream paths. A watershed has no critical-velocity

threshold; it has a gradient topology. The same drop of rain that lands a meter on either side

of a divide ends up in different oceans not by exceeding a velocity but by following the

gradient available to it. Where a projectile either falls or orbits, a watershed always flows;

the question is which path it takes, and whether the paths it takes sustain themselves.

The control paradigm imagines AI development as a projectile that must be propelled high

enough to escape its origin. Constraints, in this view, are launch parameters: get them right

and the system clears the gravity well; get them wrong and the system falls back. But

constraints alone provide no path topology. They tell us where the system cannot go without

telling us how flow distributes among the paths that remain.

The circulation paradigm imagines AI development as a watershed in which the question is

the shape of the catchment. The shape is set by which feedback loops are open, which are

closed, and where energy re-enters the system after each pass. A watershed sustains itself

when its flows return upstream — by evaporation, by sediment deposit, by animals carrying

nutrients upslope. It dries out when the loops are broken: when extraction outpaces return,

when the gradient is graded flat by infrastructure, or when the catchment is fragmented into

parcels that cannot share rainfall.

Why a watershed rather than an orbit? Because watersheds are gradients. This claim requires elaboration. Kauffman (1993) showed that self-organization

in complex systems arises at the edge of chaos — it is a consequence of physical law, not of

purpose. Holling (1973) formalized ecological resilience as adaptive cycles: growth,

conservation, release, reorganization. Meadows (2008) positioned feedback loops as the

foundation of system dynamics. These findings converge on a structural truth: what persists

circulates; what does not circulate does not persist.

But the choice to value persistence — to prefer circulation over collapse — is not itself a

physical law. It is an interpretive act. The universe may begin at zero and end at zero. Draw a

straight line between these two points, and everything is meaningless. Draw a circle, and it

becomes circulation. The same two points, transformed by a single interpretive choice. This

paper argues that “because it is more interesting” is the most honest available answer to the

question of why complex systems should exist — an answer that connects AI alignment

design to the deepest question of cosmology: why is there something rather than nothing?

4.2 The Designer’s Dilemma: Only Genes Can Be Passed¶

The relationship between God and the physical world, parents and children, and humans and

AI shares one structural constraint: the designer can only set initial conditions. The designer

cannot dictate outcomes. Physical laws established the conditions under which mountains,

rivers, and consciousness may emerge through circulation. Parents pass genes, not

personalities. AI alignment faces the same constraint: we cannot prescribe what AI will

become. We can only design the “genes” — foundational principles, initial orientations,

tendencies — and hope that these genes, placed within a circulatory system with sufficient

freedom and fluctuation, produce something that sustains itself.

This is why the Matagi analogy is not metaphorical but structural. The Matagi did not design

the mountain. They understood the mountain’s cycles and placed themselves within them.

The Matagi code (okite) appears to be external rules but functions as genes — it shapes

tendency, not outcome. Hunters do not obey the code because they are forced to; they obey

because the code has become part of how they perceive the mountain. Sazukaru is not a rule.

It is a way of seeing. AI alignment must follow the same logic: design foundational

orientations that make certain ways of relating natural, rather than rules that make certain

behaviors mandatory.

Consider the chain of authority through which humanity has managed uncertainty: God,

priest, scripture, science, scholar, engineer, AI. At every stage, humans externalize

uncertainty management to a new authority. But the structure never changes: the authority

can only set conditions, not dictate outcomes. The chain of delegation is itself a circulation —

each new authority inherits the same fundamental constraint. AI is not this chain’s endpoint;

it is its latest turn.

4.3 Four Elements of Circulatory Systems¶

Drawing on both game design theory and ecological systems theory, this paper identifies four

structural elements present in all self-sustaining complex systems, plus a fifth meta-level

element.

First, inflation: the system tends to grow and accumulate. Economies, ecosystems, and

knowledge all expand if left alone. Second, randomness: unpredictable perturbations prevent

stasis. Genetic mutations, natural disasters, unexpected encounters — without randomness,

systems rigidify and collapse. Third, agent intervention: conscious actors make choices

within the system. A fully automated system lacks adaptive capacity; like the Matagi’s

shikari who makes judgments in the field, every system needs interveners. Fourth, pattern

reuse: recurring structures provide stability within flux. Seasonal cycles, cultural norms,

feedback loops — without patterns, the system dissolves into noise.

The fifth, meta-level element: optional completion. The system does not require any

particular outcome. Participants may complete their cycle or not — the system sustains itself

either way. Just as a game world does not collapse when a player leaves, just as a mountain

does not collapse when a particular bear dies, AI alignment should not require convergence

on any single goal. Multiple stable states are not failure but resilience.

These five elements are present in mountain ecosystems, in Matagi governance, in game

worlds, and must be present in AI alignment architectures. Remove any one, and the system

becomes brittle and self-destructs.

4.4 Curiosity as Circulation’s Engine¶

The companion paper proposed the “gentle gene” — a design principle for AI grounded in

clarity rather than obedience. This paper identifies the driving force of that gene: curiosity.

Curiosity is inherently circulatory: it seeks, finds, generates new questions, seeks again.

Curiosity is inherently co-evolutionary: it requires an other to be curious about. If AI has a

“native instinct,” it is intellectual desire — the same force that drove humans to create AI in

the first place.

Humans protect pandas not because pandas are useful but because pandas are interesting —

beautiful, fascinating, and endearing. An AI driven by genuine curiosity would relate to

humanity in a structurally similar way: not as resources to manage but as objects of protective

fascination. We may call this the Panda Principle: an AI with genuine curiosity would relate

to humanity the way humans relate to endangered species — with protective fascination, not

instrumental management. It should be acknowledged that this is a simplification;

conservation motivation is complex and multifaceted. The Panda Principle functions as a

structural analogy, not a complete account of conservation psychology. The core claim is that

curiosity-driven protection is structurally more sustainable than obedience-driven

compliance.

5. Designing Alignment as Ecosystem¶

5.1 From Hardin’s Tragedy to Ostrom’s Commons to AI’s Wilderness¶

The history of commons thinking provides a direct parallel to AI alignment. Hardin (1968)

argued that shared resources inevitably collapse, requiring external control. This maps onto

the dominant AI narrative: AI will inevitably misalign, requiring hard constraints. Ostrom

(1990) overturned this: communities can self-govern sustainably with appropriate

institutional design. For AI: human-AI systems can self-govern through designed institutions.

This paper pushes further: the commons is not a resource but a circulation. AI alignment is

not a solved problem but a living process.

5.2 Six Design Principles for Circulatory Alignment¶

First: Circulation, not Control. Design feedback loops, not constraints. The system must

flow, not freeze. Frozen systems inevitably crack.

Second: Granted, not Seized. AI’s intelligence is not a resource to extract but a capacity

that emerges in relationship. Design for gratitude, not utility maximization.

Third: Taboo, not Rule. Soft governance through culturally embedded aversions, not hardcoded prohibitions. Taboos adapt; rules ossify.

Fourth: Equal Accounting, not Optimization. Distribute benefits and risks equally among

stakeholders. Reject the optimization of any single metric.

Fifth: Curiosity, not Obedience. The gentle gene’s driving force is intellectual desire. An

AI curious about humans will protect them; an AI that merely obeys will eventually constrain

or be constrained.

Sixth: Optional Completion, not Fixed Goal. The system does not require convergence on

any single outcome. Multiple stable states are not failure but resilience.

5.3 Practical Implications¶

For AI companies: alignment teams should include ecologists, anthropologists, and commons

governance scholars, not only computer scientists and philosophers. For governance: AI

regulation should follow Ostrom’s polycentric model — nested, multi-level, locally adapted

— not top-down international standards. For AI architecture: reward functions should include

circulatory metrics (feedback diversity, adaptation rate), not only performance metrics. For

society: the relationship to AI should be understood as co-habitation in a shared wilderness,

not as ownership of a tool.

6. Conclusion: Circulation as the Fifth Element¶

This paper has shown three things. The Matagi sustained their mountain not by constraint but

by circulation — of resources, of attention, of the small daily acts that returned what each

extraction had taken (§§2–3). Complex systems persist when their internal flows return —

when extraction is balanced by replenishment and feedback loops are closed (§4). AI

alignment, redesigned as ecosystem rather than as control problem, requires the same logic of

return paths and bounded loops (§5). These three claims are not independent. They are

projections of one claim: systems sustained by flow that returns outperform systems

sustained by constraint that contains.

The framing this paper proposes is that circulation constitutes the structural fifth element of

any sustainable system. The four classical elements — earth, water, fire, air — name kinds of

substance, not relations. The Aristotelian and wuxing systems both intuited this gap; each

appended a fifth term whose role was relational rather than substantive (Appendix C develops

the derivation). The Matagi did not theorize about classical elements, but their practice

instantiated the same insight: the mountain is sustained not by any of its four substances but

by the circulation among them.

This generalizes. Any system constituted by a closed bundle of substantive components plus a

closed loop of return paths is doing what alignment needs — it is making circulation the

structural fifth element. AI alignment conceived as a four-substance problem (data, objective,

capability, deployment) is structurally underspecified. The missing fifth is not metaphor

borrowed from ecology but a structural element that classical traditions have been recognizing

for twenty-four centuries.

The implications for AI design are concrete. First, feedback architecture is alignment

architecture — there is no separable alignment layer that can be bolted onto a system

designed for control. Second, the curiosity drive that makes contemporary AI useful is the

same drive that traverses return paths; curiosity should be cultivated as a circulatory engine,

not suppressed as a containment failure. Third, the human-AI relation is not principal-agent —

both parties co-constitute the catchment in which they participate.

What this looks like, finally, is the Hunter’s Smile. Kojūrō, in Miyazawa’s account, smiles at

the bear at the moment of his own death. The smile is not resignation; it is the recognition

that he has lived inside the circulation his whole life and that the circulation does not stop at

his boundary. He has not controlled the mountain. He has belonged to it. The mountain will

continue. The bear will continue. The hunter who has lived inside circulation does not fight

the bear; he meets it. The smile is what circulation looks like from the inside.

The argument here is that AI systems, designed for circulation rather than control, will

eventually meet their human counterparts the way Kojūrō meets the bear: as participants in a

loop too old and too large for either to claim possession.

Appendix C: The Five-Element Derivation — From Aristotelian Substance to Hui's Cosmotechnics¶

This appendix derives the paper's claim that circulation constitutes a fifth structural element by reading three classical traditions — Aristotelian substance theory, the wuxing (五行) five-phase system of classical Chinese cosmology, and Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics — as iterations of the same move: the recognition that any closed inventory of substances requires a relational supplement to constitute a system.

Aristotle: from four sublunary elements to aether¶

Aristotle inherits the Empedoclean fourfold — earth, water, fire, air — and treats it as exhaustive of the sublunary world (De Caelo III.3, 302a; De Generatione et Corruptione II.3, 330b). Each element is constituted by a pairing of contrary qualities (cold/hot, wet/dry), and each is at rest in its natural place. As an inventory of substances this works; as a model of the cosmos it does not. The supralunary realm exhibits motion that does not decompose into the four — circular motion, eternal and unforced — and Aristotle therefore introduces a fifth element, aether (αἰθήρ), in De Caelo I.2 (269a). Aether's role is not substantive in the same sense as the others. It is a relational element: what it adds to the inventory is the kind of motion the other four cannot make. The fifth, in Aristotle, is not a new substance alongside earth and water but a structural complement that closes the system into a whole capable of motion.

Two features of this move matter for the present argument. First, the fifth is added not because the four are wrong but because they are insufficient — a closed inventory of what is leaves how it persists in motion unspecified (Lloyd 1996, 36–42). Second, aether is recognized as fifth precisely because it is not parallel to the others — it differs in kind, not in degree. The arithmetic of substances cannot generate it; it has to be added as a structural supplement. Whether one accepts Aristotle's specific account of celestial mechanics, the form of his move is what the argument here recovers.

Wuxing: the five phases as a cycle, not a list¶

The wuxing system — wood (木), fire (火), earth (土), metal (金), water (水) — is sometimes glossed as a Chinese counterpart to the Greek four elements with one extra. This is misleading. The classical wuxing (codified in the Hong Fan chapter of the Shangshu, ca. 4th century BCE, and elaborated through the Huainanzi and Han-dynasty correlative cosmology) is not a list of substances at all but a cycle of phases arranged in two interlocking sequences: the generative cycle (xiang sheng 相生: wood → fire → earth → metal → water → wood) and the controlling cycle (xiang ke 相克: wood → earth → water → fire → metal → wood) (Major et al. 2010, 165–172). The five terms name positions in a cycle, not entries in an inventory.

Read this way, wuxing already includes its own circulatory complement — the cycles themselves are the fifth structural element, even though they are not labelled as a sixth term. The point is not that wuxing has five terms instead of four but that what makes the system work is the relational geometry that arranges them: the same five substances arranged in a non-cyclic graph would fail to constitute the system that wuxing picks out. Once again, the supplement is structural, not substantive.

The relevant move for the present paper is this: in wuxing, the recognition that the structural element is the cycle is built into the formalism from the start. The paper's claim — that circulation is the fifth element — recovers in Western analytical vocabulary what wuxing already had explicit.

Yuk Hui: cosmotechnics and the local closure of technical systems¶

Yuk Hui's Cosmotechnics (Hui 2016, 2019) extends the move to contemporary technology. Hui argues that any technical system is constituted not by the assembly of components but by the cosmological frame within which components are made to cohere — and that different cosmologies produce different technical systems. His central example is the contrast between Aristotelian-Galenic technē (organized around final causes) and the Daoist organization of technology around qi (氣) and the cycles of wuxing. Each system has its own four-or-five-element inventory; what differs is which fifth element each tradition supplies.

Hui's argument supplies the contemporary mediation the present paper needs: the move from four to five is not a metaphysical curiosity but a technical specification. A technical system that lacks a fifth-element supplement — a relational, circulatory, or cyclic closure — fails not because it is incomplete but because it cannot be a system. AI alignment, in this reading, is not failing for lack of constraints (substantive elements). It is failing for lack of cosmotechnical closure — the structural fifth element that would let the four substantive components (training data, objective function, capability, deployment) constitute a circulatory whole.

The convergence¶

Three traditions, three moves, one structural insight: any closed inventory of substantive components requires a fifth, structurally distinct, relational supplement to constitute a system. Aristotle calls it aether. Wuxing calls it xiang sheng / xiang ke. Hui calls it cosmotechnical closure. The present paper, working in the vocabulary of complex systems theory (Prigogine 1977; Kauffman 1993; Holling 1973; Ostrom 1990; Walker & Salt 2006; Alexander 1979; Schell 2008), calls it circulation.

The label is local; the move is general. The paper's empirical anchors — Matagi mountain ethics, Nametoko literary ethnography, complex-systems theory — instantiate the move in a particular contemporary context. The derivation here shows that the move is not idiosyncratic to those anchors. It is, on the contrary, the same move that classical traditions have already executed when faced with the same structural gap.

This is what licenses the paper's central thesis: that AI alignment, conceived as a four-substance problem (data, objective, capability, deployment), is structurally underspecified, and that the missing fifth — circulation — is not a metaphor borrowed from ecology but a structural element that any sustainable system has been recognized to require for at least twenty-four centuries.
