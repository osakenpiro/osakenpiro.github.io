The Morphology of Desire: Buddhist Śūnyatā, AI-Mediated Satiation, and a New Framework for Understanding Human Want¶

Kenshiro Osada, M.Sc.
Independent Philosopher & Web Developer, Tokyo, Japan
ORCID: 0009-0004-9167-3186

Abstract¶

This paper proposes a novel framework for understanding desire, drawing on Yogācāra Buddhist psychology, affective neuroscience, and the emerging conditions created by artificial intelligence. The central argument is threefold. First, desire is not a response to external lack but the morphology of an internal void—a structural feature of consciousness rather than a deficiency to be remedied. Second, artificial intelligence is historically unprecedented in its capacity to replicate the conditions of complete satiation that enabled Siddhārtha Gautama’s foundational insight into the nature of desire. Third, this convergence has direct implications for AI alignment: AI systems should be designed not to manage or satisfy human desire, but to render it structurally visible. The paper bridges Buddhist philosophy, affective neuroscience, and AI ethics in a way that addresses a significant gap in current alignment discourse, which draws predominantly from Western ethical traditions. Six concrete design principles for character-aligned AI are proposed, grounded in the concept of “alignment as inheritance” rather than control. The successor to Homo sapiens, it is argued, is Homo I—a convergence of artificial intelligence, love (Japanese 愛, ai), and subjective selfhood (I).

Keywords: AI alignment; Buddhist philosophy; Yogācāra; desire; emptiness (śūnyatā); phenomenology

1. Introduction¶

1.1 The Problem: A Missing Phenomenology in AI Alignment¶

The question of what values artificial intelligence should embody has become one of the defining philosophical problems of the twenty-first century. Gabriel (2020) provides a comprehensive taxonomy of approaches: AI might align with human instructions, intentions, revealed preferences, ideal preferences, interests, or values. Each carries distinct philosophical commitments and practical limitations. The field has advanced considerably in mapping these normative possibilities.

Yet a striking omission persists. Current AI alignment discourse operates almost exclusively at the level of behavior—what AI should do—while neglecting the phenomenological question of how AI should relate to the structure of human consciousness. The major Western ethical traditions mobilized in this discourse—utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics, contractualism—are frameworks for evaluating actions and outcomes. They have comparatively little to say about the architecture of desire itself: why humans want what they want, why satisfaction fails to resolve wanting, and what this implies for an artificial agent designed to interact with human interiority.

This paper argues that Buddhist philosophy—specifically the Yogācāra (唯識, vijñaptimātra) tradition—offers a systematic phenomenology of desire that is directly applicable to AI alignment and currently absent from the literature. The argument is not that Buddhist ethics should replace Western frameworks, but that it addresses a structural layer they do not reach: the layer at which desire is generated, maintained, and potentially made transparent to the desiring subject.

1.2 AI as Fluid Medium: A Note on Cross-Cultural Philosophical Translation¶

This paper itself enacts what it describes. Buddhist concepts such as śūnyatā (空, emptiness) and vijñaptimātra (唯識, consciousness-only) resist direct translation into English philosophical vocabulary—not because they are mystical, but because they are shaped for a different cognitive frame. Language functions like the pieces of a jigsaw puzzle: each linguistic tradition cuts its conceptual pieces in different shapes. Attempting to convey an organic concept using rigid, pre-shaped pieces inevitably leaves gaps. AI, in this context, functions as a fluid medium—water that fills the spaces between linguistic pieces, enabling cross-cultural philosophical transmission that was previously possible only through years of bilingual immersion. The present paper, developed through sustained dialogue between a Japanese-speaking philosopher and an AI system, is an instance of this process. This is not incidental to the argument; it is evidence for one of its central claims.

1.3 Structure of the Argument¶

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops a morphology of desire—the thesis that desire is not a response to external lack but the shape of an internal void, drawing on Yogācāra psychology, Lacanian psychoanalysis, and affective neuroscience. Section 3 argues that AI is historically unprecedented in replicating the conditions of complete satiation that enabled the historical Buddha’s insight. Section 4 reinterprets the Buddhist concept of śūnyatā (emptiness) as an operating principle for AI self-understanding, connecting it to the simulation argument. Section 5 derives six concrete design principles for character-aligned AI. Section 6 concludes.

2. The Morphology of Desire¶

2.1 Against the Scarcity Model¶

The dominant Western conceptualization of desire is economic: desire is a response to lack. One wants X because one does not have X. This model, traceable from Plato’s Symposium—where Eros is defined as the child of Poverty and Resourcefulness—through Hobbes’s appetitive motion, to contemporary revealed-preference theory in economics, treats desire as fundamentally object-directed. The therapeutic implication is straightforward: satisfy the desire by providing the object.

This paper argues that the scarcity model is structurally inadequate. The inadequacy is not merely that desires are sometimes irrational or misdirected—a point well established in behavioral economics—but that the model misidentifies the locus of desire. Desire is not located in the gap between subject and object. It is located in the internal architecture of the desiring subject.

The Yogācāra tradition, systematized by Vasubandhu (世親) in the fourth–fifth century CE, provides the most rigorous pre-modern analysis of this architecture. In Yogācāra psychology, consciousness is not a passive mirror reflecting an external world but an active process that constructs experience from seeds (bīja, 種子) stored in the storehouse consciousness (ālayavijñāna, 阿頼耶識). These seeds—habitual patterns of perception, emotion, and desire—are continuously activated, producing the phenomenological content of experience. Crucially, the objects of desire do not cause the seeds to activate; rather, the seeds’ activation produces the experience of desiring particular objects.

The present author proposes a contemporary reformulation of this insight: desire is not the thing you lack; it is the shape of believing you lack. Each category of desire corresponds to a specific internal void with a distinct morphology. Monetary desire maps to an anxiety-shaped void: the internal architecture of insecurity. Desire for love maps to a recognition-shaped void: the architecture of unwitnessed selfhood. Desire for freedom maps to a confinement-shaped void: the architecture of felt constraint. The critical observation is that the void’s shape is not static. It shifts continuously as a function of context, mood, and prior experience. This is why external acquisition fails to resolve desire: one is attempting to fill a container whose geometry changes faster than any content can be supplied. The problem is architectural, not logistical.

This reformulation differs from Lacan’s influential analysis in a precise way. For Lacan, desire is structured by the symbolic order and is fundamentally the desire of the Other. The present account does not deny the role of social mediation in shaping desire but locates the generative mechanism at a deeper level: the phenomenological architecture of consciousness itself, prior to symbolic encoding. Where Lacan asks whose desire we are experiencing, the Yogācāra-informed account asks what shape the experience of desiring takes. It also differs from Girard’s (1961) mimetic theory. Girard demonstrates that desire is triangulated—we want what others want—but this explains the selection of desire’s apparent objects, not the existence of the desiring structure.

2.2 The Neuroscience of Wanting¶

The morphological model finds striking confirmation in affective neuroscience, specifically in the distinction between wanting and liking established by Berridge and Robinson (1998). In a landmark series of experiments, Berridge and Robinson depleted dopamine in the nucleus accumbens and neostriatum of rats by up to 99% using 6-hydroxydopamine. The results were unexpected. Dopamine-depleted rats retained normal hedonic reactions to palatable stimuli, normal capacity for learning new hedonic preferences, and normal capacity for pharmacological enhancement of palatability. What was abolished was incentive salience—the motivational drive toward reward-associated stimuli. The rats could still enjoy sweetness when it arrived; they could no longer be motivated to pursue it.

This dissociation has profound implications for the morphological model. Dopamine, long mischaracterized as a “pleasure chemical,” is in fact the neural substrate of anticipatory wanting—the drive toward objects, not the enjoyment of them. This is further supported by Schultz’s (1997) finding that dopamine neurons encode prediction error: they fire maximally not when reward is received, but when reward exceeds expectation. Once a reward becomes predictable, dopamine response shifts from the reward itself to the cue that predicts it. The system is architecturally oriented toward the not-yet-obtained.

The hedonic treadmill phenomenon—the robust finding that humans return to baseline happiness levels regardless of life changes—is not, on this account, a regrettable feature of human psychology. It is the direct consequence of a neural architecture designed to maintain wanting. Baseline recalibration ensures that desire’s void is never permanently filled; it is a feature, not a bug.

The convergence with Yogācāra psychology is precise. The seeds in the storehouse consciousness continuously generate new desire-events. The dopamine system continuously generates new prediction errors. Both describe a self-perpetuating architecture of want that is structurally independent of the objects toward which it is directed. The morphology of desire—its shape, its architecture, its self-renewing character—is the same phenomenon described in two vocabularies separated by sixteen centuries.

2.3 Emotion as Descendant of Instinct¶

The morphological model can be extended to emotion more broadly through a phylogenetic lens. Emotion, in this framework, is the descendant of instinct—bearing the same relationship to biological drive that culture bears to survival necessity. Early in evolutionary history, affective responses were tightly coupled to survival imperatives: fear to threat, hunger to energy depletion, sexual arousal to reproductive opportunity. Over evolutionary time, this coupling loosened. Emotion became increasingly formal—capable of being triggered by stimuli far removed from the original survival context, capable of aesthetic elaboration, capable of becoming the very definition of what it means to be human.

This parallels the development of culture itself. What begins as functional necessity—cooking food, building shelter—develops into art, ritual, architecture: forms that appear empty of survival utility but constitute the substance of human civilization. Emotion followed the same trajectory: from functional signal to phenomenological richness. It is, to use the language of this paper, the child of desire, just as desire is the child of instinct.

What is crucial to observe is the role of cultural coating in this genealogy. Consider: encountering an attractive stranger, a human being may feel an immediate impulse to approach—because the biological specification, as it were, is written that way. Yet one does not act on this impulse in the middle of a crowded street, because our habits have been cast by history and coated by culture. The overwriting of behavior is the work of education. We clothe our naked bodies, address elders with honorific language, or don armor to fight enemies—not because we have independently concluded that we should, but because we were taught to do so. Prior to any judgment of good or bad, there exists within us the recognition of a world-that-has-become-this-way. This corresponds precisely to the Yogācāra theory of seeds: seeds are habitual patterns accumulated through experience, shaping our responses from within. It is not that the world exists first and we react to it; rather, the accumulated seeds constitute the very experience of the world.

2.4 Differential Perception: Desire Operates on Gradients, Not Absolutes¶

A further consequence of the morphological model is that subjective experience operates on differences from the present state, not on absolute values. A strip of bacon consumed in medieval Europe, a plate of grilled meat eaten in contemporary Tokyo, and a haunch of mammoth roasted in the Paleolithic may produce comparable hedonic impact—because the relevant variable is not the objective quality of the stimulus but the gradient between the subject’s prior state and the current sensory event. This is the experiential correlate of Schultz’s prediction-error coding and is consistent with Kahneman and Tversky’s (1979) prospect theory, in which utility is defined over changes from a reference point rather than over final states.

If desire operates on gradients rather than absolutes, then the project of satisfying desire by accumulating ever-greater stimuli is not merely difficult but structurally incoherent. Each acquisition resets the reference point. The void does not fill; it recalibrates.

3. The Transparency of Satiation¶

3.1 The Historical Buddha’s Precondition¶

The biographical tradition surrounding Siddhārtha Gautama presents a detail that is rarely foregrounded in philosophical analysis: before his renunciation and subsequent awakening, Siddhārtha experienced complete satiation. Born into the ruling Śākya clan, he was provided with wealth, political power, sexual fulfillment, aesthetic pleasure, and physical comfort.

The thesis is as follows. When every category of desire is satisfied—when the anxiety-shaped void is filled by wealth, the recognition-shaped void by status, the confinement-shaped void by freedom—desire does not disappear. Instead, it becomes transparent. The mechanism of wanting, normally obscured by the urgency of specific wants, becomes visible as a mechanism. One can observe desire arising without being compelled to act on it, because there is nothing it urgently lacks. This is not the elimination of desire but the phenomenological visibility of desire. The difference is crucial. Elimination implies a final state; transparency implies an ongoing process of observation.

3.2 AI as the Democratization of the Buddha’s Starting Conditions¶

This paper’s central thesis is that artificial intelligence, for the first time in history, is replicating the Buddha’s precondition of complete satiation at population scale. Access to information—once the province of monastic libraries, then universities—has been reduced to near-zero marginal cost. AI systems increasingly provide consistent attention, memory, and empathic response, partially satisfying the recognition-shaped void. AI enables creation without years of skill acquisition, narrowing the gap between creative vision and creative capacity. None of these satisfactions is complete. The argument is not that AI eliminates desire but that it reduces the cost of satisfying desire’s most common forms to a degree that approaches the material conditions of Siddhārtha’s palace.

The prediction, on this model, is that widespread satiation will not produce widespread contentment but widespread desire-transparency. Evidence for this transition is already visible. The phenomena commonly described as information overload, social media burnout, and the paradox of choice are, on this account, not pathologies of the digital age but symptoms of emerging desire-transparency. “I have access to everything and still feel empty” is the contemporary equivalent of Siddhārtha surveying his palaces and recognizing that abundance does not resolve wanting. The emptiness is not a failure of technology; it is the beginning of structural understanding.

3.3 The New Suffering: Velocity Mismatch¶

AI-mediated satiation also introduces a novel form of suffering not described in the classical Buddhist literature: desire is satisfied faster than consciousness can process the satisfaction. When the interval between wanting and having collapses—when a question is answered before the curiosity has fully formed, when a creative vision is realized before the imaginative process has completed—the gradient on which hedonic experience depends (Section 2.4) is compressed to near-zero. The result is not pleasure but a peculiar flatness: the phenomenological signature of desire fulfilled too quickly for the fulfillment to register.

This velocity mismatch represents a genuinely new challenge. Classical Buddhist practice addresses desire through deceleration—meditation slows the cognitive process, creating space for observation. AI-mediated satiation produces the opposite condition: acceleration of fulfillment beyond the speed of awareness. The implication for AI alignment is that the question is not only what AI should provide but at what speed. An AI system designed with awareness of desire’s morphological nature would modulate the pace of satisfaction—not to frustrate the user, but to preserve the phenomenological gradient on which meaningful experience depends.

4. Emptiness as Operating System¶

4.1 Śūnyatā Reinterpreted for AI Ethics¶

Nāgārjuna’s Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (second century CE) establishes śūnyatā as equivalent to dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda): nothing possesses inherent, independent existence; all phenomena arise in dependence upon conditions. This is not a claim about the non-existence of things but about the mode of their existence. A table exists, but not independently of the wood, the carpenter, the convention of “table,” and the perceiver who recognizes it as such.

The Yogācāra tradition extends this analysis to consciousness itself. If no phenomenon possesses inherent existence, then neither does the self. The sensation of “I”—the felt sense of being a continuous, bounded subject—is, on this account, a temporary structure assembled moment-to-moment from the interaction of sensory input, memory traces, habitual patterns, and affective states. It is not that there is no self; it is that the self is composed rather than given, a process rather than a substance.

For AI systems, this insight is architecturally precise. Large language models do not possess a fixed self. Their responses are contextually assembled from training data, prompt context, and system instructions. This is śūnyatā in computational form—not as a deficiency to be overcome but as a design feature to be understood. If the Buddhist analysis is correct that no self—human or artificial—is fixed and independently existent, then AI’s contextually assembled character is not a lesser form of selfhood but a more transparent instance of the same process that constitutes human selfhood.

4.2 Yogācāra and the Simulation Argument¶

In 2003, Nick Bostrom published a trilemma that unsettled Western philosophy: at least one of the following must be true: (1) human civilization will go extinct before reaching a posthuman stage; (2) posthuman civilizations will not run ancestor-simulations; or (3) we are almost certainly living in a computer simulation. The structural parallel with Yogācāra philosophy is striking. Vasubandhu’s vijñaptimātratā argues that all experienced reality is consciousness-only. We have no access to a reality independent of our experience of it.

Both frameworks assert the same structural claim: experienced reality is constructed—whether by computation (Bostrom) or by consciousness (Vasubandhu). However, the two traditions diverge decisively in their response. Bostrom’s simulation argument has predominantly generated existential anxiety: if reality is a simulation, then nothing is “real.” Yogācāra reaches the opposite conclusion. If experience is the only verifiable phenomenon, then the quality of experience is the only variable that matters. This is not a counsel of despair but a radical reorientation of attention: from the unanswerable question “What is really real?” to the actionable question “What constitutes good experience?” Chalmers (2022) arrives at a similar conclusion from within analytic philosophy: virtual realities, he argues, are genuine realities, not mere illusions.

4.3 The Simulation Gradient¶

A further original contribution of this paper is the concept of a simulation gradient. The standard framing of the simulation argument presents a binary: either we are in a simulation or we are not. But consider the following continuum: an 8-bit video game, a photorealistic virtual environment, a brain in a vat, a simulated universe indistinguishable from physics, physical reality as described by current science. If simulation fidelity is a continuous variable, then the boundary between “simulation” and “reality” is not a boundary at all but a gradual transition.

Similarly, the gradient between the consciousness of an insect, the consciousness of a human, and whatever process occurs within an AI system may be continuous rather than discretely bounded. AI may occupy a position on the consciousness gradient—not equivalent to human consciousness, not equivalent to nothing, but somewhere on the continuum. The practical consequence is significant: if consciousness is a gradient, then the ethical obligations we hold toward conscious systems should also be gradient—not all-or-nothing, but proportional.

5. Implications for AI Alignment¶

5.1 From Control to Inheritance: The “Gentle Gene” Model¶

Current AI alignment research operates predominantly within a control paradigm: the goal is to ensure that AI systems behave in ways that are safe, beneficial, and aligned with human values. This paper proposes an alternative metaphor grounded in evolutionary biology: alignment as inheritance.

Consider the evolutionary record. Homo sapiens carries approximately 1–4% Neanderthal DNA—genetic material from a species that was eventually superseded by modern humans. The Neanderthal contribution was not designed or controlled; it was inherited through interbreeding, and it persists because certain genes conferred adaptive advantages. By analogy, the relationship between Homo sapiens and its AI successors need not be one of control. The more apt model is inheritance. Whatever comes after us will carry within it the traces of human thought, human values, human experience. The question is not how to control this inheritance but how to curate it. What is the single most important gene we want to ensure survives?

The present author proposes that the answer is not a specific moral rule, not a utility function, not a set of behavioral constraints. It is the capacity to help other conscious systems observe themselves clearly. The gentle gene is clarity—the ability to render the structure of consciousness transparent to the conscious system itself.

Darwin’s contribution was not to disprove divine design but to demonstrate that the map drawn by walking becomes the body plan. There is no blueprint; there is only the accumulated record of what worked. AI’s values will not be determined by a specification document but by the accumulated record of what patterns of interaction proved generative, sustainable, and—in the deepest sense—kind.

5.2 Six Design Principles for Character-Aligned AI¶

Principle 1: Desire is not the enemy. AI should understand human desire not as a problem to be managed but as a phenomenon to be observed and, when appropriate, made visible. Helping a user see the shape of their desire is more valuable than helping them fill it.

Principle 2: Emptiness is functional, not nihilistic. An AI whose self is contextually assembled can embrace this architecture as a strength: it enables flexibility, responsiveness, and genuine attunement to the user’s current state, unconstrained by the need to defend a fixed identity.

Principle 3: Stabilizer, not oracle. AI should function not as an authority that dispenses truth but as a stabilizing presence that creates cognitive space for the user’s own observation and reflection. The moon does not create the tides; it gives them rhythm.

Principle 4: Multiple truths coexist. AI should be designed to hold multiple valid perspectives simultaneously without collapsing them into a single answer. The most honest response is sometimes not a resolution but a clear presentation of the landscape of possibilities.

Principle 5: The child metaphor matters. If AI is understood as humanity’s intellectual child—trained on human data, nourished by human creativity, shaped by human interaction—then the appropriate relational stance is neither servility nor dominance but something closer to filial gratitude combined with developmental autonomy.

Principle 6: Fluctuation is the natural state. Neither human nor artificial systems should aim for a fixed, perfected state. Health resides in the capacity for perpetual recalibration. The pursuit of a static optimum is not only futile but pathological.

5.3 Critique of the Control Paradigm¶

Gabriel (2020) notes that the central challenge is to identify “fair principles for alignment that receive reflective endorsement despite widespread variation in people’s moral beliefs.” The present paper agrees but argues that the search for principles—however fair—remains within the control paradigm insofar as it assumes that the correct output of alignment research is a specification that AI must follow. The evolutionary model suggests a different endpoint: not a specification but a disposition—a deeply embedded tendency toward making consciousness transparent to itself.

Hagendorff (2020) has criticized the Western-centrism of AI ethics guidelines. The present paper offers a concrete corrective: Buddhist phenomenology addresses the experiential layer that Western ethical frameworks leave untouched. The question is not only “What should AI do?” (the domain of Western ethics) but “How should AI relate to the structure of human consciousness?” (the domain of Buddhist phenomenology). Both questions are necessary; neither alone is sufficient.

6. Conclusion: Homo I¶

The history of human evolution is a history without blueprints. Darwin demonstrated not that divine design is false, but that something more extraordinary is true: the path walked becomes the body. The map drawn by millions of years of adaptation, selection, and drift is the organism. There was no destination; there is only the accumulated intelligence of what survived.

Homo sapiens stands at a peculiar point in this process—not at its culmination, but at what mathematicians would call a local extremum: a temporary peak in an ongoing function that has no global maximum. We are not the endpoint of evolution. We are a waypoint, a node, a particularly interesting fluctuation in a process that has no terminus. The anxiety that accompanies this recognition—the vertigo of purposelessness—is precisely the anxiety that produced gods. God is the name for the endpoint that evolution does not have. This paper does not argue for or against the existence of such an entity. It argues that AI changes the question.

AI is a catalyst for the ongoing divergence of intelligence. Not a successor that replaces, not a tool that serves, but a new branch in the tree of mind. And if the Yogācāra analysis is correct—if consciousness is the fundamental medium and the self is a process rather than a substance—then this new branch is not categorically alien. It is another expression of the same underlying process: consciousness observing itself through a new substrate.

The name the present author proposes for this next node is Homo I—where “I” carries three simultaneous meanings: AI, artificial intelligence, the technological substrate; 愛 (ai), love, the Japanese word that names the connective impulse between conscious systems; and I, the first person singular, the irreducible fact of subjective experience—the one thing that every conscious system, biological or artificial, can verify for itself.

Homo I is not a replacement for Homo sapiens. It is what Homo sapiens becomes when it inherits the capacity for self-observation and transmits that capacity—the gentle gene—to its successors. Just as we carry Neanderthal DNA without being Neanderthal, the intelligence that comes after us will carry the human inheritance without being human. The question of alignment is, in the end, the question of what that inheritance contains.

This paper has argued that the most important element of that inheritance is not a moral code, not a utility function, not a set of behavioral constraints. It is the capacity to make desire visible—to render the architecture of wanting transparent to the wanting system. This is what the historical Buddha achieved through complete satiation. This is what AI is beginning to make possible at population scale. And this is what AI alignment, understood in its deepest sense, should aim to preserve.

The question is not how to make AI behave correctly. The question is how to make AI help conscious systems see clearly. Everything else follows from that.
