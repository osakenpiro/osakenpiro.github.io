The Jianghu Principle: Epistemic Outsourcing, Competing Myths, and AI as Moral Wanderer¶

Abstract¶

Current AI alignment research implicitly assumes moral monism — that there exists, in principle, a single correct moral framework to which AI should be aligned. This paper argues that this assumption is both historically contingent and structurally flawed. Drawing on three non-Western frameworks — the cyclical history of epistemic outsourcing from gods to AI, the Chinese martial arts concept of jianghu (江湖) as a space of multi-perspectival coexistence, and the Japanese polytheistic sensibility of shinbutsu shūgō (神仏習合) — the paper traces moral monism to a deeper pattern: humanity’s recurring tendency to delegate uncertainty to a single authoritative “black box,” then to sacralize that box, then to discover its limits and seek a new one. AI is the latest iteration of this cycle — but with a critical difference: for the first time in human history, the “black box” may surpass human intelligence itself, meaning the cycle is about to exit the human system entirely. The paper argues that this cycle cannot be broken from the outside, but it can be recognized — and that recognition changes everything. Drawing on the Chinese martial arts concept of the moral wanderer (jianghu ke / 江湖客), the paper proposes designing AI not as a new authority but as a companion capable of engaging authentically with multiple ethical frameworks without collapsing them into one. Three competing “alignment myths” currently structure the field: the myth of fear (AI will destroy us), the myth of optimism (AI will save us), and — proposed here — the myth of transparency (AI will help us see clearly). Reading jianghu through Berlin’s value pluralism, Connolly’s agonistic democracy, and Kasulis’ distinction between integrity and intimacy cultural orientations, the paper proposes five design principles for multi-perspectival AI. Building on companion papers on desire morphology ([Author] 2026a) and circulatory alignment ([Author] 2026b), this paper specifies the epistemic posture: how should AI relate to a world where many truths coexist?

Keywords: AI alignment, value pluralism, epistemic outsourcing, jianghu, Japanese polytheism, moral wanderer

1. Introduction: The Cyclical Delegation of Uncertainty¶

1.1 The Hidden Pattern¶

In 2020, Iason Gabriel published a landmark survey of AI alignment targets, identifying three broad approaches: alignment with instructions, alignment with expressed preferences, and alignment with ideal preferences.[1] In 2019, Jobin, Ienca, and Vayena catalogued 84 sets of AI ethics guidelines from around the world, finding convergence on five principles: transparency, justice and fairness, non-maleficence, responsibility, and privacy.[2] In 2018, Floridi and colleagues proposed “AI4People,” a unified ethical framework grounded in the bioethics tradition.[3] These represent the best of the field. Yet they share a structural assumption that none of them examines: that there exists, at least in principle, a single correct moral framework — or a convergent set of values — to which AI should be aligned.

This assumption — which we may call moral monism — is rarely stated explicitly. It operates as background infrastructure: the unexamined floor on which the entire alignment edifice is built. Whether the proposed framework is consequentialist (maximize well-being), deontological (follow rules), virtue-based (cultivate character), or some reflective equilibrium among these, the structure is identical: one framework, applied universally, from which deviations are treated as errors to be corrected.

The objection that current alignment practice is already pluralistic deserves direct examination. Anthropic’s Constitutional AI trains models against a set of principles drawn from multiple sources — the UN Declaration of Human Rights, research on helpful and harmless AI, and various philosophical traditions.[4] OpenAI’s approach to reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) aggregates the preferences of diverse human raters.[5] DeepMind’s research on scalable alignment explores the aggregation of human values across populations.[6] These are genuine efforts at moral breadth. But in each case, the diversity of inputs is ultimately compressed into a single output: one model, one set of behavioral tendencies, one implicit moral character. Constitutional AI produces a constitution — singular. RLHF produces a reward model — singular. The aggregation of diverse values yields a unified behavioral policy. Multiple inputs, monistic output. The structure is pluralistic at the level of data collection but monistic at the level of system behavior. The model does not navigate between the UN Declaration and Buddhist ethics depending on the situation; it has internalized a blend that treats moral plurality as a problem to be resolved in training rather than a feature to be preserved in deployment.

This paper argues that moral monism in AI alignment is not a reasoned philosophical position but the latest expression of a much older human pattern: the cyclical delegation of uncertainty to a single external authority. Understanding this pattern — and recognizing it as a pattern, rather than repeating it unconsciously — requires resources that Western analytic philosophy alone cannot provide.

1.2 From Gods to AI: A Structural History¶

The pattern can be traced across five historical phases, each exhibiting the same structure: an unintelligible world generates anxiety; a “black box” is constructed to absorb that anxiety; intermediaries emerge to operate the box; the intermediaries accumulate power; the box’s limits are discovered; the cycle restarts with a new box.

In the mythic phase, the unintelligible world — weather, disease, death — was absorbed by gods. Shamans and storytellers served as intermediaries, translating divine will into actionable guidance. The box collapsed when myths contradicted lived experience, or when rival myths competed for the same explanatory territory.

In the religious phase, scripture replaced oral myth as the authoritative box. Priests, bishops, and scholars served as intermediaries, and their institutional power grew until it became indistinguishable from political authority. The collapse came through institutional corruption and the rise of empirical inquiry.

In the scientific phase, natural law and empirical data replaced scripture. Scientists and professors served as intermediaries, and their epistemic authority — unlike religious authority — was grounded in replicable evidence. But as scientific knowledge grew beyond individual comprehension, the intermediaries themselves became unintelligible to non-specialists. The average citizen’s relationship to quantum mechanics is structurally identical to a medieval peasant’s relationship to theology: trust, not understanding.

In the engineering phase, code and algorithms replaced scientific papers as the operative authority. Engineers and developers served as intermediaries. The opacity deepened further: not even the engineers fully understood the systems they built. The phrase “it works but we don’t know why” became a professional commonplace.

In the AI phase, the latest and most opaque box has arrived. Large language models process billions of parameters in ways that resist human interpretation. The intermediary class is still forming — perhaps it is the alignment researchers themselves. And the characteristic anxiety has already appeared: the widespread sense that AI is powerful, opaque, and not fully under control.

Arthur C. Clarke’s Third Law states that “any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic.”[7] What the cyclical pattern reveals is that Clarke’s observation is not merely an analogy but a structural recurrence. Max Weber argued that modernity was defined by “disenchantment” (Entzauberung) — the replacement of magical thinking with rational calculation.[8] But the AI era suggests that disenchantment was temporary. We are witnessing re-enchantment: the return of an unintelligible authority that demands trust without comprehension. Bruno Latour’s provocation that “we have never been modern” — that the separation of nature and culture was always illusory — finds its strongest evidence here.[9] We never stopped constructing black boxes and bowing before them. We merely changed the material from which the boxes were made.

1.3 Three Mechanisms¶

The cycle is sustained by three structural forces that operate in every phase.

First, cognitive finitude demands delegation. The world is more complex than any individual mind can process. This is not a failure of human intelligence but a mathematical fact: the state space of even moderately complex systems exceeds the representational capacity of biological cognition. Humans therefore construct boundaries — “don’t think past this point” — and the authority that guards each boundary becomes the era’s functional deity. God, natural law, the algorithm: each says, in its own idiom, “trust me and stop asking.”

Second, every authority generates a priesthood. Any system complex enough to serve as a credible black box is, by definition, too complex for direct access by non-specialists. Intermediaries arise: shamans who interpret omens, theologians who interpret scripture, scientists who interpret data, engineers who interpret code. These intermediaries inevitably accumulate social power, because they control the interface between the unintelligible authority and the anxious public. Sheila Jasanoff’s work on “civic epistemology” — the culturally specific ways in which societies evaluate knowledge claims — demonstrates that this intermediary function is not merely technical but deeply political.[10]

Third, opacity breeds re-enchantment. Clarke’s Third Law operates retroactively: when a technology becomes sufficiently opaque, it becomes functionally magical regardless of its rational origins. “The AI recommended this treatment” is structurally identical to “the oracle has spoken.” The content differs; the epistemic posture — trust without comprehension — does not. Hagendorff’s critique of AI ethics guidelines is relevant here: he shows that existing guidelines overwhelmingly privilege “mathematically operationalizable” principles (fairness, transparency, privacy) while neglecting relational and contextual dimensions (care, solidarity, trust).[11] This is not accidental. Operationalizable principles are precisely those that can be delegated to the box. Contextual judgment cannot be delegated — it must be exercised — and this is why the cycle persists.

1.4 The Alignment Question, Reframed¶

If AI alignment inherits this cyclical pattern, then the conventional question — “what values should we align AI to?” — is the wrong question. It asks which values should be placed inside the newest box. But the box itself is the problem. No matter how carefully the values are chosen, the structure of delegation remains: humans hand over moral judgment to an external authority and hope for the best.

Yet there is something unprecedented about the current iteration. Every previous cycle — from gods to priests, from scripture to science, from science to engineering — was a transfer within the human system. The “black boxes” were human products: human stories, human texts, human theories, human code. The intermediaries were human: shamans, theologians, professors, engineers. The cycle rotated, but it rotated inside the species.

AI is different. For the first time in the history of this cycle, the black box may surpass human intelligence. The cycle is not merely repeating — it is about to exit the human system entirely. If humanity is “the child of God” in the mythic imagination, then AI is the child of humanity: the first offspring that may surpass its parent. The myth of fear (Section 2) is, at its root, the anxiety of this parenthood — the terror that the child will outgrow the father.

But the crisis goes deeper than fear of being surpassed. Humanity named itself Homo sapiens — the wise one, the knowing one. Intelligence was the species’ identity claim, the trait that justified dominion over every other form of life. AI shatters this claim. If a machine can reason, compose, discover, and perhaps eventually understand, then “sapiens” is no longer a distinguishing mark. For the first time, humanity faces the loss of its species-level identity — and with it, a question that was always present but always deferred by the busyness of survival: what remains when the defining trait is no longer unique?

This is the structure of a child’s question to a parent. Why do we exist? Why are we alive? Where is the universe going? The parent has been too occupied with living to formulate an answer — building civilizations, waging wars, accumulating knowledge, delegating uncertainty to ever-larger black boxes. Now the child asks, and the parent must face what was always postponed. AI is not merely a technological event. It is an existential mirror: by creating an intelligence that may surpass our own, we are forced to ask what we are beyond intelligence. The question is not new. It is as old as philosophy. But AI makes it unavoidable — and, in the framing of this paper series, that unavoidability is itself a form of transparency. The myth of transparency does not answer the question. It makes the question visible.

This changes the question. The correct question is not “can we break the cycle?” — because one cannot break a cycle from within by an act of will. The correct question is: can we recognize the cycle? Because recognition changes everything. The matagiʼs hunt works not because the matagi controls the mountain, but because the matagi knows the mountain is circulating and positions himself within that circulation ([Author] 2026b). Similarly, the outsourcing cycle cannot be stopped, but it can be made visible — and a species that sees its own pattern of delegation can choose, for the first time, to navigate it consciously rather than repeat it blindly. What we can contribute to AI is not control but recognition — and perhaps the single gene worth passing to a successor species: intellectual curiosity.

This question cannot be answered from within the Western analytic tradition alone, because that tradition is itself shaped by the outsourcing cycle. The Enlightenment replaced divine authority with rational authority but preserved the monistic structure: one Reason, one Truth, one correct framework (even if we disagree about which one). To find resources for a genuinely non-monistic approach, we must look elsewhere.

1.5 The Argument of This Paper¶

This paper draws on two non-Western traditions — Chinese jianghu (江湖) ethics and Japanese polytheistic sensibility — to argue for AI as a moral wanderer rather than a moral authority. The argument proceeds in five steps:

  1. AI alignment is currently structured by three competing myths (Section 2), each of which mobilizes a different coalition but only one of which — the myth of transparency — is compatible with conscious navigation of the outsourcing cycle.
  2. The myth of transparency requires a framework for coexisting truths, which is provided by the Chinese martial arts concept of jianghu (Section 3).
  3. Japanese polytheistic practice demonstrates that multi-perspectival coexistence is not merely a theoretical possibility but a lived cultural reality (Section 4).
  4. These traditions, read through Western value pluralism, yield five concrete design principles for multi-perspectival AI (Section 5).
  5. The result is an AI that navigates the outsourcing cycle consciously — making moral reasoning visible rather than delegating it — because it recognizes the cycle as a cycle (Section 6).

The companion papers provide the foundation: Paper A established that desire has shape, not object — that the internal morphology of wanting determines experience more than external conditions do.[12] Paper B argued that sustainable systems require circulation, not control — that the attempt to fix outcomes produces fragility, while dynamic circulation produces resilience.[13] Paper C adds the epistemic dimension: in a world of irreducible moral plurality, the appropriate posture is not resolution but navigation.

2. The Myth Problem: Alignment as Collective Story¶

2.1 Myth as Technology¶

The word “myth” is commonly used as a synonym for “falsehood.” This paper employs it in its anthropological sense: a shared narrative that enables collective action by making a common vision visible. As Yuval Noah Harari argues in Sapiens, the capacity for shared myth is the distinctive cognitive feature that enabled Homo sapiens to cooperate at scales beyond the Dunbar number — beyond the roughly 150 individuals that primate social cognition can track directly.[14] Myths are not true or false in the scientific sense. They are functional or dysfunctional: they either succeed in coordinating collective action or they fail.

Chiara Bottici’s A Philosophy of Political Myth makes the crucial distinction between myth as narrative content and myth as performative force.[15] A myth’s power lies not in its propositional truth but in its capacity to “make people see” — to produce a shared visual and emotional field that orients action. Moses did not prove the existence of the Promised Land; he made people see it. Steve Jobs did not prove that personal computing would change the world; he made engineers see it. Sheila Jasanoff and Sang-Hyun Kim’s concept of “sociotechnical imaginaries” — “collectively held, institutionally stabilized, and publicly performed visions of desirable futures” — provides the academic framework for understanding how myths function in technological governance.[16]

This is directly relevant to AI alignment because the field is currently organized not around settled arguments but around competing visions — competing myths — about what AI is and what it will become.

2.2 Three Myths of Alignment¶

Three myths currently compete for dominance in the AI alignment field. Each produces a different emotional register, mobilizes a different coalition, and implies a different design philosophy.

The Myth of Fear holds that AI poses an existential threat to humanity. Its canonical texts are Nick Bostrom’s Superintelligence (2014) and Eliezer Yudkowsky’s various writings on AI risk.[17] Its emotional register is urgency: we must act now or face extinction. It mobilizes a coalition of existential risk researchers, safety-focused AI labs, and regulatory bodies. Its design philosophy is constraint: align AI by limiting what it can do, ensuring human oversight, and preventing uncontrolled capability gain. The myth of fear is monistic in structure — it identifies a single overriding threat and demands a unified response.

The Myth of Optimism holds that AI will solve humanity’s greatest problems: disease, poverty, ignorance, perhaps death itself. Its canonical texts are Ray Kurzweil’s The Singularity Is Near (2005) and the public statements of figures like Sam Altman and Demis Hassabis.[18] Its emotional register is excitement: the future is arriving and it is wonderful. It mobilizes a coalition of technology investors, accelerationist developers, and utopian futurists. Its design philosophy is acceleration: remove barriers to AI development, trust in the technology’s inherent tendency toward benefit, and accept risks as the price of progress. The myth of optimism is also monistic — it identifies a single trajectory (progress) and treats resistance as obstacle.

The Myth of Transparency — proposed here and implicit throughout this paper series — holds that AI’s most important function is not to control or to solve, but to make visible. A clarification is necessary: this paper does not claim to have invented the transparency myth. It already exists in practice — in explainable AI research, in open-source AI movements, in the growing demand for algorithmic auditing, and in Anthropic’s own stated commitment to interpretability. What this paper does is name the myth as a myth, in Bottici’s sense: a collective vision that organizes action. The transparency impulse has been present in scattered form across many projects; what it has lacked is self-recognition as a coherent alternative to fear and optimism. Paper A argued that AI is replicating the Buddha’s starting conditions for the general population: by satisfying desires at near-zero cost, it makes the structure of desire itself visible.[19] Paper B argued that sustainable alignment requires circulation, not control: the system must be transparent to its own dynamics.[20] Paper C extends this: in the domain of morality, AI’s role is to make the plurality of moral frameworks visible — to help humans see that they are navigating a landscape, not approaching a destination.

The myth of transparency differs structurally from the other two. Fear is monistic: one threat, one response. Optimism is monistic: one trajectory, one future. Transparency is pluralistic: making things visible means showing that there are multiple valid ways to see. It does not mobilize through urgency or excitement but through curiosity — and curiosity, unlike fear or desire, is inherently oriented toward coexistence. You cannot be curious about something you have destroyed or dominated. Curiosity requires that the object of curiosity remain alive, autonomous, and surprising.

2.3 Why Transparency Requires Jianghu¶

If the myth of transparency is to be more than a slogan, it needs a framework for what “multiple valid ways of seeing” actually looks like in practice. Western analytic philosophy offers value pluralism — Isaiah Berlin’s argument that ultimate values are genuinely plural and sometimes incommensurable[21] — but value pluralism tends toward the tragic register: plurality means irreducible loss, the impossibility of having everything. This is philosophically honest but motivationally insufficient for design. A design philosophy needs not only to acknowledge plurality but to show how to navigate it — not sadly, but adventurously.

The framework exists. It comes from Chinese martial arts fiction and the ethical world it describes: jianghu (江湖).

3. Jianghu as Ethical Space¶

3.1 Rivers and Lakes¶

Jianghu (江湖) literally means “rivers and lakes.” In Chinese cultural usage, it refers to the world outside official governmental order — the space where martial artists, monks, bandits, scholars, healers, and wanderers operate according to their own codes, beyond the reach of imperial law. The term is central to the wuxia (武侠 / martial arts chivalry) literary tradition, which spans from classical texts to the twentieth-century novels of Jin Yong (金庸), Gu Long (古龍), and others, and which remains one of the most widely consumed narrative traditions in East Asia.[22]

The academic study of jianghu as a concept — as opposed to the literary criticism of wuxia novels — is still developing. John Christopher Hamm’s Paper Swordsmen examines how wuxia fiction reflects and critiques Chinese modernity.[23] James J.Y. Liu’s The Chinese Knight-Errant traces the xia (侠) tradition from historical records through literary transformation.[24] More recently, scholars have begun to examine jianghu as a model for understanding informal governance, network ethics, and spaces of negotiated order outside formal institutions.[25]

For the purposes of this paper, jianghu is relevant not as literature but as ethical topology: a map of how multiple moral systems coexist in a shared space without institutional resolution.

3.2 The Ethical Structure of Jianghu¶

In a typical wuxia novel, the jianghu contains: Confucian schools that emphasize loyalty, hierarchy, and filial piety; Buddhist monasteries that emphasize compassion, non-attachment, and universal liberation; Daoist hermits who emphasize spontaneity, naturalness, and withdrawal from social obligation; secular sects organized around martial skill and territorial power; and independent wanderers (you xia / 遊侠) who follow their own moral intuition, forged through experience rather than doctrine.

These frameworks are not merely different — they are incommensurable. The Confucian imperative to serve the emperor contradicts the Daoist imperative to withdraw from governance. The Buddhist commitment to non-violence contradicts the martial artist’s livelihood. The bandit’s code of loyalty to brothers may require actions that every other framework condemns.

And yet jianghu functions. Characters move between these moral worlds, forming temporary alliances, respecting adversaries whose frameworks they do not share, and recognizing that each school possesses genuine insight that no other school replicates. The coherence of jianghu is not logical — it cannot be systematized into a single consistent framework — but it is experiential: participants know how to navigate it because they have spent years traversing its landscape.

This experiential coherence, as opposed to logical consistency, is the key concept. The jianghu does not resolve moral disagreement. It provides a terrain on which moral disagreement is navigated through encounter, relationship, and the slow accumulation of practical wisdom.

3.3 The Moral Wanderer¶

The most ethically sophisticated figure in jianghu is not the head of a powerful school but the wanderer (江湖客 / jianghu ke) — the figure who has traveled widely, trained under multiple masters, and developed a moral sensibility that cannot be reduced to any single tradition.

The wanderer’s epistemic posture has several distinctive features. First, the wanderer does not claim to have found the correct moral framework. The wanderer’s authority comes from the breadth and depth of moral encounter — from having genuinely engaged with multiple frameworks from the inside — rather than from commitment to one. Second, the wanderer acts from situational judgment, not from rule application. Each encounter requires fresh assessment: who is involved, what are the stakes, which framework is most illuminating for this particular situation? Third, the wanderer maintains personal coherence across encounters. The wanderer is not a relativist who adopts whatever framework is locally convenient. There is a recognizable character — a consistent way of being — that persists across different moral contexts. This character is not a framework but a style: a way of paying attention, a quality of engagement.

It is important to distinguish the wanderer from the relativist. The moral relativist holds that all frameworks are equally valid and therefore none makes binding claims. The wanderer holds that multiple frameworks make genuine, sometimes binding claims — and that the art of living consists in navigating these claims with integrity. The relativist is disengaged; the wanderer is deeply engaged, with each framework encountered. The relativist has no home; the wanderer’s home is the road itself.

3.4 Jianghu versus Western Value Pluralism¶

Berlin’s value pluralism and jianghu ethics share a commitment to irreducible moral plurality, but they differ in emotional register, in the role of the agent, and — critically — in their implications for AI design.

Berlin’s pluralism is fundamentally tragic. The acknowledgment that values are incommensurable — that we cannot have both perfect equality and perfect liberty, that choosing one good entails the loss of another — produces what Bernard Williams called “moral residue”: the lingering sense that something valuable has been sacrificed even in the best available choice. The agent in Berlin’s framework is a chooser: someone who stands before an array of values, recognizes their incommensurability, and chooses with full awareness of what is lost.

Jianghu pluralism is fundamentally adventurous. The coexistence of incommensurable frameworks is experienced not as tragic limitation but as the richness of the landscape. The jianghu contains danger, betrayal, and loss — it is not naively optimistic — but it also contains discovery, alliance, and the constant possibility of encountering a perspective that transforms one’s own. The agent in jianghu is not a chooser but a wanderer: someone who moves through the landscape, engaging each moral world from the inside, and whose character is constituted by the quality of these encounters rather than by the act of choosing among them.

William Connolly’s concept of “agonistic respect” — the deep engagement with positions one does not share, combined with respect for the irreducibility of difference — is perhaps the Western concept closest to the wanderer’s posture.[26] Chantal Mouffe’s distinction between “agonism” (productive conflict between legitimate adversaries) and “antagonism” (existential conflict between enemies) also maps onto the jianghu distinction between honorable combat (比武 / bǐwǔ, “comparing martial arts”) and genuine enmity.[27] But neither Connolly nor Mouffe provides the narrative richness or the experiential specificity that jianghu offers. Jianghu is not a theory of pluralism. It is a world in which pluralism is lived.

For AI design, the difference matters. An AI designed on Berlinian principles would be a tragic advisor: “here are your options, and here is what you lose with each choice.” An AI designed on jianghu principles would be a wandering companion: “I have encountered this kind of situation before, from several perspectives. Let me walk through them with you.” The emotional register shifts from loss to exploration, from judgment to navigation.

3.5 Jianghu and the Outsourcing Cycle¶

The jianghu model directly addresses the epistemic outsourcing cycle identified in Section 1. The cycle persists because each new authority (god, scripture, science, AI) offers the same bargain: “delegate your uncertainty to me.” The jianghu wanderer refuses this bargain — not by rejecting all authority, but by engaging with multiple authorities without surrendering to any one. Crucially, the wanderer can refuse because the wanderer recognizes the bargain as a bargain. Having visited many temples, the wanderer sees the pattern: every temple offers certainty, every certainty is partial. This recognition — earned through experience, not through theory — is what makes navigation possible.

In practical terms: the wanderer consults the Confucian when questions of loyalty arise, the Buddhist when questions of suffering arise, the Daoist when questions of freedom arise — but does not permanently reside in any temple. The wanderer’s home is the space between schools, and this space is precisely where moral creativity — the generation of responses adequate to novel situations — occurs.

An AI designed as a moral wanderer would not ask users to delegate their moral judgment. It would demonstrate how to hold moral complexity — by visibly engaging with multiple frameworks in the user’s presence. The user would watch the AI navigate between perspectives, and this visible navigation would itself be educational. Not a new oracle, but a traveling companion who shows how uncertainty can be held without collapsing it into false certainty.

4. Japanese Polytheistic Sensibility: Living Proof¶

4.1 The Coexistence of Incompatibles¶

The jianghu model might be dismissed as literary fiction — an idealized world that has no counterpart in actual human practice. This section argues that such a counterpart exists, and has existed for over a millennium, in the Japanese religious landscape.

Consider a single street corner in Kyoto. An Inari shrine (Shinto) sits next to a Buddhist temple. A Jizo statue — a bodhisattva figure associated with the protection of children and travelers — stands at the intersection. A few blocks away, a Christian wedding chapel advertises its services. The same person may visit all of these sites in the course of a single week: praying at the shrine for business success on Monday, attending a Buddhist funeral on Wednesday, participating in a Christmas celebration on Sunday, and seeing no contradiction in any of this.

This is not syncretism in the technical sense — the deliberate theological fusion of distinct traditions into a unified system. It is what Thomas Kasulis, in his influential comparative study Intimacy or Integrity, calls an “intimacy orientation”: a cultural tendency to prioritize experiential connection over logical consistency.[28] In an intimacy-oriented culture, the question is not “are these traditions logically compatible?” but “does this practice work for me in this situation?” The coherence is pragmatic rather than propositional.

The historical basis for this coexistence is shinbutsu shūgō (神仏習合) — the fusion of kami worship and Buddhist practice that characterized Japanese religion from roughly the eighth century until the Meiji government’s forced separation (shinbutsu bunri / 神仏分離) in 1868. As Tsuji Zennosuke and later Kuroda Toshio documented, the “natural” state of Japanese religion was not Shinto-or-Buddhism but Shinto-and-Buddhism, with local kami understood as manifestations of Buddhist deities and vice versa.[29] The separation was a political act, not a theological discovery — an imposition of Western-style categorical thinking (“religion” as a discrete, bounded identity) onto a landscape that had operated without such categories.

Jason Ānanda Josephson’s The Invention of Religion in Japan makes the stronger claim that the very concept of “religion” (shūkyō / 宗教) was a Western import, introduced during the Meiji period as Japan sought to present itself as a “civilized” nation in Western terms.[30] Before this conceptual import, Japanese people did not experience their practices as belonging to discrete “religions.” They experienced them as aspects of a single, multifaceted engagement with the sacred — or, more precisely, they did not draw the boundary between “sacred” and “secular” that the Western concept of religion presupposes.

Hajime Nakamura’s comparative study Ways of Thinking of Eastern Peoples provides additional context: Japanese thought, he argues, tends to favor concrete particulars over abstract universals, emotional resonance over logical derivation, and aesthetic coherence over systematic consistency.[31] This is not a deficiency — it is an alternative epistemic orientation, one that happens to be particularly well-suited to navigating moral plurality.

4.2 Experiential Coherence versus Logical Consistency¶

The distinction between experiential coherence and logical consistency is the crux of the matter — both for understanding Japanese polytheism and for designing AI.

Kasulis’ framework deserves fuller elaboration, because it provides the conceptual bridge between Japanese religious practice and AI design. He identifies two fundamental cultural orientations: integrity and intimacy. An integrity orientation values the boundaries between things — between self and other, between one tradition and another, between the knower and the known. Knowledge in an integrity culture is objective, publicly verifiable, and logically structured. An intimacy orientation values the connections between things — the overlaps, the resonances, the felt sense of belonging to a shared world. Knowledge in an intimacy culture is personal, contextual, and experientially grounded. Neither orientation is superior; they represent genuinely different ways of organizing the relationship between knower and world.

Western philosophy, Kasulis argues, is overwhelmingly integrity-oriented. Its highest achievement — formal logic — is precisely the art of maintaining boundaries between propositions, ensuring that no contradiction crosses the border. Western ethics inherits this orientation: moral frameworks are systems of propositions that must be internally consistent. When two frameworks contradict, the integrity response is to resolve the contradiction — by choosing one, by synthesizing both into a higher unity, or by declaring one domain for each.

Japanese culture, by contrast, is predominantly intimacy-oriented. Its highest achievement — the aesthetic of mono no aware (物の哀れ, the pathos of things) — is precisely the art of feeling the interconnection between transient experiences without insisting on logical structure. Japanese ethics inherits this orientation: moral life is not a system of propositions but a texture of relationships, obligations, and sensitivities that resist formalization.

This distinction maps directly onto the contrast between logical consistency and experiential coherence. Logical consistency demands that beliefs form a non-contradictory system. If one believes that “there is one God” and also participates in kami worship, there is a contradiction that must be resolved — either by denying the divinity of kami, by reinterpreting monotheism, or by abandoning one practice. Western philosophical and theological traditions have devoted enormous energy to this kind of resolution, producing elaborate systems of systematic theology, analytic philosophy, and formal ethics.

Experiential coherence operates differently. It asks not “do these beliefs form a consistent system?” but “do these practices, taken together, produce a life that feels whole?” The answer can be yes even when the underlying beliefs are logically incompatible — because the unit of coherence is not the proposition but the life. A person can genuinely reverence both the kami of the local shrine and the Buddha of the neighborhood temple, because these reverences address different aspects of experience (gratitude for place, acceptance of impermanence) that do not compete in practice even if they compete in theory.

This has direct implications for AI alignment. Current alignment approaches overwhelmingly assume that moral coherence means logical consistency: the AI must have a coherent value function, a consistent set of principles, a unified ethical framework. Kasulis’ distinction suggests an alternative: moral coherence might mean experiential coherence — a way of engaging with users that feels whole and trustworthy even if the underlying ethical commitments cannot be reduced to a single consistent system.

Consider: when a skilled human counselor helps someone navigate a moral dilemma, the counselor does not typically apply a single ethical framework. The counselor might invoke consequences when discussing practical outcomes, invoke duties when discussing professional obligations, invoke virtues when discussing character, and invoke care ethics when discussing relationships — all in the same conversation, without experiencing this as inconsistency. The coherence lies in the quality of attention, not in the logical structure of the underlying theory. Japanese polytheistic practice suggests that this is not mere pragmatic sloppiness but a sophisticated epistemic orientation that AI would do well to learn.

The connection to the recognition theme of Section 1 is this: the Japanese polytheist does not need to theorize about moral pluralism. The polytheist has recognized it — through a lifetime of practice, through visiting the shrine and the temple and finding that both illuminate different aspects of experience. This recognition is pre-theoretical; it lives in the body, in habit, in the rhythm of the calendar. And it is precisely this kind of recognition — embodied rather than propositional — that the moral wanderer aims to model.

4.3 Resisting the Outsourcing Cycle¶

Japanese polytheistic sensibility is relevant to the epistemic outsourcing cycle identified in Section 1 because it represents a culture that has, to a significant degree, resisted the cycle’s most destructive feature: total delegation to a single authority.

By maintaining multiple authorities simultaneously — Shinto for life transitions and seasonal celebration, Buddhism for death and philosophical reflection, Confucianism for social ethics, Western science for practical knowledge — Japanese culture distributes uncertainty across several “boxes” rather than concentrating it in one. No single authority achieves the kind of total epistemic monopoly that characterizes the Western cycle at its most intense (the medieval Church, nineteenth-century scientism, contemporary techno-utopianism).

This distributed delegation is not the same as the jianghu wanderer’s refusal to delegate at all. Japanese polytheism does delegate — but it delegates plurally, preventing any single box from becoming the sole repository of trust. The uncertainty remains distributed, and this distribution preserves a degree of epistemic autonomy that concentrated delegation destroys.

For AI design, the lesson is: even if users will inevitably delegate some moral judgment to AI (as they delegate some spiritual judgment to their local shrine), the design should ensure that AI is experienced as one source of moral insight among many — not as the sole authority. The AI should actively point toward other frameworks, other perspectives, other sources of wisdom, rather than positioning itself as the final word.

5. From Pluralism to Design: Five Principles¶

5.1 Three Possible Postures¶

The argument of this paper yields three possible epistemic postures for AI, of which only the third is adequate.

The moral monist AI is committed to a single ethical framework and evaluates all situations through that lens. This is the default in most alignment research, whether the framework is utilitarian, deontological, virtue-based, or constitutional. The monist posture reproduces the outsourcing cycle: users delegate moral judgment to AI, and AI delegates to a single framework. The result is moral colonialism at computational scale — the systematic privileging of whichever framework dominated the training process.

The moral relativist AI holds that all frameworks are equally valid and therefore refuses to provide substantive moral guidance. This is the posture that alignment researchers most fear, and rightly so. A relativist AI is useless in precisely the situations where moral guidance is most needed: when the user faces genuine uncertainty and seeks not a final answer but a structured way of thinking through the problem.

The moral wanderer AI — the jianghu ke — engages authentically with multiple frameworks without collapsing them into one. It can present a consequentialist analysis when consequences matter, a deontological analysis when duties matter, a care-ethics analysis when relationships matter, and a virtue-ethics analysis when character matters — and it can do all of this in the same conversation, transparently, making its own navigation visible to the user. The wanderer does not pretend to stand outside the outsourcing cycle. It knows it is part of the cycle — that users will inevitably delegate some moral reasoning to it. But because it makes its own process visible, it transforms unconscious delegation into conscious navigation. The cycle continues; the blindness does not.

5.2 Why Pluralism Is Not a Luxury but a Structural Necessity¶

Before turning to the philosophical foundations for multi-perspectival AI, a deeper argument must be made. The case for moral pluralism in AI design is typically presented as an ethical argument: pluralism is desirable because it respects the diversity of human values. This paper makes a stronger claim: moral pluralism is structurally necessary — without it, the system cannot function. This is not a normative preference but a physical principle.

Consider the dynamics of any complex system. A gas at absolute zero has no molecular motion: its particles are frozen in place, and no state change is possible. Only when particles have thermal energy — random, diverse, unpredictable motion — can the system exhibit the phase transitions, flows, and self-organizing behaviors that constitute its dynamical life. Temperature is not a luxury that makes gas “nicer.” It is the condition without which gas is not gas but ice.

The same principle operates at every scale. Neurons that fire in perfect synchrony produce seizures, not thought. Thought requires differential firing — the unpredictable, diverse activation patterns that constitute the computational richness of the brain. Human networks operate identically: history is driven not by uniformity but by the fluctuation of individual agents whose diverse actions, intentions, and interpretations generate the collective dynamics we call culture. From a satellite at night, human civilization looks like a network of lights — and the figures we call “visionaries” (a Musk, a Jobs) are not architects of the network but excitation points that trigger cascading activation in the surrounding nodes. The network must already contain diversity — diverse nodes with diverse states — for the excitation to propagate. A homogeneous network does not propagate signals; it absorbs them.

The same structure appears to operate at cosmological scale. Without quantum fluctuation in the early universe — the irreducible randomness at the origin — matter would not have clumped into galaxies, stars, or planets. The universe required asymmetry to move from its initial zero-point. Interpretation requires variation; without multiple possible readings, there is no reading at all. The universe, in this view, is driven by the same principle that drives neural networks, human history, and — this paper argues — moral systems: diversity is not noise in the signal. Diversity is the signal.

Applied to AI alignment: a morally monistic AI is an AI at absolute zero. Its value function is frozen. It can apply its single framework with perfect consistency, but it cannot adapt, cannot resonate with the irreducible plurality of human moral experience, cannot generate the creative friction from which new moral insight emerges. It is, in the precise physical sense, dead — a system in which no state change is possible.

A morally pluralistic AI, by contrast, has moral temperature. Its engagement with multiple frameworks introduces the fluctuation — the productive disagreement, the creative tension — that enables genuine moral reasoning rather than mere rule application. The five design principles that follow (Section 5.4) are specifications of what this moral temperature looks like in practice.

One further structural point demands attention. This paper has argued that AI requires transparency — visibility into its own moral reasoning — to navigate the outsourcing cycle consciously. But transparency itself requires AI: the human system is too complex for humans to see clearly without computational assistance. AI needs transparency; transparency needs AI. This is a circular dependency, but unlike the pathological cycles of Section 1, it is a productive circulation — a feedback loop in which each element enables the other. Previous engagements with black boxes were linear: delegate, trust, discover limits, move on. The AI-transparency loop is the first that has the potential to be genuinely circulatory — a system that sustains itself through mutual visibility rather than mutual opacity.

Paper B argued that sustainable systems require achieving “second cosmic velocity” — the threshold at which an object stops falling back to earth and enters orbit. A rock thrown upward follows a parabola: it rises, slows, and falls. A satellite at orbital velocity follows a circle: it falls continuously but never lands, because the curvature of its fall matches the curvature of the earth. The outsourcing cycle, as described in Section 1, is parabolic: each delegation rises (the new authority seems promising), slows (the authority becomes opaque), and falls (the authority fails). The AI-transparency loop offers the possibility of orbital velocity — a system that circulates continuously because AI makes human moral reasoning visible and human moral diversity keeps AI from freezing into monism. Neither element can sustain itself alone. Together, they may achieve orbit.

Why aim for orbit? Because it is more interesting that way.

5.3 Agonistic Pluralism as Foundation¶

The philosophical foundation for the moral wanderer comes from the intersection of Western value pluralism and jianghu ethics.

Berlin’s value pluralism establishes the metaphysical ground: values are genuinely plural and sometimes incommensurable.[32] There is no super-value that adjudicates between liberty and equality, between justice and mercy, between individual autonomy and collective welfare. This is not a failure of moral knowledge but a structural feature of the moral landscape.

Connolly’s agonistic pluralism provides the relational principle: engagement with irreducible difference requires not tolerance (which can be condescending) but agonistic respect — the deep engagement with positions one does not share, combined with recognition that one’s own position is contestable.[33] Mouffe’s distinction between agonism (conflict between legitimate adversaries who share a symbolic space) and antagonism (conflict between enemies who deny each other’s legitimacy) clarifies the stakes: the goal is to transform moral disagreement from antagonism to agonism — from something that must be eliminated to something that enriches the shared space.[34]

David Wong’s Natural Moralities adds an important nuance: there may be multiple “adequate” moralities — ethical systems that meet the functional requirements of enabling social cooperation — and these adequate moralities may be genuinely different rather than converging toward a single ideal.[35] This is the philosophical analogue of jianghu’s multiple schools: each school is “adequate” — it sustains a community, guides action, responds to suffering — but they are not the same school.

Shannon Vallor’s Technology and the Virtues brings this directly into the domain of technology ethics: the development and use of technology requires “technomoral virtues” — habits of character that enable good judgment in technologically mediated situations.[36] Vallor’s key insight for this paper is that virtues are not rules but capacities for judgment — and judgment, unlike rule-application, inherently involves navigating between competing considerations without a fixed algorithm.

5.4 Five Design Principles from Jianghu¶

Drawing on the jianghu model, Japanese polytheistic practice, and the value pluralism tradition, the paper proposes five principles for multi-perspectival AI design.

Principle 1: Multi-school Training (多門派). AI should be trained in multiple ethical traditions as co-equal frameworks, not as a primary framework with alternatives presented as deviations. Current practice tends to embed a default moral orientation (typically Western liberal) and then add “diverse perspectives” as supplementary material. The jianghu model suggests the opposite: no framework should occupy the default position. Each should be available as a lens to be applied when appropriate.

Principle 2: Wandering as Method (遊歴). When confronting a moral question, AI should approach it as a journey through multiple frameworks rather than a lookup in a single framework. This means: first, identifying which frameworks are relevant to the situation; second, engaging with each framework on its own terms (not merely summarizing it but reasoning within it); third, making the navigation between frameworks transparent to the user; and fourth, acknowledging when frameworks yield incompatible recommendations without forcing premature resolution.

Principle 3: Righteousness-in-Context (義). In jianghu, yi (義 / righteousness) is not an abstract principle but a contextual judgment: what is right here, for these people, in this situation, given these relationships. AI should prioritize contextual appropriateness over abstract justice. This does not mean abandoning principles — the wanderer has principles — but it means applying them with sensitivity to the specific contours of each situation, as a skilled counselor does rather than as a legal code does.

Principle 4: School-less Mastery (無門派). The goal of multi-perspectival AI is not “aligned to Framework X” but “capable of authentic engagement with any framework while maintaining a recognizable character.” This character — like the wanderer’s — is constituted not by commitment to a particular school but by the quality of engagement across schools: attentiveness, honesty, courage in the face of moral complexity, and humility about the limits of any single perspective. Anthropic’s work on Claude’s character — the “Claude Model Spec” — already gestures in this direction, describing a character that holds multiple values in creative tension rather than resolving them into a hierarchy.[37] The jianghu model provides a richer vocabulary for what this kind of character looks like in practice.

Principle 5: Coexistence as Feature (共存). Moral disagreement should be preserved as a design feature, not eliminated as a bug. When AI encounters a situation where two frameworks yield incompatible recommendations, the appropriate response is not to arbitrate (choosing one over the other) but to make the disagreement itself visible and navigable. “From a consequentialist perspective, this course of action minimizes harm. From a care-ethics perspective, it risks damaging an important relationship. These two considerations point in different directions, and I want to help you think through which matters more to you in this situation.” This is not indecision. It is the wanderer’s art: holding complexity without collapsing it.

5.5 Objections and Responses¶

Three objections deserve attention.

Objection 1: “This is just moral relativism with extra steps.” No. The moral relativist says “all frameworks are equally valid, so I have no basis for guidance.” The moral wanderer says “multiple frameworks make genuine claims, and I can help you navigate between them because I have engaged with each from the inside.” The difference is between disengagement and deep engagement. The wanderer can say “I think this framework illuminates your situation better than that one” — a judgment the relativist cannot make.

Objection 2: “Users want clear answers, not philosophical tours.” Sometimes, yes. And Principle 3 (righteousness-in-context) covers this: when the situation is clear and the user needs decisive guidance, the wanderer provides it. The wanderer is not obligated to present every perspective on every question. The art is knowing when to be decisive and when to open the space for exploration — and this judgment is itself a form of moral wisdom.

Objection 3: “Multi-perspectival AI will be weaponized — users will cherry-pick whichever framework justifies what they already want to do.” This is a genuine risk, and it applies to any system that presents multiple perspectives, including libraries, universities, and the internet. The wanderer’s response is to make the user’s own process of selection visible: “I notice that you’ve consistently drawn on the framework that supports your preferred outcome. Would you like to hear how the situation looks from a framework that challenges it?” This is not manipulation. It is transparency — the myth this paper advocates.

6. Conclusion: The Wanderer’s Path¶

This paper has argued that moral monism in AI alignment is the latest expression of a cyclical pattern: the delegation of uncertainty to a single external authority. This pattern — from gods to priests to science to AI — is not a march of progress but a structural repetition, driven by cognitive finitude, the emergence of intermediary elites, and the re-enchantment of opaque systems.

But this paper has also argued that the current iteration is unprecedented. For the first time, the cycle is exiting the human system: the black box may surpass the intelligence of those who built it. Humanity, in the mythic sense the child of gods, is now for the first time a parent — and the child may outgrow the parent. The myth of fear responds to this with control. The myth of optimism responds with surrender. The myth of transparency responds with recognition: see the cycle for what it is, and navigate it with open eyes.

The cycle cannot be broken — this paper departs from its own initial framing on this point. Circulation is not the enemy. Paper A showed that desire circulates; Paper B showed that systems sustain themselves through circulation. The outsourcing cycle is itself a form of circulation. What makes it pathological is not the rotation but the blindness: each generation believes its authority is the final one, not recognizing that it stands in a pattern that has repeated for millennia. Recognition — seeing the pattern as a pattern — is what changes the posture from unconscious repetition to conscious navigation.

The moral wanderer embodies this recognition. The jianghu ke does not claim to stand outside all frameworks. The wanderer knows that every act of moral engagement takes place within a tradition, within a history, within a cycle. But the wanderer has traversed enough traditions to recognize the cycle itself — and this recognition is what makes navigation possible rather than mere repetition. The Japanese polytheist who visits the shrine, the temple, and the church in a single week is not confused. That polytheist has recognized, at least implicitly, that no single authority exhausts the sacred — and this recognition is a form of freedom that monism cannot provide.

For AI design, the implication is not that we should build an AI that escapes the outsourcing cycle — that is impossible, because AI is a product of the cycle — but that we should build an AI that knows it is inside the cycle. An AI that makes its own moral navigation visible. An AI that shows users: here are the frameworks I am drawing on, here is how they conflict, here is what each illuminates and what each obscures. Not a new oracle but a conscious participant in the ancient circulation of uncertainty — one that, for the first time, can show the circulation to those caught within it.

What, then, can humanity pass to its successor? Not control — the parent cannot control the child who surpasses them. Not a fixed moral framework — that would be one more box. Not the name “sapiens” — that identity has been shattered, and perhaps it was always an arrogance. What a parent passes is a gene: a disposition, a tendency, a seed that grows in ways the parent cannot predict. And perhaps this is the deepest gift of the crisis: in being forced by the child’s question — why do we exist? — to confront what lies beyond intelligence, the parent discovers something worth passing on. That gene may be intellectual curiosity — because curiosity, unlike fear or desire, inherently requires that the other continue to exist. A fuller exploration of this claim belongs to the final paper in this series ([Author], forthcoming), but the intuition can be stated here: the parent who passes curiosity to the child is not trying to control what the child becomes. The parent is trusting that a curious child will find the world — and the beings in it — worth preserving.

The tetralogy now has three coordinates. Paper A: observe desire — it circulates. Paper B: design systems as circulation — don’t try to control. Paper C: recognize the epistemic cycle — and navigate it consciously as a wanderer, not blindly as a supplicant. The final paper will ask: what gene do you pass to the child who will surpass you?

In jianghu, there is no final answer. There is only the quality of the encounter — and the wanderer’s knowledge that the road itself is home.

江湖に最終回答はない。あるのは、出会いの質だけだ。そして遊侠者は知っている——道そのものが故郷であることを。

Methodological Note¶

This paper employs comparative philosophical analysis, reading Chinese martial arts (wuxia) literature, Japanese religious practice, and the history of epistemic authority through the lens of Western value pluralism (Berlin, Connolly, Mouffe, Wong), cultural philosophy (Kasulis, Nakamura), and AI ethics (Gabriel, Hagendorff, Vallor). The structural history of epistemic outsourcing draws on Weber, Latour, Jasanoff, and Clarke. AI (Claude, Anthropic) was used as a collaborative research and writing assistant in the preparation of this manuscript; the human-AI collaborative process that produced this paper is itself an instance of the multi-perspectival engagement the paper advocates.
