The Gravity of Jizai: Cognitive Presence and the Dynamics of Existential Weight¶

Kenshiro Osada, M.Sc.
Independent Philosopher & Web Developer, Tokyo, Japan
ORCID: 0009-0004-9167-3186

Abstract¶

Why does the same event devastate us when it happens in our neighborhood but barely register when it occurs in a distant country? Why does a childhood memory retain its gravitational pull across decades while yesterday's news dissolves overnight? This paper introduces cognitive gravity: the force generated when a discrepancy arises between one's cognitive reality and the existential weight (presence) of a given phenomenon. Two companion papers have addressed what circulates in intellectual ecosystems (Osada, 2026e) and who participates in moral consideration (Osada, 2026f). This paper completes the trilogy by asking: with what weight, and why there?

The paper proposes a three-axis model of cognitive space—spatial-sensory proximity, temporal-experiential proximity, and possessive-intimate proximity—where the inverse of distance from the origin determines existential weight. At the origin (the here-now-mine singularity), cognitive density approaches infinity: the Emptyfall, a singularity structurally analogous to a black hole, where the center is empty (śūnyatā) yet surrounded by accumulated existential debris. This debris forms a torus—the Doughnut Personality Theory—in which personality is not the content of the self but the shape of what one wears around an absent center.

The dynamics of cognitive gravity follow a Lennard-Jones-type potential: cognitive density intensifies as distance decreases, but at extreme proximity, protective avoidance emerges (cognitive overload, overwhelm, dissociation), while at great distance, density decays into indifference. Cognitive gravity determines the amplitude of attention—how much one cares—but not its emotional direction; the same gravitational intensity can manifest as love or hatred, fascination or terror. Social media systematically distorts all three axes, compressing spatial distance, freezing temporal distance, and fabricating intimate proximity, thereby destabilizing the gravitational field that ordinarily structures human attention and care.

The paper derives a weight function for the stakeholder participation proposed in the companion papers, grounding the abstract concept of 'voting weight' in measurable cognitive proximity. Together, the three papers—The Genetics of Ideas (i), The Gravity of Jizai (j), and The Gradient of Kokoro (k)—form unit vectors of a three-dimensional coordinate system for understanding how ideas circulate, why they attract, and who may participate.

Keywords: cognitive gravity, existential weight, Emptyfall, Doughnut Personality Theory, Jizai, torus topology, Lennard-Jones potential, construal level theory, conceptual spaces, attention dynamics

1. Introduction: Why Are We Drawn to Certain Things?¶

In February 2026, when the U.S. Department of Defense issued its ultimatum to Anthropic—full unrestricted access to Claude or supply-chain risk designation—the most revealing reaction came not from policymakers or ethicists but from a Pentagon official. Emil Michael's reported response—"What if this software went down?"—was not a policy analysis. It was a visceral expression of cognitive gravity at its maximum: an entity whose existential weight was enormous (spatially embedded in classified systems, temporally urgent during active conflict, possessively claimed through a $200 million contract) was suddenly threatened with removal (Tiku & Nakashima, 2026). The discrepancy between cognitive reality (Claude is ours, here, now) and the threatened state (Claude is gone) generated a force that overwhelmed strategic calculation. The panic was proportional not to the objective risk but to the subjective weight.

This asymmetry is universal. The same drone strike that keeps a Pentagon analyst awake at night is forgotten by a farmer in Uruguay before breakfast. A child's scraped knee commands more parental attention than a famine affecting millions. The death of a pet devastates its owner while the neighbor feels only mild sympathy. These are not failures of moral reasoning; they are expressions of a gravitational field that structures all cognition. Proximity generates weight; weight generates force; force generates attention, care, fear, and desire.

Two companion papers have addressed adjacent questions. The Genetics of Ideas (Osada, 2026e) proposed a token-based attribution system for tracking what circulates in intellectual ecosystems—ideas, influences, creative contributions—and how float-valued tracking can replace Boolean attribution. The Gradient of Kokoro (Osada, 2026f) proposed a six-dimensional vector for assessing who participates in moral and political consideration, replacing Boolean personhood with continuous gradation. Both papers assumed a weight function that determines how much a given stakeholder's voice matters on a given issue (Osada, 2026f, Section 6.3), but neither defined it. This paper provides that definition.

The question is deceptively simple: what determines how much a given phenomenon matters to a given being? Not morally—that is the domain of ethics. Not informationally—that is the domain of epistemology. But existentially: how much of one's cognitive space does this phenomenon occupy? The answer, this paper argues, is a gravitational field defined by three axes of proximity, centered on a singularity that is structurally empty, and governed by dynamics that closely parallel molecular physics.

2. The Three-Axis Model of Cognitive Gravity¶

Cognitive gravity is the force exerted on a being's attention by the discrepancy between its current cognitive state and the existential weight of a phenomenon. Existential weight, in turn, is a function of proximity along three axes. The closer a phenomenon is to the origin—the here-now-mine point—the greater its weight, and the greater the force generated by any perturbation.

2.1 The Spatial-Sensory Axis (Distance)¶

The first axis measures spatial and sensory proximity. Objects and events that are physically near, visible, audible, or tangible occupy more cognitive space than those that are distant or imperceptible. A murder witnessed in person is qualitatively different from the same murder reported on a news ticker. The difference is not merely informational—both convey the same propositional content—but gravitational. The witnessed event is closer on the spatial-sensory axis, and therefore heavier.

This axis explains why humans have historically organized moral communities along geographic lines: the village, the city-state, the nation. Kahneman's (2011) work on availability heuristics and proximity bias provides empirical grounding: events that are sensorially vivid are judged as more frequent, more important, and more demanding of response. The gravitational interpretation adds a structural dimension: vividness is not a cognitive error but a signal of proximity, and proximity is a legitimate indicator of relevance. The error occurs only when the signal is artificially manipulated—as social media routinely does (Section 5.3).

2.2 The Temporal-Experiential Axis (Passage)¶

The second axis measures temporal and experiential proximity. Recent events weigh more than distant ones; lived experiences weigh more than learned facts. The Japanese proverb nodo moto sugireba atsusa wasureru ("once past the throat, one forgets the heat") captures the temporal decay of existential weight. Grief diminishes over years; urgency fades after deadlines pass; even transformative experiences gradually lose their gravitational pull as they recede along the temporal axis.

However, the axis is not uniformly scaled. Certain experiences—trauma, first love, near-death encounters—warp the temporal axis, maintaining their proximity long after chronological time has passed. A combat veteran may experience a wartime event as temporally closer than yesterday's breakfast. This warping is not pathological in itself; it reflects the fact that existential weight is not a simple function of clock time but of experiential density. Events that were processed at high cognitive density (the origin was near on all three axes) retain their gravitational signature.

2.3 The Possessive-Intimate Axis (Attachment)¶

The third axis measures the degree to which a phenomenon is experienced as mine. One's own mistake weighs more than a stranger's identical mistake. A parent's attention to their child's fever exceeds their concern for an unrelated child's identical fever by orders of magnitude. This axis captures ownership, kinship, identity, and attachment—the sense that a phenomenon belongs to one's existential domain.

Importantly, this axis is not reducible to social distance as typically defined in social psychology. A celebrity may be socially distant (never met in person) yet possessively close (perceived as part of one's identity, as with parasocial relationships). Conversely, a next-door neighbor may be spatially proximate but possessively distant (no attachment, no sense of shared fate). The possessive-intimate axis captures the specifically existential dimension of "this is part of my world" that neither spatial nor temporal proximity fully accounts for.

A potential objection arises here: if cognitive gravity is determined by proximity along three axes, and the possessive-intimate axis is itself shaped by past cognitive gravity (one comes to feel something as "mine" because it has previously occupied high existential weight), then the model appears circular—the output feeds back into the input. This is not a defect but a structural feature. The same circularity characterizes gravitational physics: the gravitational field is determined by mass distribution, but mass distribution shifts under the influence of the gravitational field. This is not a paradox but a dynamical system described by differential equations, where the state at time t determines the force, the force alters the state, and the new state determines the new force. The possessive-intimate axis is a state variable that evolves under the influence of the very field it helps to define. Note that the three axes are not equivalent in this respect: the spatial-sensory and temporal-experiential axes are largely determined by external conditions (physical distance, elapsed time), while the possessive-intimate axis alone depends on internal state—the accumulated history of gravitational capture. This asymmetry is precisely what gives the Doughnut Personality Theory (Section 4) its explanatory power: the torus is built from the feedback loop between attachment and gravity, each reinforcing the other. The "gap that draws" (Section 4) is this loop experienced from the inside.

2.4 Why Three Axes—A Hypothesis, Not a Theorem¶

The three-axis model is a hypothesis. The author considers three dimensions sufficient to account for the major variation in existential weight, for reasons that are suggestive rather than conclusive. The strongest reason is empirical: the three axes appear to modulate existential weight independently, and their combinations generate predictions that match everyday experience.

The independence claim can be tested by holding two axes constant and varying only the third. (1) Vary only the spatial-sensory axis: your child is sitting beside you at the dinner table versus your child is currently living abroad. The temporal axis is identical (both are happening now); the possessive-intimate axis is identical (both concern your child). Only physical distance changed. Existential weight drops—one worries, but the visceral immediacy of presence is absent. (2) Vary only the temporal-experiential axis: a mistake you made at work today versus an equivalent mistake you made three years ago. The spatial axis is equivalent (both involve your own professional domain); the possessive-intimate axis is identical (both are yours). Only the temporal axis shifted. Weight drops—today's error gnaws; the old one is barely a footnote. (3) Vary only the possessive-intimate axis: a cut on your hand versus an identical cut on a stranger's hand, both happening right now, right in front of you. Spatial and temporal axes are identical. Only ownership changed. Weight drops dramatically—from visceral pain to mild sympathy. Each axis contributes a separable attenuation of existential weight. This separability is what justifies treating the axes as at least approximately independent dimensions rather than a single undifferentiated notion of "closeness."

First, a methodological reason. Gärdenfors (2000) demonstrated that cognitive representations can be modeled as geometric spaces with domain-specific dimensionality. His framework does not constrain the number of dimensions, which varies by domain. The present proposal is more restrictive: it hypothesizes that existential weight as a specific cognitive quantity is adequately captured by three dimensions. This claim is subject to empirical revision.

Second, a structural reason. Any polygon can be decomposed into triangles; any higher-dimensional polytope can be decomposed into simplices (tetrahedra, etc.) whose faces are triangles. In algebraic topology, simplicial complexes built from triangles provide the fundamental building blocks for describing topological spaces (Giusti et al., 2016, applied this to neural data, finding that neuronal firing patterns organize into simplicial structures). Granovetter's (1973) triadic closure demonstrates that triangles are the minimal stable unit in social networks. Three is the minimal number that admits combinatorial richness: two points define a line (too simple); three define a plane (sufficient for structure).

A related observation: human beings inhabit three-dimensional physical space and appear to reach a cognitive ceiling at approximately three dimensions of simultaneous comprehension. Beyond three, we resort to compression strategies—typologies that serve as auxiliary construction lines, metaphors that fold higher-dimensional structures into graspable images, and increasingly, AI systems that process dimensions beyond our perceptual window and return the results in a form we can grasp. This is not a proof that cognitive space is three-dimensional; it is a suggestive convergence between the dimensionality of our physical habitat, the triangular decomposition of stable structures, and the apparent limit of unaided human spatial intuition. If this convergence is not coincidental, three axes may represent the natural resolution of the human window of understanding—the bandwidth through which we apprehend the world without prosthetic aid.

Third, a dialogue with existing theory. Trope and Liberman's (2010) Construal Level Theory (CLT) describes psychological distance along four dimensions: spatial, temporal, social, and hypothetical (certainty). The present model overlaps with CLT on the first two axes but diverges in two critical respects. (a) The third axis here is possessive-intimate rather than "social distance," because the relevant variable is not social closeness per se but existential ownership—the sense that something belongs to one's domain. (b) CLT describes how distance affects construal (abstract vs. concrete representation) but not force. The present model introduces dynamics: distance generates weight, and discrepancy generates gravitational force. CLT is a map; cognitive gravity is a mechanics.

The author does not claim that three is the final number. A fourth axis—perhaps certainty, cultural identification, or moral commitment—may prove necessary. The core claim is structural: existential weight is a continuous, multi-dimensional quantity, and the dynamics described in Sections 3–5 hold regardless of whether the correct dimensionality is three, four, or seven.

3. The Emptyfall: Singularity at the Origin¶

The origin of the three-axis space is the point where all distances are zero: here, now, mine. As a phenomenon approaches this point, its existential weight increases without bound. The self, insofar as it is always here-now-mine, occupies the origin. And yet the origin is not a place where one arrives; it is a singularity that one asymptotically approaches but never reaches. This is the Emptyfall.

The name combines "empty" (śūnyatā, the Buddhist concept of emptiness at the heart of all phenomena) and "fall." The term captures two structural properties that a single metaphor cannot. The black hole aspect: as phenomena approach the origin, cognitive gravity intensifies without limit, compressing experience under ever-greater density. Under this extreme gravity, material that enters the inner orbits is not merely stored but compressed—its specific gravity increases, its influence per unit of cognitive space grows, and it becomes disproportionately heavy relative to material in the outer orbits (this is the mechanism behind "three-year-old's soul lasts a hundred years" and PTSD, discussed in Section 4). The waterfall aspect: what falls in does not retain its original form. Like water plunging into a cascade pool, experience that enters the Emptyfall is pulverized, fragmented, and recombined with existing debris. A childhood event does not persist as an intact recording; it is broken apart into emotional valence, sensory fragments, relational templates, and narrative residue, then reassembled into something that serves the current configuration of the torus. The original is destroyed; what remains is processed material—existential debris that orbits the void in new combinations. The center is empty; the periphery is dense with this accumulated, reconstituted debris—memories, attachments, habits, fears, desires—none in their original form, all bearing the marks of gravitational processing.

A clarification of the Emptyfall's status within this paper is warranted. The insight that the center of experience is empty is ancient; Buddhist philosophy articulated it as śūnyatā over two millennia ago, and Nāgārjuna's Madhyamaka formalized it as the emptiness of inherent existence (Nāgārjuna, c. 150 CE/1995). In the companion tetralogy, The Morphology of Desire (Osada, 2026a) described the consequence of this emptiness: desire circulates rather than terminates, because its object can never be fully obtained. The present paper's contribution is neither to rediscover the emptiness nor to re-describe the circulation, but to provide the dynamics—the three-axis geometry that explains why the center is unreachable (the Achilles-and-tortoise structure of self-observation), how experience is processed as it approaches the center (compression and pulverization), and what shape the resulting debris field takes (the torus of Section 4). Buddhist philosophy supplied the intuition; the tetralogy supplied the phenomenological description; this paper supplies the mechanics.

The structure is that of Zeno's paradox of Achilles and the tortoise. To fully know oneself—to close the distance between the observing self and the observed self to zero—would require an infinite number of steps. Each act of introspection creates a new observer who is not yet observed. The meditator who tries to "be present" discovers that the attempt to be present introduces a gap between the trying self and the present moment. The gap is the Emptyfall. It cannot be closed, only orbited.

The Heart Sutra (Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya) opens with the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara—whose name means "the one who observes freely" (Jizai in Japanese: 観自在)—perceiving that all five aggregates are empty (śūnya). This is not a claim about nothingness but about the structural impossibility of locating a fixed essence at the center of experience. The Yogācāra tradition's vijñapti-mātra (consciousness-only) presses the point further: even one's own hand, one's own leg, one's own brain cannot be established as definitively "one's own." Pushed to its limit, the distinction between direct experience and a brain in a vat, between waking life and a virtual world, collapses. There is no core that one can point to and say this is me; there is only the outer shape—the configuration of what orbits the void. The Emptyfall formalizes this insight: the center of the cognitive gravitational field is structurally empty, and yet it is precisely this emptiness that generates the gravitational pull. If the center were full—if the self were a fixed, complete entity—there would be no gap, no discrepancy, no force. The self is gravitationally active because it is empty.

A moment of silence—mokutō (黙祷)—offers cross-cultural evidence for this structure. In mourning, in reverence, in collective grief, the most adequate response is not speech but silence. The act of saying nothing becomes the fullest possible act. Silence is the behavioral expression of the Emptyfall: an emptiness that is more present than any content could be. Every major cultural tradition has a version of this practice—from the Christian moment of prayer to the secular minute of silence—suggesting that the experience of the Emptyfall is not a philosophical abstraction but a lived, embodied datum.

The Emptyfall connects directly to the tetralogy. In The Morphology of Desire (Osada, 2026a), desire was defined not as a response to lack but as a circulatory pattern—a form that persists through continuous movement. The Emptyfall explains why desire circulates: the origin is unreachable. If the desired object could be fully obtained—if the distance on all three axes could be reduced to zero—desire would terminate. But the Emptyfall ensures that the final step is always deferred. Desire orbits the void, and in orbiting, it constitutes the self.

4. Doughnut Personality Theory: Personality as Torus¶

If the Emptyfall is a singularity surrounded by accumulated existential debris, then personality is the shape of that debris field. The topology is that of a torus—a doughnut—with the Emptyfall at its center. A doughnut is neither the hole alone nor the ring alone; it is their inseparable unity. Remove the hole and one has a sphere; remove the ring and one has nothing. The identity of the doughnut—and of the self—is constituted by the relation between the empty center and the surrounding material. Personality is not the content of the self (there is no content at the center) but the shape of what one wears around an absent center.

Consider Saturn's rings. The planet's gravitational field captures material—ice, rock, dust—and organizes it into stable orbits. The rings are not the planet; they are what the planet holds. Similarly, the Emptyfall captures experiential material—formative memories, relational patterns, aesthetic preferences, habitual fears—and organizes it into characteristic orbits. Some debris is tightly bound (near the inner edge of the torus: childhood conditioning, attachment styles, core beliefs); some is loosely bound (near the outer edge: recent opinions, transient interests, fashion preferences); some escapes entirely (forgotten experiences, abandoned commitments). The distribution of debris across orbital distances constitutes personality.

None of this material was originally one's own. Every memory was once an external event; every belief was once someone else's utterance; every attachment was once a stranger. The Emptyfall captured it, gravitationally digested it, and incorporated it into the ring. Yet the resulting pattern—the specific distribution of debris, the particular shape of the torus—is unique. No two beings orbit the same void with the same configuration. Personality is not what one has accumulated but how one has accumulated it: the drawing is unique even though every pigment was borrowed. Moreover, the drawing is never finished. The same debris is reinterpreted as the torus evolves: a childhood memory that once orbited as shame may, decades later, orbit as compassion. The material did not change; the gravitational field around it did, and with it, the meaning. Interpretation belongs to the torus, and the torus is not fixed.

This topology accounts for several psychological phenomena that resist explanation under substance-based theories of the self.

Three-year-old's soul lasts a hundred years (mitsugo no tamashii hyaku made, a Japanese proverb equivalent to "the child is father of the man"). Early experiences are captured at extreme cognitive density—the Emptyfall was still relatively unshielded, and incoming material was processed at high gravitational intensity. This material becomes the innermost ring of the torus: tightly bound, resistant to perturbation, foundational to the shape of all subsequent orbits.

Post-traumatic stress disorder. A traumatic event is processed under conditions of maximum cognitive gravity: spatially here, temporally now, possessively mine, and accompanied by the perception that the self is under existential threat. The resulting debris is compressed into an orbit so tight that it cannot be dislodged by ordinary experience. It does not recede along the temporal axis as normal memories do; it remains gravitationally locked, distorting the entire field around it. Therapeutic approaches—EMDR, cognitive processing, prolonged exposure—can be understood as attempts to redistribute the debris into wider orbits, reducing its local gravitational dominance.

The gap that draws. The torus has a hole. The hole is not a defect; it is constitutive. Because the center is empty, there is always a gap—a sense of incompleteness, of something missing, of "there must be more." This gap generates a persistent low-level gravitational pull: the drive to fill, to acquire, to understand, to connect. In the companion paper (Osada, 2026f), curiosity was defined as the white light of the mind—the undifferentiated source from which all emotions are refracted. Here, curiosity receives its mechanical explanation: it is the gravitational pull of the Emptyfall itself, experienced from the inner surface of the torus. The hole wants to be filled; but filling it would collapse the torus, and with it, the self.

The torus topology connects to the structures described in the companion papers. In The Genetics of Ideas, the Visionium model posits that intellectual artifacts are genealogical structures with no single originator—the center of the genealogy is empty, and the circulation of influences constitutes the artifact. In The Gradient of Kokoro, the kokoro vector describes mind as a dynamic process without a Boolean core—no inner homunculus, only the shape of ongoing activity. In each case, the same topology recurs: the center is empty; the periphery's circulation constitutes existence. The Doughnut Personality Theory makes this topology explicit at the level of the individual self.

5. The Dynamics of Cognitive Gravity¶

5.1 The Force¶

Cognitive gravity is defined as the force generated when a discrepancy arises between one's current cognitive state and the existential weight of a phenomenon. Formally:

F ∝ W × d

where W is the existential weight of the phenomenon (a function of inverse distance from the origin along the three axes) and d is the magnitude of the discrepancy between cognitive reality and the state to which the phenomenon draws attention. Crucially, cognitive gravity is a scalar—it determines the amplitude of attention, not its direction. The emotional valence of a response—whether one reacts with love or hatred, fascination or terror, tenderness or rage—is not determined by the gravitational field itself but by mechanisms outside the scope of this paper. Cognitive gravity determines how much one cares, not how one cares.

Three examples illustrate this distinction. A partner's infidelity is unforgivable; a celebrity's identical scandal is entertaining. The emotional direction is opposite, but the difference in amplitude is entirely explained by cognitive gravity: the partner occupies a position near the origin on all three axes; the celebrity is distant. Yesterday's mistake is agonizing; a forty-five-day-old rumor is forgettable. Same class of event; the temporal axis has done its work of attenuation. The blood on one's own hand is terrifying; the blood in a horror film is thrilling. The spatial-sensory axis distinguishes real from mediated proximity, converting the same stimulus from threat to entertainment. In each case, cognitive gravity determines the magnitude of the emotional swing—its amplitude—while the direction of the swing (toward attraction or aversion, grief or amusement) is determined elsewhere.

This formulation resembles Pascal's wager as an expectation calculation (see Osada, 2026f, Section 5.1): the "expected cognitive impact" of a phenomenon is the product of its weight (how much it matters) and its discrepancy (how far reality deviates from expectation). But unlike Pascal's wager, the variables here are continuous. There is no binary gamble; there is a field.

The force is not linear. A small discrepancy in a high-weight phenomenon (a partner's minor mood shift) can generate more force than a large discrepancy in a low-weight phenomenon (a distant country's regime change). This non-linearity is consistent with Kahneman and Tversky's (1979) prospect theory, in which the subjective impact of gains and losses is non-linear with respect to their objective magnitude—but the cognitive gravity framework provides a structural explanation for why the non-linearity occurs: it is a consequence of the gravitational field's curvature near the origin.

5.2 Lennard-Jones-Type Potential¶

The dynamics of cognitive gravity do not follow a simple inverse-square law. Instead, they exhibit a potential energy profile closely resembling the Lennard-Jones potential used to model intermolecular forces in physics. The Lennard-Jones potential has the form:

V(r) = 4ε[(σ/r)¹² – (σ/r)⁶]

where the first term (repulsive at short range, decaying as r⁻¹²) dominates at close distances and the second term (attractive, decaying as r⁻⁶) dominates at intermediate distances. At large distances, both terms approach zero: indifference.

The cognitive analogue is as follows:

At intermediate distance: maximum engagement. A phenomenon that is moderately close on all three axes—a friend's illness, one's own career trajectory, a political issue affecting one's community—generates strong, sustained cognitive density. This is the zone of care, productive concern, and focused attention.

At close distance: intensified density, approaching saturation. As the phenomenon draws closer to the origin—a child in danger, an imminent deadline, a betrayal by an intimate partner—cognitive density increases rapidly. Attention narrows. Other phenomena lose their weight. The cognitive field becomes dominated by a single attractor. The emotional amplitude is enormous: one may love, fear, or hate with equal intensity, but one cannot be indifferent.

At extreme proximity: cognitive overload and protective avoidance. When a phenomenon is too close to the Emptyfall—when it threatens the structure of the torus itself—the system generates protective avoidance. The witness to a murder averts their gaze. The bereaved parent cannot look at photographs. The overwhelmed worker dissociates. These are not failures of courage or emotional repulsion; they are structural responses to gravitational overload. The torus protects its own integrity by deflecting attention at the boundary of the Emptyfall, just as the electron cloud prevents atomic collapse.

At great distance: indifference. Beyond a certain threshold, cognitive density decays below the threshold of attention. The distant war, the abstract statistic, the stranger's misfortune—these exist in the cognitive field but generate insufficient density to mobilize attention or action. Stalin's reported aphorism ("one death is a tragedy; a million deaths is a statistic") describes not a moral failure but a gravitational one: a million deaths, distributed across distant points in the three-axis space, sum to less cognitive density than a single death at close proximity. One cannot care about what generates no gravitational field; and what one does not know cannot generate a field at all.

5.3 How Social Media Distorts the Gravitational Field¶

Social media platforms systematically distort all three axes of cognitive space, producing a pathological gravitational environment that differs from any that human cognition evolved to navigate.

Spatial compression. Events occurring thousands of kilometers away are presented with the same sensory immediacy as local events: high-resolution video, real-time updates, visceral imagery. The spatial-sensory axis is collapsed: a bombing in a distant city occupies the same position in the visual field as a cat on one's lap. The brain, which evolved to weight sensory proximity as a reliable indicator of relevance, cannot distinguish between genuine spatial proximity and mediated spatial proximity. The result is a gravitational field in which distant phenomena exert forces appropriate to local phenomena.

Temporal freezing. On social media, the past does not recede. A tweet from 2014 can resurface with the same gravitational weight as one from this morning. The temporal-experiential axis is distorted: events that would ordinarily decay along the temporal axis are preserved at artificially high proximity. This explains the phenomenon of "context collapse" (Marwick & Boyd, 2011)—not merely as a social phenomenon but as a gravitational one. When past statements retain the weight of present statements, the entire temporal structure of the cognitive field is destabilized.

Intimate fabrication. Parasocial relationships, follower counts, and engagement metrics create the illusion of possessive-intimate proximity where none exists. The brain registers 10,000 followers as 10,000 relationships, each contributing a quantum of possessive-intimate weight. The result is a cognitive field distorted by phantom mass: enormous existential weight attached to entities (followers, influencers, brands) that occupy no genuine position on the possessive-intimate axis. When this phantom mass is suddenly removed (unfollowing, cancellation, platform ban), the resulting gravitational collapse is experienced as a loss disproportionate to any real relational change.

The pathologies commonly attributed to social media—doomscrolling, outrage cycles, anxiety, polarization—can be understood as consequences of a distorted gravitational field. Doomscrolling is the cognitive equivalent of an object trapped in a decaying orbit: the spatial compression makes distant threats feel proximate, the temporal freezing prevents them from receding, and the intimate fabrication makes the viewer feel personally implicated. The force intensifies with each scroll, but no action can reduce the discrepancy because the phenomena are not genuinely proximate—they are gravitational mirages.

6. The Weight Function: Answering Papers 5 and 6¶

6.1 Stakeholder Voting Weight as Cognitive Gravity¶

In The Gradient of Kokoro (Osada, 2026f, Section 6.3), a framework for float-valued stakeholder participation was proposed, with the caveat that the weight function determining each participant's influence would be developed in the present paper. Here we deliver that promise.

The proposal is straightforward: a stakeholder's voting weight on a given issue is proportional to their cognitive gravity with respect to that issue—that is, their proximity along the three axes. An AI safety researcher deliberating on autonomous weapons policy is spatially close (the technology is within their domain of direct interaction), temporally close (they are currently working on the problem), and possessively close (the outcome directly affects their professional identity and social responsibility). Their cognitive gravity is high; their voting weight is correspondingly high. A resident of a remote community with no direct exposure to AI systems occupies a distant position on all three axes; their voting weight on AI weapons policy is correspondingly lower—though never zero, since indirect effects (security, economy, cultural change) provide nonzero proximity.

Critically, voting weight is issue-specific. The same individual who carries high weight on AI weapons policy may carry low weight on fisheries management if they have no proximity to that domain. This contrasts sharply with both Boolean democracy (one person, one vote regardless of proximity) and plutocratic models (weight proportional to wealth regardless of issue). Cognitive gravity provides a principled, non-arbitrary basis for differential weighting: not "how much money do you have?" but "how close is this issue to your existential center?"

The failure mode of social media—described in Section 5.3—is precisely the failure to respect cognitive gravity in participation. On social media, every user has equal weight on every issue regardless of proximity. The result is the noise amplification that The Genetics of Ideas described as hard-decision cascading (Osada, 2026e, Section 4): opinions from distant, low-gravity positions are aggregated with the same weight as opinions from proximate, high-gravity positions, destroying the signal. A converse problem deserves acknowledgment: as the Lennard-Jones-type potential (Section 5.2) implies, extreme proximity can produce cognitive saturation rather than clarity. The behavior of the weight function at very small distances—where a stakeholder's involvement is so total that it overwhelms judgment—connects to the institutional practice of recusal (the exclusion of interested parties from adjudication) and warrants further investigation.

6.2 Ideas Ocean Distance as Cognitive Gravity¶

In The Genetics of Ideas (Osada, 2026e, Section 5.3), the Ideas Ocean was proposed as a vector space in which intellectual artifacts are embedded using transformer-based models, enabling automated similarity analysis. The distance between two works in the Ideas Ocean measures their intellectual proximity—how much they share in terms of conceptual content, argumentative structure, and genealogical inheritance.

This distance is a form of cognitive gravity. A researcher whose work is embedded close to a disputed artifact in the Ideas Ocean has high cognitive gravity with respect to that artifact: they understand its intellectual genealogy, can assess its novelty, and can evaluate its contribution with precision. Their voice in an attribution dispute should carry more weight than that of a researcher in a distant region of the vector space. The weight function proposed here thus connects the governance layer (Paper 6) to the economic layer (Paper 5) through a unified gravitational framework: proximity in intellectual space determines both attribution credibility and participatory weight.

6.3 Toward Measurement: Imperfect Proximity Is Better Than None¶

The obvious objection is: who measures cognitive proximity, and how? The three axes involve subjective quantities—how close something feels—that resist direct external observation. This is a genuine difficulty, but it does not invalidate the framework. The same principle established in the companion paper (Osada, 2026e, Section 5.3) applies: a rough float estimate carries more information than a Boolean default. The current alternative—one person, one vote regardless of proximity; or one dollar, one vote regardless of relevance—is not "no measurement" but measurement fixed at a constant, which is the least informative possible measurement.

Initial implementations need not be precise. Several proxy measures are already available. For the spatial-sensory axis: geographic data, professional domain classification, and direct-interaction logs can approximate spatial proximity to a given issue. For the temporal-experiential axis: publication history, employment records, and engagement timelines provide rough temporal proximity. For the possessive-intimate axis—the most difficult—self-declaration combined with behavioral signals (time invested, resources committed, stated stakes) can serve as a first approximation. Each proxy is imperfect. But the aggregation of imperfect proxies across three axes produces a weight function that is structurally superior to uniform weighting, just as soft-decision decoding with noisy estimates outperforms hard-decision decoding with clean but information-destroying thresholds (Osada, 2026e, Section 4). The precise calibration of these proxies is a task for social experimentation, not philosophical prescription. What this paper provides is the structural argument for why proximity should determine weight, and the three-axis framework for along which dimensions proximity should be measured.

7. Connection to the Tetralogy and the 3G Papers¶

This paper completes two arcs: the 3G trilogy (three companion papers) and the broader connection to the author's four-paper tetralogy on desire, alignment, moral pluralism, and thought-capital.

The Morphology of Desire (Osada, 2026a): as discussed in Section 3, the Emptyfall provides the mechanical explanation for why desire circulates rather than terminates. The morphology of desire is the orbital mechanics of cognitive gravity.

The Circulation of Alignment (Osada, 2026b) proposed that AI alignment should be understood as circulation rather than control. Cognitive gravity provides the force: the phenomena that generate alignment pressure—human values, social norms, ethical principles—are themselves distributed across the gravitational field, each exerting a pull proportional to its existential weight. Alignment is not a destination but an orbit shaped by the field.

The Jianghu Principle (Osada, 2026c) argued that moral pluralism requires coexisting frameworks rather than a single universal principle. Cognitive gravity provides the topology for this coexistence. Each moral framework—utilitarian, deontological, virtue-based, care-based—functions as a gravitational attractor in cognitive space, with its own well of existential weight. An individual does not occupy one attractor exclusively; they orbit several at different distances, shifting between them as context changes. The Jianghu (rivers and lakes)—the chaotic, multi-centered social space where competing moral authorities coexist—is the cognitive gravitational field with multiple attractors and no single dominant center. This is not moral relativism (all attractors equally valid) but moral ecology: the relative pull of each attractor is determined by cognitive gravity, and the system's health depends on no single attractor collapsing the field. The Emptyfall guarantees this: because the origin is empty, no attractor can occupy it, and therefore no moral framework can achieve absolute dominance. Pluralism is not a compromise but a topological necessity.

Thought-Capital (Osada, 2026d) introduced thought-capital as a resource generated by curiosity and circulated through intellectual ecosystems. Cognitive gravity provides the economics: the "interest" that a piece of thought-capital generates is proportional to its existential weight—its capacity to attract cognitive attention. A fascinating idea is one with high cognitive gravity; a boring idea is one with low gravity. The market of ideas is a gravitational market.

The three companion papers form a coordinate system:

The Genetics of Ideas (i): what circulates.

The Gravity of Jizai (j): why it attracts, and with what weight.

The Gradient of Kokoro (k): who participates.

i, j, k are the standard unit vectors of a three-dimensional coordinate system. This is not a coincidence but a design: the three papers describe the three orthogonal dimensions required to specify any point in the space of intellectual circulation. Remove any one, and the system collapses to a plane—a flat projection that destroys the information contained in the missing dimension.

8. Limitations and Failure Modes¶

The framework presented here is a proposal, not a proof. Several significant limitations must be acknowledged.

The arbitrariness of three axes. The three-axis model is a hypothesis. As noted in Section 2.4, the correct dimensionality of cognitive space may be higher, and the specific axes proposed here may not be the optimal decomposition. Cross-cultural research may reveal that different populations weight the axes differently, or that additional axes (certainty, cultural affiliation, moral commitment) are needed. The structural claims—that existential weight is continuous and multi-dimensional, that the origin is singular, that the dynamics follow a non-monotonic potential—are independent of the specific number of axes, but the three-axis model as stated is empirically untested.

The measurement problem. Cognitive gravity is defined in terms of subjective proximity and existential weight—quantities that are not directly observable from outside. Self-report measures are subject to introspective error; behavioral measures (attention allocation, physiological arousal) are indirect and confounded by multiple factors. If cognitive gravity is adopted as a basis for stakeholder weighting (Section 6), the question of who measures proximity and how they do so becomes a question of power. The framework inherits the same measurement-power concern raised in The Genetics of Ideas (Osada, 2026e, Section 9): any system that quantifies subjective states risks creating a new guise of objectivity.

Cultural variation. The relative weight of the three axes may vary significantly across cultures. Collectivist cultures may amplify the possessive-intimate axis (extending "mine" to the entire community); individualist cultures may weight the spatial-sensory axis more heavily (prioritizing personal, immediate experience). The model does not currently account for this variation, and any application must be culturally calibrated.

Manipulation risk. If cognitive gravity determines attention, care, and participatory weight, then the ability to manipulate cognitive gravity becomes a source of power. Propaganda, marketing, and platform algorithms already operate in this space—Section 5.3's analysis of social media distortion is an instance. A framework that makes cognitive gravity explicit could be used not only to understand manipulation but to refine it. The defense against this risk is not to avoid the framework but to ensure that the gravitational field is transparent—that the axes, distances, and weights are visible to the beings whose attention is being directed, rather than hidden behind interfaces designed to maximize engagement.

The hard problem, again. Cognitive gravity is defined functionally—in terms of observable behavior and its structural explanation—rather than phenomenologically. It does not claim to explain what it is like to experience high existential weight. This is the same methodological choice made in the companion paper's kokoro gradient (Osada, 2026f, Section 4.1): the hard problem of consciousness is bypassed, not solved. Whether cognitive gravity is accompanied by subjective experience, and whether that experience is morally relevant in ways that the functional account does not capture, remains an open question.

9. Conclusion: The Center Is Empty¶

This paper has demonstrated three things. First, that existential weight—how much a given phenomenon matters to a given being—can be decomposed along three independently operating axes of proximity: spatial-sensory, temporal-experiential, and possessive-intimate, centered on a structurally empty origin (the Emptyfall) around which the self is configured as a torus (the Doughnut Personality). Second, that the dynamics of this weight follow a Lennard-Jones-type potential: engagement intensifies as proximity increases, but extreme proximity triggers protective avoidance, and great distance decays into indifference—with cognitive gravity determining the amplitude of attention (how much one cares) but not its emotional direction. Third, that this framework provides the weight function left undefined by the companion papers: a stakeholder's voice on a given issue should carry weight proportional to their cognitive gravity with respect to that issue—their proximity along the three axes—rather than their wealth, status, or volume.

The framework answers a question left open by both companion papers: how to determine the weight that a given stakeholder's voice should carry on a given issue. The answer is not wealth, not status, not loudness, but proximity—cognitive gravity. Those closest to the Emptyfall of a given issue—those for whom it is here, now, mine—speak with the weight of existential presence. Those at a distance speak with proportionally less weight but are not silenced; their distance is itself informative, providing a perspective that proximity cannot.

The 3G papers are now complete. The Genetics of Ideas (i) described what circulates. The Gradient of Kokoro (k) described who participates. The Gravity of Jizai (j) describes why we are drawn, and with what weight. Together, i, j, k are the unit vectors of a three-dimensional coordinate system—sufficient to locate any point in the space where ideas move, beings participate, and attention falls.

Across all seven papers—four in the tetralogy, three in the 3G series—one topology recurs. The Visionium genealogy has no single author at its center. The kokoro vector has no Boolean core. The Doughnut Personality has no substance at its origin. Desire circulates because the origin is unreachable. Alignment circulates because no fixed point can serve as universal anchor. Moral frameworks coexist because no single attractor can collapse the field. In every case: the center is empty; the circulation at the periphery constitutes existence.

The word Jizai (自在) means "to dwell freely in oneself." It is the second element in Kanjizai (観自在), the bodhisattva who opens the Heart Sutra by perceiving that all aggregates are empty. To dwell freely is not to escape the gravitational field but to see it—to recognize the three axes, the Emptyfall, the torus, and the Lennard-Jones dynamics for what they are: the structure of one's own attention, not an external compulsion. The gravitationally unconscious being is pulled without knowing why. The Jizai being is pulled and knows the shape of the pull.

This is not transcendence. The gravitational field does not disappear upon recognition; one does not cease to care more about one's child than about a stranger's. But one gains the capacity to observe the field, to notice when social media is distorting it, to recognize when a high-weight phenomenon is generating avoidance rather than engagement, and to choose—within the constraints of the field—how to orient. Jizai is not freedom from gravity. It is freedom within gravity. The torus still orbits the void. But now it orbits with awareness.
